DISEASE: Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD) / Acid Reflux
CATEGORY: Gastrointestinal
ICD-10: K21
SEVERITY: Chronic — manageable with lifestyle and medication, can be disabling

DESCRIPTION:
GERD occurs when stomach acid flows backward (reflux) into the esophagus regularly,
causing irritation and inflammation. Lower esophageal sphincter (LES) is a valve that
should prevent backflow but becomes weakened. Can range from occasional heartburn
(non-pathological) to severe with complications like Barrett's esophagus or strictures.

TRIGGERS OF ACID REFLUX:
- Certain foods: Spicy, fatty, citrus, tomato, chocolate, caffeine
- Alcohol and smoking
- Large meals (especially late at night)
- Lying down within 3 hours of eating
- Obesity and excess abdominal pressure
- Pregnancy (increased abdominal pressure, hormonal changes)
- Stress
- Medications: NSAIDs, aspirin, some antibiotics

SYMPTOMS:
COMMON:
- Heartburn: Burning sensation in chest, behind breastbone
- Acid regurgitation: Sour taste, bitter liquid in mouth
- Chest pain: Can mimic heart attack (distinguish by relation to food/position)
- Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia): Feels like food stuck in throat
- Belching and bloating

LESS COMMON (ATYPICAL):
- Chronic cough: Acid irritates airway
- Hoarseness: Voice changes, throat clearing
- Asthma-like symptoms: Wheezing, shortness of breath
- Sore throat: Recurrent pharyngitis
- Laryngitis: Voice box inflammation
- Sleep disturbance: Nighttime reflux worsens when lying down
- Tooth erosion: Stomach acid erodes tooth enamel

SEVERITY SCALE:
- Mild: Occasional heartburn (2-3 times/week), responds to antacids
- Moderate: Frequent symptoms (3-5 times/week), disrupts sleep, needs medication
- Severe: Daily symptoms, interferes with quality of life, complications developing
- Complicated: Barrett's esophagus, esophageal ulcers, strictures, bleeding

MEDICATIONS USED:

ANTACIDS (Fast relief, short duration):
1. CALCIUM CARBONATE (Antacid)
   - Neutralizes acid immediately
   - Dosage: 2-3 tablets every 2-4 hours as needed
   - Brand names: Tums, Campral
   - EFFECT: Relief in 5-10 minutes, lasts 1-3 hours
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Can cause constipation or rebound acid production

2. MAGNESIUM HYDROXIDE (Milk of Magnesia)
   - Faster onset than calcium carbonate
   - Dosage: 30 mL once/twice daily
   - Effects last longer than calcium carbonate

3. SODIUM BICARBONATE
   - Very fast relief but short-lived (10-15 minutes)
   - Dosage: 1/2 tsp in water
   - CAUTION: Can cause rebound hyperacidity

H2-RECEPTOR ANTAGONISTS (Slower onset, longer duration):
4. RANITIDINE / FAMOTIDINE
   - Reduces acid production by 70%
   - Famotidine: 20 mg twice daily or 40 mg at night
   - Brand names: Gastrine, Pepcid
   - Effect: 30-60 minutes to work, lasts 8-12 hours
   - PREFERRED over antacids for regular use

PROTON PUMP INHIBITORS (Most powerful, long-term control):
5. OMEPRAZOLE / LANSOPRAZOLE / PANTOPRAZOLE
   - Strongest acid-reducing medication
   - Omeprazole: 20 mg once daily, preferably morning
   - Lansoprazole: 15-30 mg once daily
   - Brand names: Prilosec, Esomeprazole, Pantoprazole
   - Effect: Takes 4-5 days of use to reach full effect
   - Once daily dose powerful enough for most patients
   - MUST take on empty stomach (30 minutes before food)

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID WITH GERD:
- NSAIDs: Ibuprofen, Diclofenac (increase acid, damage esophageal lining)
- Aspirin: Irritates esophagus
- Calcium channel blockers: Relax LES (have reflux as side effect)
- Anticholinergics: Reduce esophageal contractions
- AVOID for at least 4 hours with PPIs: Clopidogrel (Plavix) — reduced effectiveness

FOODS TO EAT:
- Non-acidic fruits: Banana, apple, pear, melon
- Leafy greens / Palak, methi: Alkaline, non-acidic
- Broccoli and green beans: Low-acid vegetables
- Asparagus: Prebiotic fibers, non-irritating
- Rice and whole grains: Oatmeal, brown rice (avoid white bread)
- Lean proteins: Chicken, fish, turkey, tofu
- Eggs: Mild protein source
- Ginger / Adrak: Anti-inflammatory, improves stomach function
- Oatmeal: Absorbs stomach acid
- Almonds: Alkaline, contain oils that coat stomach
- Honey / Shahad: Has antacid properties
- Legumes / Dal: High fiber helps digestion
- Herbal tea: Chamomile, licorice (non-caffeinated)

FOODS TO STRICTLY AVOID (Common triggers):
- Citrus fruits: Orange, lemon, lime, grapefruit, Mosambi
- Tomato and tomato products: Sauces, ketchup, chutney
- Spicy foods: Red chili, black pepper, excess spice mixes
- Fried and fatty foods: Chips, pakora, samosa, fatty meats
- Chocolate: Cocoa irritates esophagus, relaxes LES
- Caffeine: Coffee, strong black tea, cola
- Alcohol: Beer, wine, spirits (all trigger reflux)
- Mint (some people): Relaxes LES, though helps others
- Onion and garlic / Lahsun: Sulfur compounds increase acid
- Vinegar and acidic dressings: Salad dressings
- High-fat dairy: Full-fat yogurt, cream, butter (low-fat OK)
- Carbonated beverages: Soda, fizzy drinks

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS (Most important!):
- Elevate head of bed: 6-8 inches (use wedge pillow under mattress, not just pillows)
- Avoid lying down within 3 hours of eating
- Eat smaller, frequent meals: 5-6 small meals instead of 3 large
- Slow down eating: Chew thoroughly, eat mindfully
- No eating 2-3 hours before bedtime
- Lose weight: 5-10% weight loss can significantly improve symptoms
- Loosen tight clothing: Reduces abdominal pressure
- Manage stress: Stress increases acid production
- Quit smoking: Smoking weakens LES, increases acid
- Limit alcohol: Or eliminate completely
- Avoid bending over or lying flat after eating
- Stay hydrated: Sip water throughout day

SLEEP POSITION:
- Sleep on LEFT side (not right): Reduces reflux
- Avoid supine (flat on back) position at night
- Keep head elevated 30-45 degrees

MEDICATIONS THAT AGGRAVATE REFLUX:
- NSAIDs: Ibuprofen, naproxen, diclofenac
- Aspirin
- Antidepressants (TCA): Amitriptyline
- Calcium channel blockers: Diltiazem, verapamil
- Some antibiotics: Doxycycline, tetracycline
- Antihistamines: Diphenhydramine
- Consider alternatives with doctor

COMPLICATIONS IF UNTREATED:
- Barrett's esophagus: Precancerous lining change (1% undergo malignant transformation)
- Esophageal strictures: Narrowing from scarring (dysphagia develops)
- Esophageal ulcers: Can cause upper GI bleeding
- Aspiration pneumonia: Acid enters lungs
- Laryngeal cancer: Chronic acid exposure (rare)
- Dental erosion: Acid damages tooth enamel irreversibly

WHEN TO SEEK EMERGENCY CARE:
- Sudden severe chest pain (rule out heart attack)
- Vomiting blood or black material (melena)
- Severe difficulty swallowing
- Persistent vomiting

WHEN TO SEE DOCTOR:
- Symptoms > 2 weeks despite medication
- New symptoms of dysphagia (difficulty swallowing)
- Involuntary weight loss
- Not improving on treatment after 2-4 weeks
- Atypical symptoms: Persistent cough, voice changes, asthma-like symptoms

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Pregnancy: Hormonal and mechanical (growing uterus) factors increase reflux
  Most antacids and H2 blockers safe; avoid some PPIs or discuss with OB
- Elderly: May have different presentation (chest pain, aspiration) — needs evaluation
- PPI long-term use: Associated with B12 deficiency, calcium malabsorption, increased pneumonia risk
- Long-term management: Goal is symptom control, not eliminating all acid (acid is needed for digestion)
