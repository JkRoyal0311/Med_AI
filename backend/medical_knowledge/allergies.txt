DISEASE: Allergies (Allergic Rhinitis, Allergic Dermatitis, Hay Fever)
CATEGORY: Immunological
ICD-10: J30, L20-L25
SEVERITY: Mild-Moderate usually; can be severe in some patients

DESCRIPTION:
Allergies are immune system overreactions to harmless substances (allergens). The immune system
mistakenly identifies a substance as dangerous and produces antibodies (IgE), triggering release
of histamine and other chemicals. This causes inflammation and symptoms. Can be seasonal (hay fever)
or perennial (year-round). Severity ranges from mild annoyance to severe (anaphylaxis with certain allergens).

TYPES OF ALLERGIES:

1. ALLERGIC RHINITIS (Hay fever, nasal allergies)
   - Most common type
   - Seasonal: Tree pollen (spring), grass pollen (summer), ragweed (fall)
   - Perennial: Dust mites, pet dander, mold

2. ALLERGIC DERMATITIS / ECZEMA
   - Itchy, inflamed skin
   - Contact dermatitis: From direct contact with allergen
   - Atopic dermatitis: Genetic predisposition, environmental triggers

3. FOOD ALLERGIES
   - Immediate reaction (anaphylaxis in severe cases)
   - Common: Peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, eggs, dairy, sesame

4. DRUG ALLERGIES
   - Include penicillin allergy (most common)

5. INSECT ALLERGIES
   - Bee/wasp sting anaphylaxis
   - Dust mite allergy

SYMPTOMS (vary by type and severity):

ALLERGIC RHINITIS:
- Sneezing: Repeated, sudden
- Nasal congestion: Stuffy nose, difficulty breathing through nose
- Nasal discharge: Clear, watery (allergic rhinitis) vs. thick colored (bacterial infection)
- Itching: Nose, eyes, throat, ears
- Watery eyes: Tears without crying
- Darkening under eyes: "Allergic shiners" from nasal congestion and inflammation
- Postnasal drip: Drainage running down back of throat
- Cough: From postnasal drip
- Fatigue: From poor sleep due to congestion
- Headache: From sinus congestion
- Loss of taste/smell: From nasal congestion

ALLERGIC DERMATITIS:
- Itching: Intense, sometimes intolerable
- Rash: Red, raised, scaly
- Blisters: In acute cases, may weep fluid
- Oozing/crusting: Infected skin from scratching
- Dry, cracked skin: In chronic cases
- Location: Depends on allergen (linear rash from plant contact, areas of frequent touching)

FOOD ALLERGIES (Immediate type):
- Oral itching/tingling: Lips, mouth, throat
- Lip/tongue swelling: Angioedema
- Nausea and vomiting
- Abdominal cramping and diarrhea
- Skin hives/urticaria
- Anaphylaxis (severe): Wheezing, throat tightness, difficulty breathing, hypotension, shock
  [SEEK EMERGENCY CARE IMMEDIATELY for anaphylaxis]

URTICARIA (Hives):
- Itchy welts/wheals on skin
- May appear and disappear in hours
- Triggered by allergen exposure

ANGIOEDEMA:
- Deep tissue swelling, especially face, lips, tongue
- Serious if involves throat (airway compromise)

RISK FACTORS:
- Family history of allergies
- Early childhood infections reduce allergy risk (hygiene hypothesis)
- Eczema or asthma (increased allergy risk)
- Elevated IgE levels

MEDICATIONS USED:

ANTIHISTAMINES (Block histamine, relieve symptoms):

First-Generation (Older, cause drowsiness):
1. DIPHENHYDRAMINE (Benadryl)
   - Dosage: 25-50 mg every 4-6 hours (max 300 mg/day)
   - Onset: 15-30 minutes
   - Duration: 4-6 hours
   - SIDE EFFECT: Drowsiness (anticholinergic)
   - USES: Good for nighttime itch relief, sedation effect useful
   - AVOID: Before driving or machinery; in elderly (confusion risk)

Second-Generation (Newer, non-drowsy):
2. CETIRIZINE (Zyrtec)
   - Dosage: 10 mg once daily
   - Onset: 20-30 minutes
   - Duration: 24 hours
   - Minimal drowsiness
   - Brand names: Cetaphil, Allercet
   - Good first choice

3. LORATADINE (Claritin)
   - Dosage: 10 mg once daily
   - Onset: 1-3 hours
   - Duration: 24 hours
   - Non-drowsy
   - Brand names: Loratadine, Claratine

4. FEXOFENADINE (Allegra)
   - Dosage: 180 mg once daily
   - Non-drowsy
   - Takes slightly longer to work than others

DECONGESTANTS (Reduce nasal congestion):
5. PSEUDOEPHEDRINE
   - Dosage: 30-60 mg every 4-6 hours (max 120 mg/day)
   - Reduces blood vessel swelling in nasal passages
   - Onset: 30 minutes
   - Duration: 4-6 hours
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Can increase heart rate, blood pressure, anxiety
   - AVOID if: Hypertension, heart disease, anxiety disorder
   - Brand names: Sudafed (formulas vary by region)

6. OXYMETAZOLINE (Nasal spray)
   - Decongests nasal passages topically
   - Dosage: 1-2 sprays each nostril, twice daily
   - AVOID: Regular use > 3 days (causes rebound congestion/rhinitis medicamentosa)
   - Use only as directed

INTRANASAL CORTICOSTEROIDS (Most effective for allergic rhinitis):
7. FLUTICASONE / MOMETASONE / TRIAMCINOLONE
   - Reduce inflammation in nasal passages
   - Fluticasone: 1-2 sprays each nostril once or twice daily
   - Mometasone: 2 sprays each nostril daily
   - Onset: 12-24 hours
   - Duration: Builds up with regular use; takes 2-3 days for full effect
   - Brand names: Flonase, Avamys, Nasonex
   - SAFE: Minimal systemic absorption if used as directed
   - Most effective single agent for allergic rhinitis

LEUKOTRIENE ANTAGONISTS (Block inflammatory pathway):
8. MONTELUKAST (Singulair)
   - Dosage: 10 mg once daily in evening
   - Works for nasal allergies, especially if asthma coexistent
   - Takes 1-2 weeks for full effect
   - Fewer side effects than earlier formulations

NASAL SALINE RINSE:
9. NASAL SALINE IRRIGATION
   - Neti pot or saline spray
   - Flushes allergens and mucus from nasal passages
   - No side effects, safe long-term use
   - Helps reduce medication dependence

TOPICAL TREATMENTS (For skin allergies):
10. HYDROCORTISONE CREAM
    - Dosage: 1% cream, apply 2-4 times daily
    - For mild dermatitis
    - Brand names: Various

11. STRONGER TOPICAL CORTICOSTEROIDS (Prescription)
    - For moderate-severe dermatitis

12. EMOLLIENTS / MOISTURIZERS
    - Cetaphil, Eucerin
    - Apply multiple times daily
    - Prevention and treatment of dry skin

IMMUNOTHERAPY (Long-term treatment):
13. ALLERGY SHOTS (Immunosuppression)
    - Gradually increasing allergen exposure builds tolerance
    - Takes 3-5 years of regular injections
    - 60-90% effective when completed
    - For specific allergen sensitivities

14. SUBLINGUAL TABLETS
    - Allergen tablets placed under tongue
    - Becoming popular alternative to shots

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID:
- Decongestants in hypertension or heart disease (pseudoephedrine can spike BP)
- NSAIDs in some patients (can worsen allergic reactions)
- Beta-blockers if anaphylaxis risk (can worsen anaphylaxis)
- Beta-blockers with epinephrine treatment controversy (consult doctor)

ALLERGEN AVOIDANCE (Most important step):

FOR POLLEN ALLERGIES:
- Keep windows closed during high pollen seasons
- Wear sunglasses outdoors (protects eyes)
- Shower and shampoo after being outdoors (removes pollen)
- Wash clothes separately from other laundry (minimize pollen spread)
- Use pollen filters in car
- Monitor pollen count, plan outdoor activities accordingly
- Dry laundry indoors (not on clothesline)

FOR DUST MITE ALLERGIES:
- Encase mattress and pillow in allergy-proof covers
- Wash bedding in hot water weekly (>130°F kills dust mites)
- Reduce humidity (dust mites thrive in moisture)
- Use HEPA filters in vacuum and air purifier
- Minimize stuffed animals and plush toys
- Regular cleaning reduces dust accumulation

FOR PET DANDER ALLERGIES:
- Ideally, avoid pets if severe allergy
- If keeping pet: Keep out of bedroom, vacuum frequently with HEPA filter
- Bathe pet regularly (reduces dander)
- Use air purifier in bedroom

FOR MOLD ALLERGIES:
- Fix leaks and prevent moisture accumulation
- Use dehumidifier (keep humidity <50%)
- Clean bathrooms with bleach solution
- Remove moldy materials
- Increase ventilation

FOR FOOD ALLERGIES:
- Strict avoidance of allergen
- Check food labels carefully
- Inform restaurants of allergies
- Severe allergies: Carry EpiPen (epinephrine auto-injector)
- Medical alert identification

FOR DRUG ALLERGIES:
- Maintain list of allergic medications
- Inform healthcare providers
- Consider allergy testing to clarify true allergy vs. side effect
- Some non-cross-reacting alternatives often available

FOODS TO EAT (Anti-inflammatory, supportive):
- Quercetin-rich: Apples, onions, green tea (natural antihistamine)
- Omega-3: Fish, flaxseed (anti-inflammatory)
- Vitamin C: Citrus, kiwi (natural antihistamine, not proven but supportive)
- Turmeric / Haldi: Curcumin is anti-inflammatory
- Ginger / Adrak: Anti-inflammatory
- Leafy greens / Palak, methi: Antioxidants
- Honey (local): Anecdotal evidence of desensitization (not proven)
- Probiotics: Yogurt, fermented foods (supporting evidence for immunity)

FOODS TO AVOID (May worsen allergies or contain allergens):
- Histamine-rich foods: Aged cheeses, cured meats, fermented foods (in histamine intolerance)
- Cross-reactivity foods: If allergic to birch pollen, may react to apple, hazelnut, carrot raw
  (cooking often prevents reaction)
- High-histamine foods: Avocado, eggplant, tomato, chocolate (if sensitive)
- Food additives: Sulfites, food dyes
- Excess alcohol (increases histamine)

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:
- Air filtration: HEPA filters in bedroom and living areas
- Humidity control: 40-50% relative humidity (dust mites and mold thriving range)
- Regular laundering: Allergen-free bedding, pillowcases weekly
- Furnishing choices: Hardwood/tile instead of carpet (if possible), washable curtains
- Pets: Consider dander-free pets or no pets if severe allergy
- Regular cleaning: Vacuum with HEPA filter 1-2 times weekly
- Strategic planning: Check pollen counts before outdoor activities
- Stress management: Stress exacerbates allergies

WHEN TO SEEK MEDICAL ATTENTION:
- Anaphylaxis: Difficulty breathing, swelling of throat/tongue, hypotension — SEEK EMERGENCY CARE IMMEDIATELY
- Severe skin reactions: Extensive blistering, skin infection (signs of weeping, pus)
- Allergies not controlled after 2 weeks treatment
- Allergic rhinitis causing sleep problems and fatigue
- Considering immunotherapy (allergy shots) — requires allergy testing and specialist

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Pregnancy: Avoid first-generation antihistamines; second-generation and intranasal corticosteroids generally safe
- Children: Different dosing; avoid first-generation sedating antihistamines (behavior/learning effects)
- Elderly: Age increases allergy risk; second-generation antihistamines preferred
- Comorbid asthma: Control allergies to reduce asthma triggers; montelukast helps both
- Anaphylaxis: EpiPen prescription, self-injection training, emergency action plan essential

LONG-TERM MANAGEMENT:
- Regular preventive medication during allergy season
- Allergy testing to identify specific allergens (allergy shots if indicated)
- Combination approach most effective: Avoidance + medication + immunotherapy
