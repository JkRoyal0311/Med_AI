DISEASE: Anemia (Iron Deficiency Anemia)
CATEGORY: Hematological / Metabolic
ICD-10: D50
SEVERITY: Mild-Moderate usually treatable; severe anemia is serious

DESCRIPTION:
Anemia occurs when there are insufficient healthy red blood cells (RBCs) to carry oxygen
to body tissues. Iron deficiency anemia is the most common type globally, caused by low iron stores.
Blood with low hemoglobin cannot carry adequate oxygen, causing fatigue and weakness.
Common causes: Poor diet, blood loss, pregnancy, malabsorption.

CAUSES OF IRON DEFICIENCY ANEMIA:
- Insufficient iron in diet (vegetarian, low meat intake)
- Chronic blood loss: Heavy menstrual bleeding, GI bleeding (ulcers, hemorrhoids)
- Poor absorption: Celiac disease, Crohn's disease, surgical removal of part of intestine
- Pregnancy and breastfeeding: Increased iron demands
- Blood donation frequency: Especially in regular donors
- Parasitic infections: Hookworm (common in India)
- Medications: Some reduce iron absorption

SYMPTOMS (depend on severity & speed of onset):
MILD ANEMIA (Hemoglobin 10-12 g/dL):
- Often no symptoms initially
- Mild fatigue with strenuous activity
- Slight shortness of breath with exertion

MODERATE ANEMIA (Hemoglobin 7-10 g/dL):
- Fatigue and weakness (most common)
- Shortness of breath with normal activity
- Dizziness and lightheadedness
- Frequent headaches
- Pale skin and pale inner eyelid (conjunctiva)
- Pale nail beds
- Cold hands and feet
- Rapid or irregular heartbeat
- Difficulty concentrating ("brain fog")
- Irritability
- Sore or swollen tongue (glossitis)
- Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia)

SEVERE ANEMIA (Hemoglobin < 7 g/dL):
- Severe shortness of breath even at rest
- Chest pain or pressure
- Severe fatigue — difficulty doing basic tasks
- Fainting
- Tachycardia (rapid heart rate > 100 bpm)
- Heart palpitations
- Mental confusion
- RISK: Heart failure if untreated

CURIOUS SYMPTOMS OF IRON DEFICIENCY:
- Pica: Craving non-food items (ice, dirt, chalk) — indicates severe deficiency
- Restless leg syndrome: Uncomfortable sensation in legs at night

DIAGNOSIS:
- Hemoglobin level: Normal 13.5-17.5 g/dL (men), 12-15.5 g/dL (women)
- Iron studies: Serum ferritin, serum iron, TIBC
- Mean corpuscular volume (MCV): Low in iron deficiency
- If below 12 g/dL or symptoms present: Medical evaluation needed

MEDICATIONS USED:

IRON SUPPLEMENTS:
1. IRON FERROUS SULFATE (Oral)
   - First-line treatment
   - Dosage: 325 mg (65 mg elemental iron) once daily, preferably morning
   - Best absorbed on empty stomach, but can take with food if GI upset
   - Take with vitamin C (orange juice) for better absorption
   - Brand names: Ferroglobin, Hemalate, Ferofort
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Dark colored stools (normal), constipation, abdominal discomfort
   - Takes 2-4 weeks to see improvement, 2-3 months for full response

2. IRON FERRIC HYDROXIDE POLYMALTOSE
   - Better tolerated, fewer GI side effects
   - Dosage: 100-200 mg once daily
   - Brand names: Avaglim, Hemobax

3. IRON INTRAVENOUS (IV)
   - For severe anemia (Hb < 5 g/dL)
   - For patients unable to tolerate oral iron
   - Faster response than oral supplements
   - Used in hospitals

VITAMIN AND MINERAL SUPPLEMENTS:
4. VITAMIN B12 (Cyanocobalamin)
   - Often low with iron deficiency
   - Dosage: 500-1000 mcg daily or weekly injections
   - Brand names: Cycobal, Neurobion

5. FOLIC ACID
   - Works with iron to build RBCs
   - Dosage: 1-5 mg daily
   - Important in pregnancy

6. VITAMIN C (Ascorbic Acid)
   - Enhances iron absorption
   - 100-200 mg daily with iron supplement
   - Take together, any time

TREATMENT OF UNDERLYING CAUSE:
- If heavy menstrual bleeding: Hormonal birth control may help
- If GI bleeding: Treat ulcers, control hemorrhoids
- If poor diet: Dietary counseling

MEDICATIONS THAT REDUCE IRON ABSORPTION:
- Antacids: Reduce stomach acid (needed for iron absorption)
- Proton pump inhibitors (PPI): Reduce acid further
- Tetracycline antibiotics: Can bind to iron, inhibit absorption
- Calcium supplements: If taken at same time as iron, reduce absorption

IRON-RICH FOODS (most important!):
- Red meat / Gosht: Highest iron content, best absorbed form (heme iron)
- Chicken / Murgh: Moderate iron, enhances absorption of plant iron
- Liver / Kaleja: Extremely high iron and B vitamins
- Spinach / Palak: High iron (though some binds to oxalates)
- Beans and lentils / Dal: Good source, but pair with meat to enhance absorption
- Fortified cereals and grains
- Pumpkin seeds / Kaddu ke beej: High iron
- Cashews / Kaju: Moderate iron
- Dates / Khajoor: Good sweet source
- Raisins / Kishmish: Dried fruits concentrate iron
- Eggs: Moderate iron (yolk), enhances plant iron absorption

VEGETARIAN IRON SOURCES (plant-based/non-heme):
- Legumes: Chickpeas / Chana, red lentils / Masoor
- Leafy greens: Cooked spinach (cooking reduces oxalates that block iron)
- Seeds: Sesame / Til, pumpkin, beetroot leaves
- Whole grains: Brown rice, oats
- Dried fruits: Apricots, prunes
- Molasses (unsulfured)
BOOST ABSORPTION: Eat with vitamin C (citrus), meat, or fish

FOODS THAT REDUCE IRON ABSORPTION (avoid with iron-rich meals):
- Tea and coffee: Tannins bind iron, reduce absorption
- Calcium supplements: If taken simultaneously (separate by 2 hours)
- Whole grains and high-fiber foods: Phytates bind iron when eaten together
- Red wine (tannins)
- Dairy excess: Less of a problem, but can interfere

FOODS TO STRICTLY AVOID DURING TREATMENT:
- Avoid restricting diet — ANY iron helps
- Alcohol excess: Damages liver, reduces iron metabolism
- Just avoid combining iron-blocking foods with iron-rich meals

SPECIAL DIETARY COMBINATIONS FOR MAXIMUM ABSORPTION:
- Spinach + Citrus (orange juice): Vitamin C enhances iron absorption
- Lentils + Meat: Meat enhances plant iron absorption (heme iron enhances non-heme)
- Red meat + Green vegetables: Best combination
- Pulses + Vegetables: Daily combinations improve overall iron intake

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:
- Regular exercise: Promotes red blood cell production
- Adequate sleep: 7-9 hours nightly
- Stress reduction: Chronic stress impairs iron absorption

PREGNANCY-SPECIFIC:
- Iron requirements double during pregnancy (from 8 mg to 27 mg/day)
- Universal iron supplementation recommended 3rd trimester
- Anemia in pregnancy increases risk of preterm delivery and low birth weight
- Postpartum hemorrhage risk higher if anemic during pregnancy

MONITORING TREATMENT:
- Hemoglobin checked 6-8 weeks after starting treatment
- Should increase 1 g/dL every 2-3 weeks
- After reaching normal, continue low-dose iron for 3 months to replete stores
- Annual monitoring for someone with history of deficiency

WHEN TO SEEK DOCTOR:
- Persistent fatigue despite treatment (may indicate another cause)
- GI symptoms: Severe constipation, abdominal pain
- New symptoms: Chest pain, severe shortness of breath
- Not improving after 2 months of treatment
- For severe anemia: Hemoglobin < 7 g/dL

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Children: Can develop developmental delays if severely anemic
- Elderly: May have underlying GI bleeding — investigate cause
- Women of childbearing age: Screen regularly if heavy periods
- Vegetarians: Need higher iron intake, consider supplementation
- Chronic kidney disease: Leads to anemia (different cause, different treatment)
