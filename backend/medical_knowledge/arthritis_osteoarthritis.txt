DISEASE: Osteoarthritis (Degenerative Joint Disease)
CATEGORY: Musculoskeletal / Rheumatological
ICD-10: M15-M19
SEVERITY: Chronic — progressive if untreated, but manageable with treatment

DESCRIPTION:
Osteoarthritis (OA) is the degeneration of joint cartilage and underlying bone over time.
Cartilage acts as a shock absorber; when it wears away, bones can rub together causing pain,
stiffness, and reduced motion. Most common type of arthritis, especially in knees, hips, hands,
and spine. Age, genetics, and joint stress are main risk factors.

AFFECTED JOINTS (most common):
- Knees (weight-bearing)
- Hips (weight-bearing)
- Hands (finger joints)
- Spine (neck and lower back)
- Great toe (foot)

SYMPTOMS:
- Joint pain: Worsens with activity, improves with rest (early stage)
- Morning stiffness: Lasting 30 minutes or longer
- Joint stiffness: Improves with movement
- Swelling around affected joints
- Reduced joint mobility and flexibility
- Cracking or grinding sensation in joint (crepitus): Without pain initially
- Muscle weakness around joint
- Difficulty with activities: Climbing stairs, walking, gripping objects
- Pain worse in damp, cold weather (not scientifically proven but commonly reported)

PROGRESSION STAGES:
- Mild: Occasional pain, normal activity possible
- Moderate: Regular pain, activity limited but manageable
- Severe: Constant pain, significant loss of function, may need walking aids

MEDICATIONS USED:

FOR PAIN & INFLAMMATION:
1. PARACETAMOL (Acetaminophen)
   - Mild to moderate pain relief
   - Dosage: 500 mg every 6 hours (max 2g/day)
   - Brand names: Crocin, Dolo
   - SAFEST choice, minimal GI side effects

2. NSAIDS (Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs)
   - Reduce inflammation and pain
   - Ibuprofen: 400 mg 3 times daily with food
   - Diclofenac: 50 mg 2 times daily with food
   - Naproxen: 250 mg 2 times daily
   - Brand names: Ibugesic, Voveran, Calpol
   - TAKE WITH FOOD to reduce stomach upset
   - AVOID if: Kidney disease, heart disease, history of ulcer

3. INTRA-ARTICULAR INJECTIONS (for single painful joint):
   - Hyaluronic acid: Joint lubrication (viscosupplementation)
   - Corticosteroid: Short-term inflammation reduction (not repeated often)
   - Platelet-rich plasma (PRP): Newer, experimental

JOINT SUPPORT & DISEASE-MODIFYING:
4. GLUCOSAMINE & CHONDROITIN (Nutritional supplements)
   - Help maintain cartilage structure
   - Evidence is mixed, but many patients report benefit
   - Dosage: 1500 mg glucosamine + 1200 mg chondroitin daily
   - Take for 3 months minimum to assess benefit
   - Brand names: Sona, Osteocare

5. MSMS (Methylsulfonylmethane)
   - May reduce pain and inflammation
   - Dosage: 1-4 grams daily
   - Often combined with glucosamine

6. COLLAGEN PEPTIDES
   - Support cartilage regeneration
   - Dosage: 10-15 grams daily
   - May help with mobility after 2-3 months

TOPICAL CREAMS:
7. DICLOFENAC GEL / IBUPROFEN GEL
   - For localized joint pain (hand, knee, foot)
   - Apply 3-4 times daily
   - Brand names: Voveran gel, Ibugesic gel

PHYSICAL THERAPY & DEVICES:
- Knee braces: Support joint during activity
- Walking aids: Cane, walker to reduce joint stress
- Occupational therapy: Adaptive techniques for daily activities
- Physical therapy: Exercises to strengthen supporting muscles

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID:
- Aspirin: Higher dose NSAIDs, not recommended for OA
- High-dose NSAIDs continuously: Risk of kidney damage, ulcers, GI bleeding
- Excess corticosteroids: Can worsen bone thinning

FOODS TO EAT:
- Fatty fish (Salmon, Mackerel): Omega-3 reduces inflammation
- Turmeric / Haldi: Curcumin is anti-inflammatory
- Ginger / Adrak: Anti-inflammatory, reduces pain
- Garlic / Lahsun: Anti-inflammatory compounds
- Green leafy vegetables (Palak, methi): Vitamin K, antioxidants
- Citrus fruits (Orange, Mosambi): Vitamin C for collagen formation
- Nuts and seeds (Almonds, Walnuts): Antioxidants, magnesium
- Bone broth: Contains collagen and amino acids
- Eggs: Choline and vitamin D
- Beans and lentils / Dal: Protein, antioxidants
- Whole grains: B vitamins
- Olive oil: Oleocanthal has NSAID-like effect

FOODS TO STRICTLY AVOID:
- Trans fats: Increase inflammation (packaged, fried foods)
- Excess sugar and refined carbs: Promote inflammation
- Alcohol: Interferes with inflammation control (limit to 1-2 drinks/week)
- Red meat excess: High in omega-6, pro-inflammatory
- Processed foods: High sodium, additives trigger inflammation
- Saturated fats: Increase inflammation markers
- Nightshade vegetables (for some people): Tomato, potato, eggplant, chili pepper
  (Not scientifically proven but anecdotally worsen symptoms in some patients)

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:
- Weight loss: Every pound reduces knee joint stress by 4 pounds
- Exercise: Low-impact activities (swimming, walking, cycling) 30 min, 5 days/week
- Stretching: Daily gentle stretching 10-15 minutes
- Heat therapy: Warm bath, heating pad 15-20 minutes before activity
- Cold therapy: Ice pack 15 minutes after aggravating activity
- Adequate sleep: 7-8 hours nightly (inflammation increases with poor sleep)
- Stress management: Chronic stress increases inflammation

EXERCISES TO BUILD STRENGTH:
- Quadriceps strengthening: Leg lifts, wall sits (if knee OA)
- Hip strengthening: Clamshells, side-lying leg lifts (if hip OA)
- Hand exercises: Squeezing therapy ball, dexterity exercises (if hand OA)
- Core strengthening: Protects joints (planks, bird-dogs)
- Balance training: Tai Chi, yoga (reduces fall risk)

ACTIVITIES TO AVOID:
- High-impact sports: Running, jumping, tennis on hard courts
- Heavy weight lifting: Can damage joints
- Prolonged standing: Alternate with sitting
- Repetitive joint stress: Avoid activities that specifically aggravate the joint
- Climbing many stairs: Use elevators, ramps when possible

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- No cure: Goal is symptom management and slowing progression
- Early intervention important: Once cartilage severely damaged, harder to treat
- Seasonal variation: Cold weather increases stiffness and pain for many
- Individual response: Response to treatment varies; may need medication adjustments
- Progression: Can lead to severe disability if untreated; early diagnosis prevents this
