DISEASE: Gastric Ulcer / Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)
CATEGORY: Gastrointestinal
ICD-10: K25 (gastric ulcer), K26 (duodenal ulcer)
SEVERITY: Mild-Moderate; can be serious if complicated (bleeding, perforation)

DESCRIPTION:
Peptic ulcer disease is erosion of mucosa (inner lining) of stomach or duodenum creating open sore.
Caused primarily by H. pylori infection (60%) or NSAIDs (25%). Can cause pain, bleeding, perforation.
Once common, now less frequent in developed countries due to H. pylori treatment. Still significant in India.

CAUSES:

H. PYLORI INFECTION (Most common — ~60%):
- Gram-negative bacterium colonizing stomach
- Transmitted via contaminated food/water, poor hygiene
- Causes chronic inflammation

NSAID use (25%):
- Aspirin, Ibuprofen, Diclofenac, Naproxen
- Damage protective mucus layer and reduced PG synthesis

RARE CAUSES:
- Zollinger-Ellison syndrome: Gastrin-secreting tumor
- Stress ulcers: From severe stress (now rare)
- Crohn's disease: GI inflammation

SYMPTOMS:

PAIN:
- Epigastric (upper abdominal) pain: Below ribs, center
- Quality: Burning, gnawing, aching
- Timing: Often 2-3 hours after eating or at night
- Duration: Minutes to hours
- Trigger: Foods, stress, NSAIDs
- Relief: Food, antacids (initially, but worsens for duodenal ulcers with eating)

GASTROINTESTINAL:
- Nausea and vomiting
- Loss of appetite
- Early satiety: Feeling full quickly
- Bloating and gas
- Heartburn/regurgitation

SIGNS OF COMPLICATIONS:
- Severe pain: Sudden onset, severe (possible perforation)
- Vomiting blood (hematemesis): Dark coffee-ground appearance
- Melena: Black, tarry stools (upper GI bleeding)
- Severe anemia symptoms: Fatigue, shortness of breath

DIAGNOSIS:
- H. pylori testing: Urea breath test (most sensitive), stool antigen, serology
- Upper endoscopy (esophagogastroduodenoscopy / EGD): Direct visualization
  * Gold standard, can biopsy, treat
- Barium X-ray: Less common now (less diagnostic)
- Gastric pH testing: Assess acid level

MEDICATIONS USED:

FOR H. PYLORI INFECTION (Triple or Quadruple therapy):
1. OMEPRAZOLE (PPI)
   - Dosage: 20 mg twice daily for 10-14 days
   - PLUS:
   
2. AMOXICILLIN
   - Dosage: 1000 mg twice daily for 10-14 days
   - PLUS:

3. CLARITHROMYCIN (Macrolide antibiotic)
   - Dosage: 500 mg twice daily for 10-14 days
   
   [TRIPLE THERAPY — success rate ~80-85%]

ALTERNATIVE (Quadruple therapy — higher success):
1. Omeprazole: 20 mg twice daily
2. Bismuth subsalicylate: 525 mg four times daily
3. Metronidazole: 500 mg three times daily
4. Tetracycline: 500 mg four times daily
   [14 days — success rate ~90%]

AFTER H. PYLORI ERADICATION:
4. ACID SUPPRESSION (Continue for 4 weeks):
   - Omeprazole: 20 mg once daily
   - OR Lansoprazole: 30 mg once daily
   - To allow ulcer healing

FOR NSAID-INDUCED ULCER:
- Stop NSAID (if possible)
- Use PPI for 4-8 weeks for healing
- Omeprazole: 20-40 mg daily (doubled dose)
- Together with H2-blocker initially if severe

SYMPTOM RELIEF:
5. H2-RECEPTOR ANTAGONISTS (Less potent than PPI, for symptom relief):
   - Ranitidine: 150 mg twice daily
   - Famotidine: 20 mg twice daily

ANTACIDS (For temporary relief):
6. CALCIUM CARBONATE or ALUMINUM HYDROXIDE
   - As needed for pain (up to 3-4 times daily)
   - Works within 30 minutes but short duration (1-3 hours)

PROTECTIVE AGENT:
7. SUCRALFATE
   - Dosage: 1 gram four times daily (1 hour before meals and bedtime)
   - Creates protective coating over ulcer
   - Takes 4-8 weeks for healing
   - CAUTION: Interferes with absorption of other medications (take separately)

PROKINETICS (If gastroparesis):
8. METOCLOPRAMIDE
   - Dosage: 10 mg three times daily before meals
   - Enhances gastric emptying

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID:
- NSAIDs: If ulcer active; use acetaminophen for pain instead
- Aspirin: Avoid during active ulcer
- Steroids: Can worsen ulcer
- Potassium chloride supplements: Can cause ulceration (if needed, use liquid/diluted)

FOODS TO EAT:
- Milk: Buffering effect (controversial, but generally safe moderation)
- Plain yogurt / Dahi: Probiotics, calcium
- Lean meats: Chicken, fish, turkey
- Eggs: Gentle protein
- Whole grains: Brown rice, whole wheat (fiber)
- Legumes / Dal: Protein
- Leafy greens / Palak, methi: Nutrients
- Bananas: Soothing, potassium
- Apples: Gentle fruit
- Honey / Shahad: Antimicrobial, soothing
- Turmeric / Haldi: Anti-inflammatory
- Ginger / Adrak: Anti-inflammatory, improves digestion
- Cabbage juice: Traditional remedy (antacid properties)
- Garlic / Lahsun: Antimicrobial (but trigger for some people)
- Licorice: DGL (deglycyrrhizinated) helps healing (not raw licorice)

FOODS TO STRICTLY AVOID:
- Coffee: Stimulates acid production (even decaf)
- Chocolate: Cocoa stimulates acidic secretion
- Citrus fruits: Orange, lemon (acidic)
- Tomato and tomato products: Highly acidic
- Spicy foods: Chili peppers, excess spices (trigger pain)
- Fatty/fried foods: Delay gastric emptying, trigger acid
- Alcohol: Irritates lining, increases acid
- Caffeine: In tea, cola (stimulates acid)
- Milk (excess): Can rebound with more acid
- Refined sugars: Impairs healing
- Carbonated beverages: Increase bloating, acid irritation
- High-fat dairy: Full-fat cheese, cream (delay emptying)

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:
- Small, frequent meals: 5-6 small instead of 3 large
- Avoid eating within 3 hours of bedtime: Increases nighttime acid
- Stress reduction: Stress increases acid
- Smoking cessation: Smoking increases acid, impairs healing
- Limit alcohol: Or eliminate
- Avoid NSAIDs: Or use lowest dose shortest duration with PPI protection
- Elevate head of bed: 6 inches (reduces nocturnal acid reflux)
- Avoid lying down after eating
- Chew gum: Increases saliva (buffering effect) — sugar-free only
- Manage stress: Meditation, yoga, exercise
- Regular exercise: But not immediately after eating

SPECIAL RECOMMENDATIONS:
- Monitor symptoms: Even after treatment starts
- Repeat H. pylori testing: 4 weeks after eradication therapy (confirm success)
- Avoid re-infection: Hygiene practices, safe water in endemic areas

WHEN TO SEEK URGENT ATTENTION:
- Severe, sudden epigastric pain: Possible perforation
  [SEEK EMERGENCY CARE IMMEDIATELY]
- Vomiting blood (hematemesis): Upper GI bleeding
  [SEEK EMERGENCY CARE IMMEDIATELY]
- Black, tarry stools (melena): Upper GI bleeding
  [SEEK EMERGENCY CARE IMMEDIATELY]
- Signs of shock: Lightheadedness, rapid heart rate (from bleeding)
  [SEEK EMERGENCY CARE IMMEDIATELY]

WHEN TO SEE DOCTOR:
- Epigastric pain >1 week
- Symptoms not improving on antacids
- Suspect H. pylori: Test and treatment if positive
- Starting NSAID long-term: May need PPI prophylaxis
- Severe pain: Need evaluation to rule out other causes

COMPLICATIONS (If untreated):
- Bleeding: From eroded blood vessels (hematemesis, melena)
  * Can cause severe anemia
  * Requires endoscopy for hemostasis
- Perforation: Ulcer erodes through full thickness
  * Sudden, severe pain
  * Abdominal rigidity
  * Peritonitis (life-threatening)
  * Requires emergency surgery
- Penetration: Ulcer erodes into adjacent organs (pancreas)
  * Severe pain
  * Elevated amylase
- Obstruction: From scarring/strictures (rare now)
  * Vomiting, early satiety
  * May need surgery

HEALING TIMELINE:
- H. pylori treatment: Takes 10-14 days
- Ulcer healing: 4-8 weeks after treatment starts
- Symptom relief: Days to weeks (may persist even after ulcer heals)

MONITORING:
- Symptom improvement: Should see within 1-2 weeks
- If not improving: Repeat testing for H. pylori, reassess diagnosis
- Follow-up endoscopy: Generally NOT needed if H. pylori test confirms eradication
- NSAID users: May need prophylactic PPI (discuss with doctor)

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- H. pylori treatment compliance: Must take all antibiotics (don't stop early)
- Antibiotic resistance: Increasing; some treatments failing (use recommended regimens)
- Pregnancy: Most PPI/H2 blockers safe; bismuth-based therapy avoided
- Elderly: Higher risk of complications, medication interactions
- Chronic kidney disease: Dosing adjustment needed for many medications
- Smoking: Doubles healing time, increases complications
