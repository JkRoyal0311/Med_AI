DISEASE: COVID-19 (Coronavirus Disease 2019)
CATEGORY: Infectious / Viral
ICD-10: U07.1
SEVERITY: Asymptomatic to Critical; age and comorbidities predict severity

DESCRIPTION:
COVID-19 is respiratory illness caused by SARS-CoV-2 coronavirus. Emerged December 2019, became
pandemic. Primarily affects lungs but multisystem effects now recognized. Highly transmissible. Most
asymptomatic/mild, but can progress to severe pneumonia, acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS),
thromboembolism. Vaccines highly effective at preventing severe disease.

TRANSMISSION:
- Respiratory droplets: Coughing, sneezing, talking (especially indoors)
- Aerosol: Small particles linger in air (enclosed spaces)
- From presymptomatic to symptomatic period: Infectiousness variable per individual
- Incubation period: 2-14 days (typically 5-7)
- Communicable period: Up to 10 days after symptom onset (longer if immunocompromised)
- Fomite transmission: Surfaces (surface transmission lower than initially thought)

SYMPTOMS:

ASYMPTOMATIC:
- 40-50% infected have no symptoms
- Still potentially transmissible

MILD-MODERATE (80% symptomatic):
- Fever: Often first symptom (102-103°F / 39-39.4°C)
  * May be absent in elderly
- Cough: Dry cough (with sputum less common initially)
- Fatigue: Profound ("bone-breaking")
- Shortness of breath: Mild, with exertion
- Loss of taste/smell: Anosmia, ageusia (distinctive feature)
- Headache: Often severe
- Muscle/body aches: Myalgia
- Sore throat: Pharyngitis
- Nausea/vomiting: More common in children
- Diarrhea: GI involvement

SEVERE (15-20% need hospitalization):
- Shortness of breath at rest: Dyspnea
- Chest pain: Especially with breathing
- Confusion or altered mental status
- Hypoxemia: O2 sat <94% on room air
- Cyanosis: Bluish skin from low oxygen
- Rapid respiratory rate: >30 breaths/minute

CRITICAL (ICU admission):
- ARDS: Acute respiratory distress syndrome (bilateral pneumonia)
- Sepsis: Systemic infection response
- Septic shock: Hypotension unresponsive to fluids
- Thromboembolism: DVT, PE, stroke
- Myocarditis: Heart muscle inflammation
- Organ failure: Kidney, liver, multiorgan
- Mortality: 1-5% hospitalized (higher in elderly, comorbidities)

POST-COVID SYMPTOMS ("Long COVID"):
- Persistent fatigue: Debilitating
- Dyspnea: Persistent shortness of breath (weeks-months)
- Brain fog: Cognitive impairment, difficulty concentrating
- Chest pain: Can persist
- Sleep disturbance
- Joint pain: Myalgia
- Sensory disturbance: Dysgeusia (taste), dysgeusia (smell)
- Palpitations
- Exercise intolerance: Can't return to baseline activity level

DIAGNOSIS:

MOLECULAR TESTING (Gold standard):
- RT-PCR (Reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction): Most sensitive
  * Nasopharyngeal swab (URT)
  * Oropharyngeal swab
  * Saliva (less sensitive but easier)
- Takes 1-3 days for results
- Positive: Confirmed active infection

ANTIGEN TESTING (Rapid):
- Rapid antigen test: 15-30 minutes results
- Lower sensitivity than PCR (85-95%)
- High specificity
- Negative rapid doesn't rule out (especially if high pretest probability)

SEROLOGY (Antibody testing):
- IgG: Indicates past or recent infection (weeks to months later)
- IgM: Early infection marker (less specific)

IMAGING:
- Chest X-ray: If respiratory symptoms, looking for pneumonia
  * Findings: Bilateral infiltrates ("ground glass"), interstitial pattern
- CT chest: Higher sensitivity for pneumonia
  * Bilateral lower lobe infiltrates typical

CLINICAL LABS:
- CBC: WBC count variable (can be low, normal, or high)
- CRP, D-dimer, ferritin: Elevated in severe disease
- LDH: Elevated (marker of lung injury)
- Liver enzymes: May be elevated
- D-dimer: Elevated (thromboembolism risk marker)
- Arterial/venous blood gas: Hypoxemia assessment

MEDICATIONS USED:

FOR MILD DISEASE:
- Supportive care: Rest, fluids, fever management

1. ACETAMINOPHEN or IBUPROFEN
   - Dosage: Acetaminophen 500-1000 mg every 6 hours; Ibuprofen 400-600 mg every 6 hours
   - Fever, pain relief
   - Both acceptable (earlier concerns about NSAIDs not sustained)

2. COUGH SUPPRESSANTS:
   - Dextromethorphan: 10-20 mg every 4-6 hours
   - Guaifenesin: Expectorant (helps thin mucus)

ANTIVIRAL (If high-risk, within 5 days of symptom onset):

3. PAXLOVID (Nirmatrelvir + Ritonavir)
   - Dosage: 300 mg/100 mg twice daily x 5 days
   - Reduces symptomatic infection by ~88% (decreases progression to severe/death by ~90%)
   - Most effective if given within 5 days
   - DRUG INTERACTIONS: Many (ritonavir is CYP3A4 inhibitor)
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Dysgeusia (altered taste — very common), nausea
   - Effective against variants including Omicron
   - NOT for prophylaxis unless immunocompromised high-risk

4. REMDESIVIR (Veklury)
   - IV antiviral, used in hospitalized patients
   - Dosage: 200 mg first day, then 100 mg days 2-5
   - Shortens median hospital stay by 4 days
   - Used in hypoxemic patients
   - Hospital-based only

FOR MODERATE-SEVERE HOSPITALIZED DISEASE:

5. CORTICOSTEROIDS:
   - Dexamethasone or Methylprednisolone
   - Reduces mortality in hypoxemic patients
   - Dosage varies by clinical status
   - Most important in severe disease

6. TOCILIZUMAB (Monoclonal antibody to IL-6):
   - For hospitalized high-inflammatory state
   - Reduces mortality in severe disease

7. VENOUS THROMBOEMBOLISM PROPHYLAXIS:
   - Enoxaparin (Low-molecular-weight heparin): SC injection
   - Routine for hospitalized COVID patients (high thrombosis risk)
   - Prevents DVT, PE

8. OXYGEN THERAPY:
   - Nasal cannula: Low-flow (2-4 L/min)
   - Non-rebreather mask: High-flow (up to 15 L/min)
   - High-flow nasal cannula (HFNC): Hospital
   - Mechanical ventilation: If ARDS

IMMUNIZATION (VACCINATION):
- CRITICAL: Most important prevention
- mRNA vaccines (Pfizer-BioNTech, Moderna): >95% efficacy against severe disease
- Viral vector vaccines (AstraZeneca, Janssen): ~80% efficacy
- Schedule: Primary series (2 doses standard) + boosters (timing varies)
- Omicron booster: Bivalent boosters (original + Omicron) recommended
- Annual "vaccines" may be future approach

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID:
- Antibiotics: Only if secondary bacterial infection
- Antivirals of unproven efficacy: Hydroxychloroquine (shown ineffective), ivermectin (unproven, dangerous at high doses)

FOODS TO EAT (Recovery support):
- Broths: Nutritious, hydrating (chicken broth traditional)
- Fruits: Citrus (vitamin C), berries, papaya
- Vegetables: Leafy greens, ginger (anti-inflammatory)
- Lean proteins: Chicken, fish, eggs (rebuild)
- Whole grains: Brown rice, oats
- Legumes / Dal: Protein, fiber
- Turmeric / Haldi: Anti-inflammatory
- Ginger / Adrak: Aids digestion, anti-inflammatory
- Honey / Shahad: Soothing, antimicrobial
- Garlic / Lahsun: Immune support
- Nuts and seeds: Magnesium, selenium

FOODS TO AVOID:
- Heavily processed foods: During and after illness
- High sugar: Energy spikes/crashes
- Spicy foods: Initially irritating if throat sore
- Alcohol: Dehydrating, immune suppression

HYDRATION:
- Critical: 2-3 liters fluid daily
- Water, electrolyte drinks (coconut water), broths
- Monitor urine color (should be light yellow)

LEISURE/ACTIVITY:
- Rest: Critical early on
- Gradual return to activities: 1-2 weeks minimum before exercise
- Long COVID: Slower recovery, pacing essential (post-exertional malaise)

WHEN TO SEEK URGENT ATTENTION:
- Shortness of breath: At rest or mild exertion
- Chest pain or pressure
- Confusion or altered mental status
- Cyanosis: Bluish lips, fingers
- Severe fatigue: Inability to get out of bed
- High fever >103°F that won't break with medication

[SEEK EMERGENCY CARE IMMEDIATELY IF SEVERE RESPIRATORY DISTRESS, CHEST PAIN, ALTERED CONSCIOUSNESS]

WHEN TO SEE DOCTOR:
- Suspected COVID: Get tested
- Positive test with risk factors: Antiviral consideration (within 5 days)
- Symptoms not improving after 5-7 days
- Worsening despite treatment

ISOLATION:
- If positive: Isolate 5 days minimum (CDC recommendation)
- Wear mask around others: 10 days if still symptomatic
- Household: Isolate from others if possible
- Return to work/activities: When fever-free 24 hours without medication AND improving

MONITORING:
- Symptom tracking: Daily log of temperature, oxygen, symptoms
- Pulse oximetry: If available (normal >94% on room air)
- Seek help if O2 sat drops or respiratory symptoms worsen

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Pregnancy: Increased risk of severe disease; vaccines safe
- Immunocompromised: Extended treatment with remdesivir, post-exposure prophylaxis with monoclonal antibodies
- Elderly: Much higher risk of severe disease (100x mortality vs young)
- Chronic comorbidities: Diabetes, HTN, COPD, heart disease increase severity
- Post-COVID syndrome: Emerging entity, debilitating (no specific treatment yet, supportive care)

PROGNOSIS:
- Asymptomatic: No illness, no sequelae
- Mild-moderate: Recovery in 1-2 weeks, most complete recovery
- Severe: Hospitalization 1-2 weeks, some lasting effects (mild hypoxemia, dyspnea)
- Critical: High mortality (5-20% with mechanical ventilation), survivors often have PTSD, physical limitations

PREVENTION:
- Vaccination: Most effective (>.95% vs severe)
- Booster vaccination: Maintain immunity (wanes over time)
- Testing: If exposed or symptomatic
- Isolation: If positive (prevent transmission)
