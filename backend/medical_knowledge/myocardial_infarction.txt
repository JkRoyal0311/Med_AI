DISEASE: Myocardial Infarction / Heart Attack
CATEGORY: Cardiovascular / Cardiac Emergency
ICD-10: I21
SEVERITY: Critical/Life-threatening; medical emergency

DESCRIPTION:
Myocardial infarction (MI) is death of heart muscle due to blocked coronary artery (lack of blood
flow/oxygen). Leading cause of death in men and women. Occurs when atherosclerotic plaque ruptures,
causing thrombosis. Time-critical emergency: "Time is muscle" — every minute matters. Rapid
reperfusion (thrombolysis or angioplasty) saves life and preserves heart function. Symptoms often
described as crushing chest pain, but presentation variable.

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY:
- Atherosclerotic plaque: Cholesterol accumulation in coronary artery
- Plaque rupture: Exposed lipid core initiates clotting cascade
- Thrombosis: Blood clot completely blocks artery
- Myocardial necrosis: Heart muscle dies from lack of oxygen
- Timing: Irreversible damage begins ~20-30 minutes, extensive by 3-4 hours
- Collateral vessels: Pre-existing good collaterals may limit damage

TYPES:

STEMI (ST-Elevation MI):
- Complete coronary occlusion: Full-thickness myocardial infarction
- ST elevation on EKG: Indicates full-thickness injury
- High risk of complications (cardiogenic shock, arrhythmia)
- Typically anterior MI (LAD) more serious than inferior

NSTEMI (Non-ST Elevation MI):
- Partial occlusion or spontaneous thrombolysis
- No ST elevation but elevated troponin
- Usually subendocardial infarction (partial thickness)
- Better prognosis than STEMI but still serious

RISK FACTORS:

MODIFIABLE:
- Smoking: Oxygen reduction, plaque irritability
- High cholesterol: Atherosclerotic plaque formation
- Hypertension: Vessel wall stress, plaque growth
- Diabetes: Accelerates atherosclerosis, increases clot risk
- Obesity: Inflammation, metabolic dysfunction
- Physical inactivity: Cardiovascular deconditioning
- High stress: Emotional stress can trigger MI
- Excessive alcohol: Cardiomyopathy, arrhythmias
- Cocaine/methamphetamine: Vasospasm, tachycardia
- Oral contraceptives: Thrombotic risk (minimal with modern formulations)
- Hormone replacement therapy: Slight thrombotic risk

NON-MODIFIABLE:
- Age: Increases with age (men >45, women >55)
- Male gender: Higher risk in men
- Family history: Premature CAD in relatives
- Race/ethnicity: Variations by ethnicity
- Prior MI: Recurrent risk high (~10% per year without prevention)

SYMPTOMS:

CLASSIC PRESENTATION (Chest pain):
- Chest pain/pressure: Central, crushing ("like elephant on chest")
- Quality: Often described as tightness, squeezing, pressure
- Location: Substernal (behind breastbone), can radiate
- Radiation: To left arm, jaw, neck, back, epigastrium
- Duration: >20 minutes (unlike angina which resolves with rest)
- Associated: Diaphoresis (sweating), nausea, dyspnea

ASSOCIATED SYMPTOMS:
- Shortness of breath: Most common symptom in women, elderly
- Nausea/vomiting: Especially inferior MI
- Lightheadedness/syncope: From hypotension, arrhythmia
- Palpitations: From arrhythmia
- Extreme fatigue: Profound weakness
- Anxiety: Sense of impending doom

ATYPICAL PRESENTATIONS (Often delayed diagnosis):
- Women: Often atypical (back pain, nausea, fatigue more common than chest pain)
- Elderly: Dyspnea, weakness rather than chest pain
- Diabetics: Often asymptomatic or minimal pain ("silent MI" — autonomic neuropathy)
- Asian populations: May present later (cultural attitudes toward seeking care)

DIAGNOSIS:

ELECTROCARDIOGRAM (EKG) — URGENT:
- ST elevation: In anatomic distribution (STEMI)
- ST depression: Posterior wall or reciprocal changes
- T wave changes: Inversion or flattening
- Arrhythmias: Frequent in acute phase

CARDIAC BIOMARKERS:
- Troponin I/T: Most sensitive (elevated within 3-4 hours, peak 2-3 days)
  * Normal troponin in first hour doesn't exclude MI (serial testing needed)
  * Extremely specific for myocardial necrosis
- CKMB: Older marker, less specific, faster rise/fall (2-3 hours)
- Myoglobin: Non-specific, earliest rise (1-2 hours)
- Serial troponins: If first negative, repeat in 3 hours and 6-9 hours

IMAGING:
- Echocardiography: Within 10 minutes
  * Shows wall motion abnormality (hypokinesis/akinesis in occluded vessel territory)
  * Assesses LV function, complications (VSD, papillary muscle rupture, free wall rupture)
  * Guides management
- Coronary angiography: During cardiac catheterization
  * Gold standard for identifying culprit lesion
  * Part of emergent management (PCI)
- Chest X-ray: Assess for pulmonary edema, cardiac silhouette size

OTHER TESTS:
- CBC: Check hemoglobin (anemia can precipitate MI, complicate treatment)
- Metabolic panel: Kidney function, electrolytes
- Lipid panel: Later (after acute phase)
- BNP/NT-proBNP: Prognostication

MEDICATIONS USED:

EMERGENT (Pre-hospital/ER):

1. ASPIRIN:
   - Dosage: 300-325 mg chewed (or 160-325 mg IV)
   - IMMEDIATELY: Antiplatelet, reduces mortality ~25%
   - Unless contraindication
   - Brand names: Generic

2. ANTICOAGULATION (LMWH or UFH):
   - Enoxaparin: 0.3-0.5 mg/kg IV, then 1 mg/kg SC every 12 hours
   - OR Unfractionated heparin (UFH): 60-70 U/kg IV bolus, then infusion
   - Prevents thrombus expansion

3. P2Y12 INHIBITORS:
   - Clopidogrel (Plavix): 300-600 mg loading dose
   - Prasugrel: 60 mg loading (30 mg if <60 kg)
   - Ticagrelor: 180 mg loading dose
   - Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT): With aspirin
   - Reduces stent thrombosis, recurrence

REPERFUSION (URGENT):

4. REPERFUSION STRATEGY (Time-sensitive):

   A. PRIMARY PCI (Preferred if <12 hours, door-to-balloon <90 min):
      - Percutaneous coronary intervention
      - Catheter-based angioplasty ± stent placement
      - Reopens occluded artery
      - Success rate 95% in expert centers
      - Requires cardiac catheterization lab
      
   B. FIBRINOLYTIC THERAPY (Thrombolysis — if PCI unavailable):
      - Alteplase (tPA): 15 mg IV bolus, then infusion
      - Reteplase: 10 units IV twice
      - Tenecteplase: Weight-based dosing single bolus
      - Dissolves thrombus: Within 12 hours of onset (ideal <3 hours)
      - Success: 50-60% achieve TIMI-3 flow
      - Risk: Bleeding (0.7-0.8% intracranial hemorrhage)
      - Contraindications: Active bleed, recent surgery, coagulopathy, uncontrolled HTN

ACUTE MANAGEMENT:

5. ANTIANGINALS:
   - Nitroglycerin: 0.3-0.6 mg sublingual, can repeat every 5 minutes
     * Dilates coronary/peripheral vessels
     * Reduces preload
     * Usually given IV in acute MI
   - Morphine: 2-4 mg IV for pain, anxiety, venodilation
     * Pain relief critical (psychological + physiologic)

6. BETA-BLOCKERS (Early, if no contraindication):
   - Metoprolol: IV 25-50 mg, then oral
   - Atenolol: IV 5 mg, repeat x 2
   - Reduces oxygen demand, arrhythmia risk
   - CAUTION: Contraindicated in cardiogenic shock, bradycardia, heart block

7. ACE INHIBITORS:
   - Lisinopril: 5 mg initially, then daily dosing
   - Ramipril: Similar dosing
   - Improves LV remodeling, reduces mortality
   - Start within 24 hours

8. ALDOSTERONE ANTAGONISTS:
   - Spironolactone: 25 mg daily (if LVEF <35%)
   - Reduces mortality in post-MI heart failure

INTENSIVE CARE:

9. ICU MONITORING:
   - Continuous EKG monitoring: Detect arrhythmias
   - Oxygen: To maintain O2 sat >94%
   - IV fluids: Carefully (monitor for pulmonary edema)
   - Catheterization: For hemodynamic monitoring if needed

10. ARRHYTHMIA MANAGEMENT:
    - Amiodarone: For VF/VT
    - Transcutaneous pacing: If bradycardia with hypotension

SECONDARY PREVENTION (Long-term post-MI):

11. DUAL ANTIPLATELET:
    - Aspirin 81 mg + Clopidogrel/Prasugrel/Ticagrelor
    - Duration: 12 months standard

12. HIGH-INTENSITY STATIN:
    - Atorvastatin 80 mg daily
    - Rosuvastatin 40-80 mg
    - Reduces LDL goal <70 mg/dL (or <55 mg/dL for very high risk)

13. BETA-BLOCKER (Long-term):
    - Carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, bisoprolol
    - Indefinite unless contraindication
    - Reduces mortality

14. ACE-I/ARB (Indefinite):
    - Lisinopril, ramipril, captopril
    - Especially if LVEF <40%
    - Cardioprotective

15. CARDIAC REHABILITATION:
    - Exercise training: 2-3 months program
    - Improves exercise capacity, reduces depression
    - Risk factor modification
    - Psychological support

FOODS TO EAT (Cardiac recovery, secondary prevention):
- Vegetables: Leafy greens, broccoli, carrots
- Fruits: Berries, apples, oranges (vitamin C)
- Fatty fish: Salmon, sardines (omega-3, reduce inflammation)
- Whole grains: Brown rice, oats, whole wheat
- Legumes / Dal: Protein, fiber, low glycemic
- Nuts: Almonds, walnuts (omega-3, but portion control)
- Olive oil: Unsaturated fat, anti-inflammatory
- Low-fat dairy: If tolerated
- Turmeric / Haldi: Anti-inflammatory
- Ginger / Adrak
- Garlic / Lahsun

FOODS TO AVOID/STRICTLY LIMIT:
- Red meat: Excess (high saturated fat)
- Trans fats: Processed, fried foods
- High-sodium foods: Salt restriction (<2 g/day)
- High-cholesterol: Organ meats, egg yolks
- Excess alcohol: Cardiomyopathy risk
- Simple sugars: Refined carbs, sweets
- Full-fat dairy: High saturated fat

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:

EXERCISE (Cardiac rehabilitation):
- Supervised program: 2-3 months
- Gradual increase: Walking → cycling → running
- Goal: 150 min/week moderate activity
- Strength training: 2-3 times weekly
- Exercise increases post-MI (protects, improves quality of life)

STRESS MANAGEMENT:
- Major triggers: Stress, anger, emotional shock can precipitate MI
- Techniques: Meditation, yoga, counseling
- Depression screening: Common post-MI, treat aggressively

SMOKING CESSATION:
- Critical: Smoking increases cardiac events 2-4x
- Never too late to quit

WEIGHT MANAGEMENT:
- Target BMI <25
- Weight loss if overweight

SLEEP:
- 7-9 hours nightly
- Sleep apnea screening: Treat if present

SEXUAL ACTIVITY:
- Usually can resume 4-6 weeks post-MI
- Discuss timing with cardiologist

WHEN TO SEEK EMERGENCY IMMEDIATELY:

[CALL EMERGENCY (100/111/999) IMMEDIATELY]:
- Chest pain or pressure
- Shortness of breath
- Pain in jaw, neck, arm, back
- Nausea with dyspnea
- Diaphoresis (sweating)
- Loss of consciousness

[DO NOT DELAY — EVERY MINUTE COUNTS]

DO NOT:
- Wait to see if it goes away
- Drive yourself (unless absolutely necessary)
- Minimize symptoms
- Wait for other people

COMPLICATIONS:

EARLY (<48 hours):
- Cardiogenic shock: Hypotension from severe LV damage
- Acute pulmonary edema: From LV dysfunction
- Ventricular arrhythmias: VF, VT (can be fatal)
- Bradycardia/heart block: From sinus node/conduction damage

LATE (Days-weeks):
- Ventricular septal defect (VSD): Septal rupture
- Papillary muscle rupture: Acute severe MR
- Free wall rupture: Catastrophic, rapid death
- Dressler syndrome: Post-MI pericarditis (weeks-months)
- LV thrombus: From dyskinetic/akinetic region (stroke risk)

CHRONICALLY:
- Heart failure: LV remodeling, systolic dysfunction
- Recurrent MI: Without secondary prevention

PROGNOSIS:
- STEMI: 2-6% in-hospital mortality (varies with care, comorbidities)
- NSTEMI: 3-5% in-hospital mortality
- With rapid reperfusion: Much better outcomes
- Long-term: 5-year mortality ~20-30% (decreases with prevention)
- Quality of life: Most return to activities, some permanent limitation (depends on damage extent)

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Women: Often present later, atypical symptoms delay diagnosis
- Diabetics: Higher mortality, more complications
- Elderly: Higher mortality, more complications, more drug interactions
- Cocaine users: Increased recurrence risk, special management

[MYOCARDIAL INFARCTION IS ALWAYS A MEDICAL EMERGENCY — RAPID TREATMENT IS LIFE-SAVING]
