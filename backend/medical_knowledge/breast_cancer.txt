DISEASE: Breast Cancer
CATEGORY: Oncology / Endocrine-Related
ICD-10: C50
SEVERITY: Serious; varies by stage and type

DESCRIPTION:
Breast cancer is malignant tumor of breast tissue. Most common cancer in women (affects men too, <1%).
Subtypes differ genetically (hormone receptor status, HER2). Prognosis depends on stage at diagnosis.
Modern treatments highly effective, especially if caught early. Requires multidisciplinary approach:
surgery, radiation, chemotherapy, hormonal/targeted therapy.

RISK FACTORS:

FEMALE HORMONES:
- Estrogen exposure: Duration of menstruating years
- Early menarche: Before age 12
- Late menopause: After age 55
- No/late childbearing: Nulliparity, first birth >30
- Short/no breastfeeding: Protective factor not utilized
- Hormone replacement therapy (HRT): Increases risk
- Oral contraceptives: Slightly increased (resolves after discontinuation)

GENETIC:
- Family history: First-degree relative with breast cancer
- BRCA1/BRCA2 mutations: Lifetime risk 45-87%
- Other genetic predisposition genes: TP53, PTEN

PERSONAL HISTORY:
- Previous breast cancer: Risk of second primary
- Benign breast disease: Certain types (atypical hyperplasia)
- Chest radiation: Before age 30 (significantly increases risk)

LIFESTYLE/ENVIRONMENTAL:
- High BMI (>25): Especially postmenopause (estrogen from adipose tissue)
- Alcohol: 1+ drink daily increases risk
- Smoking: Modest increase
- Physical inactivity: Sedentary lifestyle
- Red/processed meat: Controversial, possible increased risk
- Obesity: Through estrogen elevation

AGE:
- Risk increases with age: 1 in 8 women develop breast cancer lifetime
- Peak incidence: Ages 60-70

BREAST DENSITY:
- High density ("radiographically dense breasts"): 4-6x higher risk
- Genetic or hormonal basis (not modifiable)

SYMPTOMS:

EARLY (Often asymptomatic, found on screening):
- Screening mammogram abnormality: Calcifications, mass

PALPABLE SYMPTOMS:
- Breast lump: Hard, painless (in breast self-exam)
- Nipple discharge: Unilateral, spontaneous (bloody/blood-tinged most concerning)
- Nipple inversion: New or changes
- Skin dimpling or puckering: "Orange peel" appearance
- Breast pain: Unilateral (persistent, not cyclical)
- Axillary lymphadenopathy: Swollen armpit nodes

SIGNS OF ADVANCED DISEASE:
- Constitutional: Weight loss, fatigue
- Metastatic symptoms: Bone pain, shortness of breath, cognitive changes

INFLAMMATORY BREAST CANCER (Red flag):
- Breast edema, erythema (red, swollen)
- Rapid onset
- Aggressive type

DIAGNOSIS:

SCREENING:
- Mammography: Standard screening (ages 40-50 depending on guidelines)
- Ultrasound: For dense breasts, supplemental
- MRI: High-risk individuals (BRCA+, family history)

DIAGNOSTIC:
- Clinical breast exam: Doctor evaluation
- Mammography: Diagnostic (not screening)
- Ultrasound: Characterize lesion
- Core needle biopsy: Tissue diagnosis (preferred)
- Surgical excision: If biopsy inconclusive
- Pathology: Confirms malignancy, subtype

STAGING:
- TNM (Tumor, Node, Metastasis)
  * Stage 0: Carcinoma in situ (DCIS, LCIS)
  * Stage 1: Small tumor, no nodes
  * Stage 2: Tumor or nodal involvement
  * Stage 3: Advanced local/regional disease
  * Stage 4: Distant metastases (incurable but treatable)

TUMOR SUBTYPING:
- Hormone receptor status: ER+, PR+, or ER-/PR- (triple-negative if also HER2-)
- HER2 status: HER2+ or HER2-
- Grade: 1-3 (histological aggressiveness)

MEDICATIONS USED:

CHEMOTHERAPY (Varies by type, stage, subtype):

1. DOXORUBICIN (Anthracycline)
   - Part of AC (Adriamycin/cyclophosphamide) regimen
   - IV infusion
   - RED HAND syndrome: Red urine (harmless)
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Cardiomyopathy (dose-dependent), alopecia, nausea
   - Often combined with taxane (paclitaxel/DocetaxelDocuments)

2. PACLITAXEL or DOCETAXEL (Taxanes)
   - IV infusion or oral
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Peripheral neuropathy (sometimes permanent), alopecia, nausea
   - Commonly used in early stage and metastatic

3. CYCLOPHOSPHAMIDE (Alkylating agent)
   - Part of AC or "Red devil" regimens
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Hemorrhagic cystitis (bladder bleeding), alopecia, nausea
   - Mesna given to protect bladder

HORMONAL THERAPY (For ER+ breast cancer):

4. TAMOXIFEN
   - Dosage: 20 mg daily for 5-10 years
   - Blocks estrogen receptor in breast tissue
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Hot flashes, vaginal dryness, blood clots (0.5%)
   - Can increase uterine cancer (rare but real)
   - Good for premenopausal women

5. AROMATASE INHIBITORS (AI) — For postmenopausal:
   - Letrozole: 2.5 mg daily
   - Anastrozole: 1 mg daily
   - Exemestane: 25 mg daily
   - Block estrogen synthesis in peripheral tissues
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Hot flashes, joint pain, bone loss (osteoporosis risk)
   - Duration: 5-10 years
   - Better than tamoxifen for postmenopausal

TARGETED THERAPY (For HER2+ cancer):

6. TRASTUZUMAB (Herceptin)
   - Monoclonal antibody against HER2
   - IV infusion over 1 year
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Cardiomyopathy (monitor ejection fraction), infusion reactions
   - Transforms prognosis of HER2+ disease
   - Often combined with chemotherapy

7. PERTUZUMAB (Perjeta)
   - Different HER2 epitope, combined with Trastuzumab
   - For metastatic HER2+ breast cancer

CDK4/6 INHIBITORS (Newer, for HR+ advanced):

8. PALBOCICLIB
   - Dosage: 125 mg daily (21 days on, 7 days off)
   - Combined with aromatase inhibitor or fulvestrant
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Neutropenia, diarrhea, elevated liver enzymes
   - Improves progression-free survival significantly

RADIATION THERAPY:
- Post-surgical: Reduces recurrence risk
- Palliative: For bone/brain metastases, pain control
- Side effects: Skin changes, fatigue, cardiac (if left-sided chest irradiation)

SURGERY:
- Breast-conserving surgery (lumpectomy): Remove tumor + margins
  * Requires radiation post-op
  * Good cosmetic outcome
- Mastectomy: Remove entire breast (with/without reconstruction)
- Sentinel lymph node biopsy: Staging
- Axillary dissection: If positive nodes

SUPPORTIVE CARE:
9. ANTI-NAUSEA:
   - Ondansetron: 8 mg three times daily during chemotherapy

10. BONE HEALTH:
    - Zoledronic acid: IV infusion every 3-4 months (prevent bone loss, reduce metastases)
    - Calcium and vitamin D supplementation

FOODS TO EAT (General wellness):
- Vegetables: Leafy greens, cruciferous (broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage)
- Fruits: Berries (antioxidants), citrus
- Whole grains: Brown rice, whole wheat
- Legumes / Dal: Protein, fiber
- Fatty fish: Salmon (omega-3 fatty acids)
- Lean proteins: Chicken, turkey
- Nuts and seeds: Moderation (high calorie)
- Turmeric / Haldi: Curcumin has anti-inflammatory properties
- Green tea: Antioxidants
- Extra virgin olive oil: Heart-healthy
- Herbs: Garlic, ginger for general health

FOODS TO AVOID/LIMIT:
- Red meat: Limit (not explicitly contraindicated but epidemiologically higher with high consumption)
- Processed foods: Preservatives, nitrates
- High-fat dairy: Limit
- Alcohol: Avoid or minimize (increases estrogen)
- High-calorie foods: GainStat through weight gain
- Soy: Controversial (generally safe, but some caution in ER+ disease — discuss with oncology)
- Excess sugar: Avoid refined sugars
- Smoking: Absolutely avoid

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:
- Exercise: 150 min/week aerobic activity (reduces recurrence risk)
- Weight management: Obesity increases recurrence risk
- Stress reduction: Yoga, meditation (effect on recurrence debated but helpful for wellness)
- Sleep: 7-8 hours nightly
- Smoking cessation: Better outcomes, fewer complications
- Limit alcohol: <1 drink daily if any
- Regular follow-up: Surveillance for recurrence

MONITORING/FOLLOW-UP:
- Clinical exams: Every 3-6 months in first 2-3 years
- Imaging: Annual mammography (contralateral breast)
- Tumor markers: Not routinely used for screening (high false-positive)
- Cognitive/emotional: Depression screening, rehabilitation programs

COMPLEMENTARY THERAPIES (Supportive, not curative):
- Acupuncture: For hot flashes, pain
- Yoga: Stress reduction, physical wellness
- Meditation: Anxiety, stress
- Nutritional support: Dietitian-guided
- Psychosocial support: Cancer support groups, therapy

WHEN TO SEEK DOCTOR:
- Breast lump: New or persistent
- Nipple discharge: Unilateral, spontaneous
- Skin changes: Dimpling, redness, puckering
- Persistent breast or axillary pain
- Screening recommendations: Age-appropriate
- Family history: Consider BRCA testing

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Premenopausal: Pregnancy options before treatment (egg/sperm banking)
- ER+ young women: Less aggressive often, tamoxifen preferred
- BRCA+ mutation: Prophylactic mastectomy/oophorectomy consideration
- Pregnancy after cancer: Generally safe if ≥5 years disease-free
- Fertility preservation: Options exist before chemotherapy
- Menopause symptoms: Managing hot flashes during tamoxifen/AI therapy challenging
- Secondary malignancies: Small risk years later (surveillance important)

PROGNOSIS:
- Stage 1: 5-year survival ~99%
- Stage 2: 5-year survival ~93%
- Stage 3: 5-year survival ~72%
- Stage 4 (metastatic): 5-year survival ~28% (highly variable, some live 10+ years)
- HER2+ tumors: Dramatically improved with trastuzumab (60-80% response)
- Triple-negative: More aggressive, harder to treat
