DISEASE: Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)
CATEGORY: Endocrine / Gynecological
ICD-10: E28.2
SEVERITY: Chronic — manageable, affects fertility and metabolic health

DESCRIPTION:
PCOS is a common hormonal disorder affecting women of reproductive age. Characterized by
irregular menstruation, elevated androgen (male hormone) levels, and ovarian cysts (multiple small cysts).
Affects 6-20% of women globally, higher prevalence in India. Causes irregular periods, infertility,
metabolic syndrome, increased diabetes and heart disease risk.

CAUSES:
- Exact cause unknown
- Suspected: Insulin resistance (70-80% of PCOS patients)
- Genetic predisposition
- Chronic inflammation
- Hormonal imbalance in LH and FSH

SYMPTOMS:
REPRODUCTIVE:
- Irregular periods: Absence of periods for months (amenorrhea), heavy bleeding, unpredictable cycles
- Infertility: Difficulty conceiving due to irregular ovulation
- Increased miscarriage risk

METABOLIC:
- Weight gain: Especially abdominal obesity, despite modest caloric intake
- Difficulty losing weight: Insulin resistance makes weight loss hard
- Dark skin patches / Acanthosis nigricans: Usually neck, underarms
- Skin tags: Small growths, especially neck and armpits

HORMONAL:
- Excessive facial hair / Hirsutism: Beard, sideburns, chin hair
- Hair loss on scalp: Male-pattern baldness
- Acne: On face, chest, back (elevated androgens)
- Oily skin
- Deepening voice (rare)

PSYCHOLOGICAL:
- Depression and anxiety: Common mood disorders
- ADHD symptoms: Concentration difficulties

FERTILITY ISSUES:
- Irregular ovulation: Makes conception difficult
- Elevated FSH/LH ratio: Disrupts egg development
- Pregnancy complications: Higher miscarriage and gestational diabetes risk

DIAGNOSIS:
Rotterdam Criteria (2 of 3 required):
1. Irregular or absent periods (anovulation)
2. Clinical/biochemical signs of hyperandrogenism (high male hormones)
3. Polycystic ovaries on ultrasound (12+ follicles per ovary, or >10 mL volume)

Tests:
- Testosterone (free and total)
- DHEA-S (Dehydroepiandrosterone sulfate)
- LH/FSH ratio: Usually elevated in PCOS
- Fasting glucose and insulin: Assess insulin resistance
- Lipid panel: Check triglycerides, cholesterol
- TSH: Rule out thyroid disease (mimics PCOS)
- Pelvic ultrasound: Assess ovarian morphology
- HOMA-IR score: Quantifies insulin resistance

MEDICATIONS USED:

FOR MENSTRUAL REGULATION & ANDROGEN REDUCTION:
1. ORAL CONTRACEPTIVES (Birth control pills)
   - Regulate menstrual cycle, reduce androgens
   - Combined estrogen + progestin
   - Dosage: Standard 21-day pack, take daily for 21 days, 7-day break
   - Brand names: Yasmin, Diane-35, Loette
   - EFFECTIVENESS: Reduces androgen, regulates cycles, improves acne and hair loss
   - Effects: Takes 2-3 months for noticeable improvement
   - MUST MONITOR: BP, clotting risk (PCOS increases thrombotic risk)

2. PROGESTIN-ONLY PILLS or MEDROXYPROGESTERONE
   - Alternative if estrogen contraindicated
   - Dosage: 10 mg medroxyprogesterone daily for 10-12 days monthly
   - Triggers withdrawal bleeding (periods)

FOR INSULIN RESISTANCE (Most important if overweight):
3. METFORMIN
   - Improves insulin sensitivity
   - Dosage: 500 mg twice to three times daily with meals
   - Takes 3-6 months to see full effect
   - SIDE EFFECTS: GI upset (nausea, diarrhea), given with meals
   - Brand names: Glucophage, Glycomet
   - EFFECTIVENESS: 30-40% weight loss improvement, improves ovulation
   - Can use even if not diabetic (off-label in PCOS)

4. INOSITOL (Myo-inositol and D-chiro-inositol)
   - Natural compound that improves insulin signaling
   - Dosage: 2-4 grams daily
   - More tolerable than metformin
   - May improve ovulation and fertility
   - Takes 2-3 months for effect
   - Often combined with metformin for synergistic effect

FOR ANDROGEN REDUCTION (Topical, cosmetic):
5. SPIRONOLACTONE (Anti-androgen)
   - Blocks male hormone effects
   - Dosage: 50-200 mg daily (divided doses)
   - Reduces hirsutism, acne, hair loss
   - MONITOR: Potassium levels (can increase)
   - Takes 6-12 months for visible improvement in hair
   - Usually combined with oral contraceptive

FOR FERTILITY:
6. CLOMIPHENE CITRATE
   - Induces ovulation
   - Dosage: 50-150 mg daily for 5 days (days 3-7 of cycle)
   - 70-80% will ovulate, 40-50% will conceive per cycle
   - Ovulation usually occurs 5-10 days after last dose
   - Multiple pregnancy risk: 10%
   - Monitor with ultrasound to assess follicle growth

7. LETROZOLE (Aromatase Inhibitor)
   - Newer alternative to clomiphene
   - Lower multiple pregnancy risk than clomiphene
   - May be more effective

FOR ACNE:
8. TRETINOIN (Vitamin A derivative)
   - Topical for acne prescription
   - Dosage: Apply at night, 2-3 times weekly initially
   - OR use oral contraceptives (see above)

MEDICATIONS THAT WORSEN PCOS:
- Avoid corticosteroids with long-term use (increase insulin resistance)
- Some antidepressants cause weight gain (discuss alternatives)

FOODS TO EAT (Anti-inflammatory, insulin-friendly):
- Low glycemic index foods: Steel-cut oats, brown rice, whole wheat
- Legumes / Dal: Lentils, chickpeas (high fiber, low GI)
- Leafy greens / Palak, methi: Nutrient-dense, low calorie
- Cruciferous vegetables: Broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage (reduce androgens)
- Berries: Blueberries, raspberries (antioxidants, insulin-friendly)
- Fish: Salmon, mackerel (omega-3 reduces inflammation)
- Nuts and seeds: Almonds, flaxseed, sunflower seeds (magnesium, fiber)
- Olive oil: Anti-inflammatory
- Turmeric / Haldi: Curcumin reduces inflammation, may improve insulin sensitivity
- Ginger / Adrak: Anti-inflammatory
- Cinnamon / Dalchini: May improve insulin sensitivity
- Lean poultry / Murgh: Protein without excess saturated fat
- Eggs: Complete protein
- Greek yogurt: Protein, probiotics (if tolerated)

FOODS TO STRICTLY AVOID:
- Refined carbohydrates: White bread, white rice, pasta (spike insulin, trigger inflammation)
- Sugar and sweets: Soda, candy, desserts (direct insulin spike)
- Processed foods: High sugar, refined carbs, additives (inflammatory)
- Trans fats: Packaged snacks, fried foods
- Saturated fats: Excess butter, ghee (inflammatory)
- Alcohol: Increases insulin resistance

SUPPLEMENTS THAT MAY HELP:
- Myo-inositol: 2-4 grams daily (improves ovulation, insulin sensitivity)
- Vitamin D: 1000-4000 IU daily (many PCOS patients deficient)
- Chromium: 200 mcg daily (may improve insulin sensitivity)
- Omega-3: 2-3 grams daily (anti-inflammatory)
- Magnesium: 300-400 mg daily (insulin sensitivity, muscle cramps)
- Zinc: 15-30 mg daily (reproductive hormone balance)
- NAC (N-acetylcysteine): 1800-3000 mg daily (improves fertility in some)

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS (CRITICAL):
- Weight loss: 5-10% reduction improves insulin sensitivity, restores ovulation
- Exercise: 150 min/week moderate aerobic + strength training twice weekly
  * Even without weight loss, exercise improves insulin sensitivity
  * Strength training: Build muscle, improve metabolism
  * Consistency more important than intensity
- Stress management: Yoga, meditation (stress increases androgen)
- Sleep: 7-9 hours, consistent schedule (sleep deprivation worsens insulin resistance)
- Reduce inflammation: Anti-inflammatory diet (Mediterranean or Paleo often recommended)
- Track cycle: Understanding menstrual pattern helps identify ovulation for conception

FERTILITY MANAGEMENT:
- If regular periods restrained: Likely ovulating, attempt timed intercourse
- If irregular or absent: Use ovulation tests (LH surge) or ultrasound monitoring
- If clomiphene: Use ovulation predictor kits or ultrasound to time intercourse
- Consider IVF if > 1-2 years infertility despite treatment
- Egg quality often better if weight loss and metabolic improvement achieved first

METABOLIC COMPLICATIONS TO MONITOR:
- Type 2 diabetes: 30-40% of PCOS patients develop diabetes (vs 8% general population)
- Metabolic syndrome: 33-50% prevalence in PCOS
- Cardiovascular disease: 2-3x higher risk than non-PCOS women
- Fatty liver disease: 23-26% with NAFLD
- Annual screening: Fasting glucose, triglycerides, metabolic panel

SCREEN FOR:
- Sleep apnea: PCOS increases risk (ask about snoring, daytime sleepiness)
- Depression/anxiety: Common, affects quality of life

MONITORING DURING TREATMENT:
- Menstrual cycles: Track to assess response
- Metabolic markers: Annual glucose, lipids
- Androgen levels: Check every 6-12 months (if on treatment)
- Ultrasound: Not needed routinely once diagnosis confirmed
- BMI and waist circumference: Track weight loss progress

WHEN TO SEE DOCTOR:
- Severe acne not responding to oral contraceptive
- Severe hirsutism affecting quality of life (may increase spironolactone)
- Not ovulating after 3-6 months of lifestyle changes
- Infertility > 1 year
- Weight loss plateau despite adherence
- Depression or anxiety symptoms
- Suspected pregnancy complications

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Infertility: Often requires specialist (reproductive endocrinologist)
- Pregnancy: Continue lifestyle changes; some medications stopped (contraceptives), others appropriate
- Miscarriage risk elevated: May need progesterone support in first trimester
- Gestational diabetes risk: Increased; gestational diabetes screening essential
- Breastfeeding: Some medications safe (metformin, inositol), others not (contraceptives if solely breastfeeding)
- Long-term: PCOS doesn't disappear but is manageable with lifestyle + medication
- Mental health: PCOS affects body image, fertility concerns, depression; counseling often helpful
