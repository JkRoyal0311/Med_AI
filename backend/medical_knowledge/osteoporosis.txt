DISEASE: Osteoporosis (Bone Loss)
CATEGORY: Metabolic / Rheumatological
ICD-10: M81
SEVERITY: Mild-Moderate; serious if fractures occur; can be progressive

DESCRIPTION:
Osteoporosis is decreased bone mineral density (BMD) and deterioration of bone microarchitecture
increasing fracture risk. Affects 1 in 3 women and 1 in 5 men over age 50. Often asymptomatic
until fracture occurs. Preventable and treatable with medication and lifestyle changes.

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY:

BONE REMODELING:
- Continuous process: Old bone removed, new bone formed
- Balanced in young adults
- Imbalance in osteoporosis: Bone loss > bone formation
- Results in porous, weaker bone

CAUSES:

PRIMARY OSTEOPOROSIS:
- Postmenopausal: Estrogen loss accelerates bone loss (10% loss in first 5 years)
- Age-related: Decreased bone forming capacity with aging
- Genetic: Family history, ethnicity (Caucasian, Asian higher risk)

SECONDARY OSTEOPOROSIS (From underlying conditions):
- Endocrine:
  * Hyperthyroidism: Excess thyroid hormone increases bone turnover
  * Hypogonadism: Low testosterone (men), low estrogen (women)
  * Hyperparathyroidism: PTH increases bone resorption
  * Diabetes: Type 1 (loss of insulin-like growth factor), Type 2 (paradoxical despite high BMD)
  * Cushing's syndrome: Excess cortisol inhibits bone formation
- GI:
  * Celiac disease: Malabsorption of calcium/vitamin D
  * Crohn's disease: Inflammation, malabsorption
  * Post-gastrectomy: Reduced intrinsic factor
  * Inflammatory bowel disease: Multiple mechanisms
- Rheumatological:
  * Rheumatoid arthritis: Inflammatory cytokines, disease-modifying drugs
  * Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)
  * Ankylosing spondylitis
- Medications:
  * Corticosteroids: Most common medication cause
  * Some anticonvulsants: Phenytoin
  * Thyroxine (excess): If over-replacing
  * GnRH agonists: Prostate cancer treatment
  * Some antiretrovirals: HIV treatment

RISK FACTORS:
- Female: 1 in 3 fracture risk
- "FRAX" score: Online tool for fracture risk assessment
- Age >50 years
- Family history
- Low body weight: BMI <19
- Smoking: Reduces bone formation, increases resorption
- Alcohol: Excess (>2-3 drinks daily)
- Physical inactivity: Weight-bearing exercise protective
- Calcium/vitamin D deficiency: Critical for bone health
- Recurrent falls: From balance, vision, medication issues

SYMPTOMS:

EARLY STAGES:
- Asymptomatic: No symptoms, discovered on screening

HEIGHT LOSS:
- Kyphosis: "Dowager's hump" (forward curvature of spine)
- Progressive height loss: From vertebral compression fractures
- Can lose 3-6 inches over decades

LATE STAGES (After fractures):
- Acute pain: With fracture (sudden, severe)
- Chronic pain: From vertebral compression deformities
- Functional impairment

DIAGNOSIS:

BONE MINERAL DENSITY (BMD):
- DXA scan (Dexa scan): Dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry
  * Gold standard screening
  * T-score: Compare to young adult
    - +1 to -1: Normal
    - -1 to -2.5: Osteopenia (low bone mass)
    - < -2.5: Osteoporosis
    - < -2.5 with fracture: Severe osteoporosis

FRACTURE RISK ASSESSMENT:
- FRAX: WHO tool calculates 10-year fracture probability
- Consider: Age, sex, BMI, smoking, alcohol, corticosteroid use, RA, parent fracture history

OTHER TESTS:
- Serum calcium, vitamin D, alkaline phosphatase
- 24-hour urine calcium (assess absorption)
- Thyroid function: Rule out hyper/hypothyroidism
- Celiac serology: If GI symptoms

MEDICATIONS USED:

FIRST-LINE: BISPHOSPHONATES

1. ALENDRONATE (Fosamax)
   - Dosage: 70 mg weekly OR 10 mg daily
   - Oral: MUST take on empty stomach with full glass of water
   - Stay upright 30 minutes after taking (prevent esophageal irritation)
   - Reduces hip/vertebral fracture risk by ~50%
   - SIDE EFFECTS: GI upset, jaw necrosis (osteonecrosis of jaw — rare), atypical fractures (very rare)
   - Well-tolerated, widely used

2. RISEDRONATE (Actonel)
   - Dosage: 35 mg weekly OR 5 mg daily
   - Similar to alendronate
   - May be better tolerated GI-wise
   - Same efficacy

3. IBANDRONATE (Boniva)
   - Dosage: 150 mg monthly oral OR 3 mg IV quarterly
   - Convenience of monthly dosing
   - Similar efficacy

4. ZOLEDRONIC ACID (Reclast)
   - Dosage: 5 mg IV infusion once yearly
   - Most convenient (once yearly)
   - Similar efficacy
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Acute phase reaction with first infusion (flu-like symptoms), osteonecrosis of jaw

SECOND-LINE: OTHER AGENTS

5. DENOSUMAB (Prolia)
   - Dosage: 60 mg SC injection every 6 months
   - Monoclonal antibody against RANKL
   - Mechanism different from bisphosphonates
   - Slightly superior hip fracture reduction
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Hypocalcemia (rare), osteonecrosis of jaw
   - Good alternative if bisphosphonate intolerant

6. HORMONE REPLACEMENT THERAPY (HRT)
   - Estrogen + progesterone (in women with uterus)
   - Estrogen alone (in hysterectomy women)
   - Preserves bone, BUT cardiovascular/cancer risks limit use
   - Mostly used for short-term menopausal symptom relief
   - NOT first-line for osteoporosis (due to risk-benefit)

7. SELECTIVE ESTROGEN RECEPTOR MODULATOR (SERM):
   - Raloxifene: 60 mg daily
   - Estrogen-like on bone, antagonist on breast/uterus
   - Reduces spine fracture risk but not hip fracture
   - May reduce breast cancer risk (benefits offset with other risks)

PTH ANALOGS (Anabolic agents — Build bone):

8. TERIPARATIDE (Forteo)
   - Dosage: 20 mcg SC daily
   - Stimulates bone FORMATION (not resorption inhibition)
   - Increases BMD faster than bisphosphonates
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Nausea, leg cramps, injection site reaction
   - Maximum 2 years use (then switch to bisphosphonate)
   - Expensive
   - For severe osteoporosis or bisphosphonate failure

CALCIUM and VITAMIN D (Fundamental):

9. CALCIUM SUPPLEMENTATION
   - Dosage: 1000-1200 mg/day (total, from diet + supplement)
   - Divided doses (absorption better)
   - TUMS (calcium carbonate) or separate supplement
   - Sources: Dietary preferred when possible

10. VITAMIN D (Cholecalciferol)
    - Dosage: 1000-2000 IU daily (or 1-2 mcg/day)
    - Critical for calcium absorption
    - Many deficient (especially elderly, dark-skinned in Northern climates)
    - Once-weekly: 7000 IU
    - Toxicity rare (requires >10,000 IU daily for prolonged period)

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID:
- Excessive corticosteroids: If necessary, use lowest dose shortest duration
- Anticonvulsants: If possible alternatives exist
- Thyroxine excess: Avoid over-replacement
- Lithium: Affects PTH regulation

FOODS TO EAT (Calcium/vitamin D support):
- Dairy: Milk, yogurt (dahi), cheese — if tolerated
- Dark leafy greens / Palak, methi: Calcium, magnesium
- Fortified plant-based milk: Calcium (check label)
- Almonds: Calcium, magnesium
- Sesame seeds: Calcium
- Sardines: Calcium + vitamin D (if bones consumed)
- Salmon: Vitamin D, omega-3
- Fortified cereals: Check labels for calcium + vitamin D
- Eggs: Vitamin D (yolks)
- Mushrooms: Vitamin D (if sun-exposed)
- Orange juice (fortified): Calcium, vitamin D
- Beans / Dal: Plant sources of calcium + magnesium

FOODS TO AVOID/LIMIT:
- High sodium: Increases urinary calcium loss
- High caffeine: Excess (>400 mg daily) inhibits calcium absorption
- High phosphate: From processed foods, carbonated drinks
- Alcohol: Excess (inhibits vitamin D metabolism)
- High protein: Very high excess worsens calcium loss (normal amounts ok)

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:

WEIGHT-BEARING EXERCISE:
- Walking: 30 minutes daily
- Jogging/running: High-impact, effective
- Dancing: Enjoyable weight-bearing
- Stair climbing: Practical
- Tai Chi: Gentle, balance improvement
- Resistance training: 2-3 times weekly (weights, bands)
- Frequency: Daily or 5x weekly optimal

IMPACT EXERCISE:
- Jumping: Teens/young adults (builds bone stronger)
- Running: If no contraindication
- Dancing: Fun, weight-bearing

BALANCE/FALL PREVENTION:
- Important if already osteoporotic (prevent fractures)
- Tai Chi: Balance, strength, proprioception
- Yoga: Balance, flexibility
- Strength training: Muscle support for bones
- HOME SAFETY: Remove rugs, adequate lighting, grab bars
- Vision check: Correct refractive errors, cataracts
- Medication review: Avoid fall-risk medications

SMOKING CESSATION:
- Critical: Smoking accelerates bone loss

LIMIT ALCOHOL:
- <2 drinks daily for men, <1 for women
- Excess alcohol inhibits bone formation

MONITORING:
- Baseline DXA: Age 65 for women, 70 for men
- Postmenopausal women <65: If risk factors
- Recheck DXA: Every 2 years on treatment, then every 2-3 years if stable
- Monitor fracture risk: FRAX score annually
- Serum calcium/vitamin D: Baseline and if symptoms (fatigue from vitamin D deficiency)

LIFESTYLE FACTORS FOR BONE HEALTH:
- Adequate sleep: 7-9 hours (bone remodeling occurs in sleep)
- Stress reduction: Cortisol elevation from stress increases bone loss
- Regular sunlight: Vitamin D synthesis (15-30 minutes daily)

WHEN TO SEEK DOCTOR:
- Height loss: 1-2 inches loss from young adulthood
- Screening age: 65+ women, 70+ men
- Fracture risk: Calculate FRAX score
- New medications: If any anti-bone drugs started
- Fracture: Even minor (indicates osteoporosis severity)

PREVENTION:
- Adolescence/young adulthood: Build peak bone mass
  * Adequate calcium/vitamin D
  * Weight-bearing exercise
  * Don't smoke, minimize alcohol
- Perimenopause: Monitor, consider estrogen if loss rapid (though usually trial-based)
- Postmenopause: Active prevention (as above)

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Postmenopausal women: Accelerated loss first 5-10 years
- Men >70: Under-screened (risk increases with age)
- Celiac disease: Treat celiac, improve absorption
- Kidney disease: Vitamin D metabolism impaired
- Diabetes (Type 1): Higher fracture risk despite normal/high BMD
- Prior vertebral fracture: Significantly increases refracture risk
- GERD: If on long-term PPI, may impair calcium absorption

TREATMENT DURATION:
- Bisphosphonates: Generally 5 years initial, then drug holiday discussion
- After 5 years: Reassess need, risk of atypical fractures with extended use
- Many patients: Remain on indefinitely if fracture risk remains high
