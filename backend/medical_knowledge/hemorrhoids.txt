DISEASE: Hemorrhoids / Piles
CATEGORY: Gastrointestinal / Proctological
ICD-10: K64
SEVERITY: Mild-Moderate; usually self-limiting but can cause complications

DESCRIPTION:
Hemorrhoids are swollen, inflamed veins in lower rectum/anus. Extremely common (75% population by age 45).
Can be internal (above dentate line) or external (below). Caused by increased venous pressure, straining,
constipation, prolonged sitting. Most resolve with conservative treatment. Cosmetic concern more than
serious disease but can bleed, cause pain, thrombose.

TYPES:

INTERNAL:
- Above anal verge
- Usually painless (lack sensory innervation above dentate line)
- Can bleed (bright red blood on stool, toilet paper)
- Can prolapse (protrude outside)
- Grades: 1 (bleeding only), 2 (prolapse, self-reduce), 3 (prolapse, manual reduction), 4 (permanent prolapse)

EXTERNAL:
- Below anal verge
- Painful (somatic innervation)
- Visible swelling around anus
- Can thrombose (clot inside, very painful)

CAUSES:
- Straining: Main cause from constipation, diarrhea, prolonged voiding
- Chronic constipation: Hard stools increase pressure
- Diarrhea: Repeated wiping, irritation
- Prolonged sitting: Loss of venous drainage, pressure
- Pregnancy: Increased intra-abdominal pressure
- Portal hypertension: Liver disease increasing venous pressure (severe form)
- Anal intercourse: Trauma
- Genetics: Family predisposition

SYMPTOMS:

INTERNAL:
- Bright red blood: On toilet paper, in toilet, on surface of stool
- Painless bleeding: Usually only symptom
- Prolapse: Feeling of bulge, tissue protruding
- Mucus discharge: Soiling underwear
- Itching: From mucus
- Usually no pain (unless thrombosed, then severe)

EXTERNAL:
- Pain: Especially if thrombosed (severe, sudden)
- Itching: Especially with hygiene irritation
- Burning sensation: With bowel movements
- Visible lump: Swelling around anus
- Discomfort: Sitting, wiping, bowel movement
- Bleeding: Usually minimal with isolated external

THROMBOSED HEMORRHOID (Emergency):
- Severe pain: Sudden onset, intense
- Hard lump: Palpable swollen vein with clot
- Bleeding: Usually minimal
- Swelling: Localized
- Photo: Dark purple/black appearance if bleeding into tissues

DIAGNOSIS:
- Visual inspection: Look for hemorrhoids
- Digital rectal exam: Feel for masses, evaluate
- Anoscopy: Telescope to visualize internal hemorrhoids
- Colonoscopy: If ≥50 years, family history Ca, or persistent bleeding to rule out cancer

MEDICATIONS/TREATMENTS USED:

TOPICAL AGENTS:

1. HEMORRHOIDAL CREAMS:
   - Preparation H: Contains glycerin, yeast extract
   - Dosage: Apply externally or internal (with applicator) 2-3 times daily
   - Provides temporary relief
   - NOT effective long-term but reduces inflammation
   - Brand names: Preparation H, others

2. ANESTHETIC CREAMS:
   - Lignocaine/Lidocaine: 2-5% cream
   - Numbs pain temporarily
   - For external hemorrhoid pain relief
   - Use before bowel movements

3. CORTICOSTEROID CREAMS:
   - Hydrocortisone 1%: Reduces inflammation
   - Apply 2-3 times daily
   - Reduces swelling, itching
   - Short-term use (1-2 weeks) to avoid skin atrophy

4. SUPPOSITORIES (For internal):
   - Hydrocortisone + bismuth oxide suppository
   - Insert rectally morning/evening
   - Reduces inflammation

ORAL MEDICATIONS:

5. STOOL SOFTENERS (Most important):
   - Docusate sodium: 50-100 mg twice daily
   - Prevents constipation (root cause)
   - Non-habit forming
   - Takes 3-5 days for effect
   - Brand names: Colace

6. OSMOTIC LAXATIVES:
   - Psyllium husk (Ispaghula): 1-2 teaspoons daily with water
   - Polyethylene glycol (PEG 3350): 17g daily (Golytely)
   - Increases stool bulk, softens stool
   - Prevents constipation
   - Takes 2-3 days for effect

7. BIOFLAVONOID (Diosmin):
   - Dosage: 600 mg daily
   - OTC supplement
   - Reduces bleeding in some studies
   - Limited evidence but safe

INJECTABLE/PROCEDURAL (For persistent):

8. RUBBER BAND LIGATION:
   - Outpatient procedure
   - Places small rubber band on hemorrhoid base
   - Cuts off blood supply, tissue necroses and falls off
   - 80-90% effective
   - Most common outpatient treatment
   - Minor discomfort, minimal risk

9. SCLEROTHERAPY:
   - Injects hardening agent (sodium tetradecyl sulfate)
   - Causes inflation, fibrosis
   - For bleeding internal hemorrhoids
   - Success rate 50-80%

SURGICAL:

10. HEMORRHOIDECTOMY:
    - Surgical removal of hemorrhoids
    - For severe, refractory, or thrombosed
    - Most effective (long-term success >95%)
    - Pain post-op (major drawback)
    - Rare complications (infection, fecal incontinence)

FOR THROMBOSED HEMORRHOID:
11. INCISION/DRAINAGE:
    - Emergency treatment: Incise clot, express (under local anesthesia)
    - Immediate pain relief (80%)
    - Office procedure
    - Alternative: Expectant management (resolves in 1-2 weeks but painful)

FOODS TO EAT (Fiber, stool softening):
- Whole grains: Brown rice, whole wheat, oats
- Legumes / Dal: High fiber (moong dal, masoor dal)
- Vegetables: Leafy greens, carrots, beans, peas
- Fruits: Apples, pears, kiwi, prunes (natural laxative)
- Leafy greens / Palak, methi: Fiber, nutrients
- Seeds: Pumpkin, sunflower (in moderation)
- Yogurt / Dahi: Probiotics
- Water: Most important (2-3 liters daily)

FOODS TO AVOID/LIMIT:
- Spicy foods: Irritate rectum
- Red meat: Low fiber
- Processed foods: Low fiber, dehydrating
- Alcohol: Dehydrating
- Coffee: Excess (dehydrating, can worsen constipation)
- White bread/rice: Refined, low fiber
- Cheese/dairy: Can cause constipation (if excess)
- Fried foods: Heavy, low fiber

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:

BOWEL HABITS:
- Don't strain: Most important (straining causes hemorrhoids)
- Don't spend >5 minutes on toilet: Increases pressure
- Use footstool: Elevate feet to improve angle, natural position
- Respond to urge: Don't delay bowel movements
- Relax: Take time, don't force

HYGIENE:
- Gentle wiping: Use soft toilet paper (or wet wipes)
- Warm water: Sitz bath 3-4 times daily (especially after bowel movement)
  * Temperature: Warm, not hot
  * Duration: 10-15 minutes
  * Provides pain relief, cleans area
- Pat dry: Don't rub
- Avoid harsh soaps: Use plain water or gentle cleanser

DAILY HABITS:
- Regular exercise: 150 min/week (improves bowel regularity)
- Stay hydrated: 2-3 liters water daily
- Don't delay bowel movements: Use toilet when urge present
- Avoid prolonged sitting/squatting: Take breaks
- Elevate legs: While sitting (improves anorectal blood flow)

PREVENTION:
- Fiber: 25-35 g daily (prevent constipation)
- Water: 2-3 liters daily
- Exercise: Regular activity
- Don't ignore urge: Use toilet promptly
- Adequate time: Don't rush bowel movements

WHEN TO SEEK DOCTOR:
- Bleeding: Persistent bright red bleeding
- Pain: Especially if severe or persistent
- Prolapse: Tissue hanging out
- Thrombosed: Sudden severe pain with hard lump
- Infection: Signs of infection (fever, spreading redness)
- Age >40 and first bleeding: Rule out other causes (colonoscopy)

WHEN URGENT (Seek care):
- Severe pain: From thrombosed hemorrhoid
- Significant bleeding: Soaking toilet paper, dizziness
- Fever: Signs of infection/abscess

WHEN EMERGENCY (ER):
- Massive bleeding: Blood loss causing dizziness, weakness
- Fever + severe pain: Possible abscess, infection

COMPLICATIONS (Rare):
- Anemia: From chronic bleeding
- Infection/abscess: If hemorrhoid becomes infected
- Fecal incontinence: Rare with treatment, more with surgery
- Bleeding: Can be significant with internal, especially grade 4

MONITORING:
- Symptom tracking: Resolution with conservative treatment
- Bleeding pattern: If persistent despite treatment, may need colonoscopy
- Response to treatment: 80-90% improve with conservative management

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Pregnancy: Very common, usually resolve post-delivery
- Children: Different presentation, investigate if significant bleeding
- Portal hypertension: Different cause, requires treatment of liver disease
- IBD (Crohn's): Hemorrhoids can be complicating factor

PROGNOSIS:
- Conservative management: 80-90% improve with fiber, hydration, hygiene
- Bleeding typically mild: Usually stops with treatment
- Symptoms can recur: Prevention critical (fiber, hydration, regular bowel habits)
- Surgical success: >95% for hemorrhoidectomy (but post-op pain significant)
