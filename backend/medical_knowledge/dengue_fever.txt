DISEASE: Viral Fever / Dengue Fever
CATEGORY: Infectious / Viral
ICD-10: A90 (Dengue), A91 (Hemorrhagic dengue)
SEVERITY: Mild-Severe; can be life-threatening (dengue hemorrhagic fever)

DESCRIPTION:
Dengue fever is a mosquito-borne viral disease transmitted by Aedes mosquito (daytime biter).
Endemic in tropical regions including India. Caused by one of 4 dengue virus serotypes. Can range
from asymptomatic to severe dengue hemorrhagic fever (DHF) with shock. Most common arboviral disease
affecting humans globally.

TRANSMISSION:
- Aedes aegypti mosquito: Daytime biter (unlike malaria mosquito)
- Peak biting: Early morning and late afternoon
- From infected person: Female mosquito bites, virus replicates in mosquito salivary glands
- No person-to-person transmission (except rarely transplacental)
- No animal reservoir: Humans main source

SYMPTOMS:

DENGUE FEVER (Classic):
- Fever: Biphasic pattern (high fever 103-105°F / 39.4-40.5°C)
  * Phase 1 (Days 1-3): High fever, chills, malaise
  * Defervescence (Days 4-5): Fever drops, "critical period"
  * Phase 2 (Days 6-8): Fever may return ("saddle-back fever pattern")
- Headache: Severe, frontal, retro-orbital (behind eyes)
- Eye pain: Pronounced ("break-bone fever" — severe pain)
- Myalgia: Severe muscle/joint pain (worse in back, legs)
- Arthralgia: Joint pain
- Rash: Typically appears days 3-5 after fever onset
  * Initially sparse maculopapular rash on trunk/limbs
  * May involve face ("flushed" appearance)
  * Can desquamate (peel) later
- Nausea and vomiting
- Loss of appetite
- General malaise

CRITICAL PERIOD (Days 4-7):
- As fever resolves: Risk of plasma leakage, bleeding complications
- Warning signs (need immediate attention):
  * Persistent vomiting
  * Severe abdominal pain
  * Bleeding (gums, nose, skin, vomit/stool)
  * Lethargy or restlessness
  * Liver enlargement
  * Rapid drop in platelet count + hematocrit rise

DENGUE HEMORRHAGIC FEVER (DHF) — SEVERE:
- All classic dengue symptoms PLUS:
- Hemorrhagic manifestations:
  * Petechiae: Small red/purple spots from bleeding
  * Ecchymoses: Larger bruises/bleeding
  * Bleeding from gums, nose (epistaxis), GI bleeding
  * Hematuria: Blood in urine
- Shock (dengue shock syndrome): Circulatory collapse
  * Hypotension
  * Rapid, weak pulse
  * Cool skin
  * Restlessness or lethargy
  * Metabolic acidosis
- Mortality: 5-10% untreated, <1% with supportive care

DIAGNOSIS:
- Dengue IgM antibody: Positive in acute phase (PCR more specific early on)
- Platelet count: Thrombocytopenia (usually mild)
- Hematocrit: Elevated (hemoconcentration from plasma leakage)
- Liver enzymes: Transient elevation
- Rapid diagnostic tests: Available

MEDICATIONS USED (Supportive care only — no antiviral):

NO SPECIFIC ANTIVIRAL:
- Unlike bacterial infections, dengue is viral
- No medication kills the virus
- Treatment is supportive (help body fight it)

PAIN AND FEVER MANAGEMENT:
1. PARACETAMOL
   - Dosage: 500 mg every 6 hours (max 2g/day)
   - For fever and pain relief
   - Brand names: Crocin, Dolo
   - PREFERRED over NSAIDs (see below)

AVOID THESE (IMPORTANT):
- NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Diclofenac, Naproxen): CONTRAINDICATED
  * Increase bleeding risk in dengue
  * Increase GI bleeding risk
  * Can worsen symptoms
  * STICK TO PARACETAMOL ONLY

- Aspirin: Also increases bleeding risk

SUPPORTIVE CARE:
2. FLUID REPLACEMENT (Most important)
   - Oral rehydration: If mild, able to drink
     * ORS (Oral Rehydration Solution): 1 liter per day
     * Or: Frequent small sips of water, juice, electrolyte drinks
   - IV fluids: If severe, unable to drink, hemorrhagic features
     * Normal saline or Ringer's lactate
     * Depends on degree of dehydration/shock
   - MONITOR: Urine output (should be adequate, not dark)

3. BLOOD PRODUCTS (If hemorrhage/shock):
   - Platelet transfusion: If critical bleeding or very low platelets
   - Fresh frozen plasma: For coagulation support
   - Packed RBCs: If significant hemorrhage/anemia

FOODS TO EAT (Recovery support):
- Water and oral rehydration: PRIMARY treatment
- Fruits: Papaya (believed to support platelet count — no scientific proof but culturally used)
- Citrus fruits: Orange, sweet lime (vitamin C)
- Leafy greens / Palak, methi: Vitamins, minerals
- Protein: Chicken, eggs, fish (rebuild from illness)
- Coconut water: Natural electrolytes
- Light broths: Nourishing, hydrating
- Bananas: Potassium for electrolyte balance
- Pomegranate: Iron, antioxidants
- Whole grains: Brown rice, oats
- Nuts and seeds: Magnesium
- Honey: Energy

FOODS TO AVOID:
- High sugar foods: Strain body during recovery
- Spicy foods: Initially irritating to inflamed mouth/throat
- Greasy/fried foods: Difficult digestion during illness
- Alcohol: Dehydrating, stressful on liver

HYDRATION (CRITICAL):
- Drink continuously: Even small sips
- ORS preferred: Commercial packets or homemade
- Monitor: Dark urine suggests inadequate hydration
- Avoid: Caffeine (diuretic)

WHEN TO SEEK URGENT ATTENTION:
- Severe abdominal pain
- Persistent vomiting (unable to keep fluids down)
- Bleeding: From gums, nose, skin, vomit/stool
- Lethargy or decreased consciousness
- Rapid breathing
- Very cold skin with drenching sweats
- Seizures
- Dropping platelet count (if known)
- Suspected dengue shock syndrome
[SEEK EMERGENCY CARE IMMEDIATELY FOR SEVERE SYMPTOMS]

WHEN TO SEE DOCTOR:
- Suspected dengue (fever + headache + myalgia + rash)
- Day 4-7: Critical period (highest risk for deterioration)
- Not improving after 3-5 days
- Bleeding symptoms
- Persistent vomiting
- Anyone suspected dengue should be monitored closely

COMPLICATIONS:
- Dengue hemorrhagic fever: Intrinsic to dengue, higher with secondary infection
- Dengue shock syndrome: Circulatory collapse, mortality high without treatment
- Acute liver failure: Rare but possible
- Encephalitis: CNS involvement (rare)
- Myocarditis: Heart involvement (rare)
- Pregnancy: Increased risk of fetal loss

PREVENTION:
- Mosquito avoidance: Most important
  * Insect repellent: DEET-containing (25-30%)
  * Long sleeves and pants: Especially early morning/late afternoon
  * Window screens: Air conditioning if available
  * Avoid stagnant water: Mosquito breeding sites
- Dengue vaccine (Dengvaxia): Available in some countries (approved in India for age ≥9 years)
  * Requires serological confirmation of prior dengue (not for naive individuals)
  * 3 doses given
  * Reduces symptomatic dengue by ~60%

MONITORING:
- Daily assessment: During acute phase, especially days 4-7
- CBC: Watch platelet count trends
- Hematocrit: Rising hematocrit suggests plasma leakage
- Vitals: BP, HR (signs of shock)
- Urine output: Indicator of hydration

RECOVERY:
- Prolonged fatigue: Common, can last weeks
- Usually improvements in 7-10 days
- Return to activities gradually
- Inadequate rest increases relapse risk

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Secondary infection: Same serotype (homologous) provides lifelong immunity
  * Different serotype (heterologous): Increases DHF risk (pathologic immunity)
  * 2-5 times higher DHF risk with secondary dengue infection
- Pregnancy: Vertical transmission rare; increased complications in pregnant women
- Children: Milder symptoms often; better outcome
- Elderly: More severe disease, worse outcomes
- Preexisting conditions: Diabetes, heart disease increase severity
