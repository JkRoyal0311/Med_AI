DISEASE: Prostate Enlargement (Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia — BPH)
CATEGORY: Urological 
ICD-10: N40
SEVERITY: Mild-Moderate; affects quality of life, not life-threatening

DESCRIPTION:
Benign prostatic hyperplasia is noncancerous enlarged prostate gland common in aging men. Prostate surrounds
urethra; enlargement compresses urethra, causes urinary symptoms. Extremely common (>50% men age 60,
>90% age 85). Distinct from prostate cancer (though can coexist). Treatable with medications or surgery.

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY:
- Age-related hyperplasia: Normal aging process
- Testosterone/DHT: Dihydrotestosterone drives growth
- Estrogen: Age-related changes increase estrogen effect
- Smooth muscle proliferation: Increased stromal/epithelial tissue
- Dynamic obstruction: Both mechanical (enlarged gland) + smooth muscle tone

RISK FACTORS:
- Age: Primary determinant (rare <40, common >60)
- Family history: Some genetic predisposition
- Higher androgens: Genetic sensitivity to testosterone
- Obesity: Some studies suggest association
- Metabolic syndrome: Insulin resistance possibly involved
- Cardiovascular disease: Some association

SYMPTOMS:

LOWER URINARY TRACT SYMPTOMS (LUTS):

OBSTRUCTIVE (from compression):
- Weak urinary stream: Reduced force
- Hesitancy: Difficulty initiating stream
- Intermittency: Stream stops/starts
- Straining: Need abdominal pressure to void
- Incomplete emptying: Feeling bladder not fully emptied
- Prolonged voiding: Takes longer than normal
- Post-void dribbling: Leakage after voiding

IRRITATIVE (from unstable bladder contractions):
- Frequency: >8 voids per day
- Nocturia: Waking to urinate at night (≥2x/night concerning)
  * Major quality-of-life impact (sleep disruption)
- Urgency: Strong urge to urinate
- Urgency incontinence: Unable to delay (urge incontinence)

SEVERE COMPLICATIONS (Rare):
- Urinary retention: Inability to void (painful, requires catheterization)
- Bladder stones: From stagnant urine
- Upper urinary tract dilation: From back-pressure
- Acute kidney injury: From chronic obstruction

SYMPTOM SEVERITY SCORING:
- AUA-SI (American Urological Association Symptom Index): 0-35 scale
  * <8: Mild
  * 8-19: Moderate
  * >20: Severe

DIAGNOSIS:

CLINICAL ASSESSMENT:
- History: Onset, symptom progression
- Voiding diary: Frequency, nocturia patterns
- AUA symptom score: Standardized assessment

PHYSICAL EXAMINATION:
- Digital rectal exam (DRE): Feel enlarged prostate
  * Size: Small nubbins vs large gland
  * Consistency: Should be bosselated, not nodular (cancer concern)
  * Symmetry: May be asymmetric
  * NOTE: Size doesn't correlate with symptoms

TESTING:
- Post-void residual (PVR): Ultrasound of bladder after voiding
  * Normal <50 mL, concerning >100 mL
- Uroflow study: Measures stream strength, void time
  * Low peak flow (<10 mL/s) suggests obstruction
- Urinalysis: Check for infection, hematuria
- PSA (Prostate Specific Antigen): Screen for cancer
  * Elevated in BPH and cancer
  * >4 ng/mL needs evaluation
  * Shared decision-making regarding screening (not universally recommended)
- Cystoscopy: Only if hematuria, inability to distinguish cancer risk

MEDICATIONS USED:

ALPHA-BLOCKERS (First-line agent):

1. TAMSULOSIN (Most selective for prostate):
   - Dosage: 0.4 mg daily (taken 30 minutes after meal)
   - Relaxes smooth muscle in prostate + bladder neck
   - Onset: 1-2 weeks for full effect
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Dizziness, orthostatic hypotension (especially first dose), retrograde ejaculation (~5%), headache
   - Effective: 30-50% symptom improvement
   - Brand names: Flomax

2. DOXAZOSIN:
   - Dosage: Start 1 mg, increase to 4-8 mg daily
   - Also lowers blood pressure (benefit if hypertensive)
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Dizziness, orthostatic hypotension
   - Generic available

3. TERAZOSIN:
   - Dosage: 2-5 mg daily
   - Also lowers BP (benefit if hypertensive)
   - Similar efficacy to doxazosin
   - Generic available

5-ALPHA REDUCTASE INHIBITORS (For larger glands):

4. FINASTERIDE:
   - Dosage: 5 mg daily
   - Reduces DHT (blocks conversion of testosterone)
   - Slower onset: 3-6 months for full effect
   - Reduces prostate size 20-30%
   - Better for larger glands (>40 mL)
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Decreased sexual function (~5%), decreased libido, erectile dysfunction
   - PREGNANCY: Teratogenic if pregnant woman exposed (contraceptive use essential in female partners)
   - Generic available

   Brand name: Proscar (5 mg for BPH; Propecia 1 mg for hair loss)

5. DUTASTERIDE:
   - Dosage: 0.5 mg daily
   - Blocks both DHT conversion pathways (more complete)
   - Slightly more effective than finasteride
   - Same side effect profile
   - Generic available
   - Brand names: Avodart

COMBINATION THERAPY (For moderate-severe):
- Alpha-blocker + 5-alpha reductase inhibitor: For larger glands (>40 mL)
  * Tamsulosin + finasteride (or dutasteride)
  * Better symptom control than either alone

6. BETA-3 AGONIST (Newer option):
   - Mirabegron: 25-50 mg daily
   - Relaxes bladder smooth muscle (different mechanism)
   - For urgency/frequency symptoms
   - No erectile dysfunction
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Hypertension (monitor BP)
   - Effective for irritative symptoms

7. PHOSPHODIESTERASE-5 INHIBITORS (If erectile dysfunction coexists):
   - Tadalafil (Cialis): 5 mg daily
   - FDA-approved for LUTS/BPH
   - Improves erectile function + urinary symptoms
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Back pain, myalgia, headache
   - If both ED and BPH, good option

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID:
- Anticholinergics: Problematic (worsen retention)
  * Antihistamines (diphenhydramine )
  * Decongestants (pseudoephedrine)
  * Antidepressants with anticholinergic effects
- Over-the-counter cold medicines: Many contain alpha-stimulants (worsen symptoms)

OVER-THE-COUNTER SUPPLEMENTS (Uncertain efficacy):
- Saw palmetto: Limited evidence, modest benefit
- Beta-sitosterol: Some evidence, modest benefit
- Pygeeum africanum: Mixed evidence
- Pumpkin seed: Anecdotal

SURGICAL/PROCEDURAL OPTIONS (If medical failure):
- TURP (Transurethral resection of prostate): Gold standard surgical option
- Laser procedures: HOLEP, PVP (less morbidity than TURP)
- Minimally invasive: UroLift, water vapor ablation (newer, office-based)

FOOD/LIFESTYLE:

FOODS TO EAT:
- Lycopene sources: Tomato, watermelon (lycopene may help)
- Fatty fish: Omega-3s
- Vegetables: Leafy greens, peppers
- Whole grains: Brown rice, oats
- Legumes / Dal
- Nuts: Moderation (high calorie)
- Berries: Antioxidants
- Turmeric / Haldi: Anti-inflammatory

FOODS/SUBSTANCES TO AVOID/LIMIT:
- Caffeine: Stimulates bladder, increases frequency/urgency
- Alcohol: Diuretic, irritates bladder
- Spicy foods: Irritate bladder
- Large fluid intake in evening: Causes nocturia
- High-fat foods: Some studies suggest association

HYDRATION TIMING:
- Adequate daytime: 2-3 liters
- Restrict evening: Reduce after dinner to minimize nocturia
- Bedtime routine: Void completely before sleep

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:

VOIDING BEHAVIOR:
- Double-void technique: After finishing, wait 30 seconds, void again (help empty residual urine)
- Scheduled voiding: Set times to prevent urgency
- Avoid straining: Relax abdominal muscles, take time

ACTIVITY:
- Regular exercise: Physical activity improves symptoms
- Walking: Helps bladder function

STRESS REDUCTION:
- Stress worsens urinary symptoms
- Relaxation techniques

NOCTURIA MANAGEMENT:
- Elevate legs during day: Reduces fluid in legs at night
- Compression stockings: If peripheral edema
- Diuretic timing: If on HTN medications, take in morning
- Sleep position: Supine reduces nocturia slightly

BEHAVIORAL STRATEGIES:
- Pelvic floor exercises: Limited evidence but safe
  * Kegel exercises: Strengthen urethral sphincter
  * Difficult to teach/perform correctly

WHEN TO SEEK DOCTOR:
- Symptoms developing: Urinary hesitancy, weak stream, frequency
- Nocturia affecting sleep quality
- Worsening despite treatment
- Hematuria: Blood in urine (need cancer evaluation)
- Symptoms of infection: Dysuria, frequency

WHEN URGENT:
- Complete retention: Unable to void (painful, requires catheterization)
- Severe hematuria: Bright red bleeding (usually benign but rule out cancer)
- Fever with urinary symptoms: Possible UTI/prostatitis

MONITORING:
- Symptom tracking: AUA-SI periodically
- Medication efficacy: Assess at 4-6 weeks initial
- PSA: Screening questionable, but if baseline high, follow trends
- Post-void residual: If symptoms not improving (check if mechanical obstruction)

TIMELINE TO IMPROVEMENT:
- Alpha-blockers: 1-2 weeks
- 5-alpha inhibitors: 3-6 months for max effect
- Combination: 6-12 months to see benefit

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Diabetes: Can worsen symptoms (elevated glucose, dehydration)
- Cardiovascular disease: Orthostatic hypotension risk (use alpha-blockers cautiously)
- OSA (Sleep apnea): Nocturia may be related, treating OSA may improve
- Insomnia: Nocturia major cause; treating BPH may improve sleep quality
- Erectile dysfunction: Coexists in >50%; some treatments overlap (tadalafil beneficial)

PROGNOSIS:
- Without treatment: Gradual progression (30-40% over 5 years)
- With medication: 30-50% symptom improvement (variable)
- Medical failure: 15-20% progress to surgery within 4-5 years
- Surgical success: 80-90% symptom improvement, durable effect
