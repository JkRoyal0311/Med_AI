DISEASE: COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease)
CATEGORY: Respiratory / Pulmonary
ICD-10: J43 (emphysema), J44 (COPD)
SEVERITY: Moderate-Severe; progressive, incurable

DESCRIPTION:
COPD is chronic progressive lung disease causing airflow obstruction, making breathing difficult.
Encompasses emphysema (alveolar destruction) and chronic bronchitis (inflammation, mucus). Primarily
from smoking (85%), but air pollution exposure also contributes. Affects 10% adults >40 years.
Currently 4th leading cause of death globally. Irreversible but manageable with treatment.

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY:

EMPHYSEMA:
- Alveolar destruction: Loss of elastic recoil
- Lack of elastic fibers: Loss of airways' supporting structure
- Air trapping: Difficulty exhaling
- Increased residual volume: Hyperinflation

CHRONIC BRONCHITIS:
- Inflammation of airways: Mucous gland hypertrophy
- Mucus plugging: Obstruction, impaired clearance
- Remodeling: Smooth muscle proliferation, fibrosis
- Infection: Recurrent bacterial superinfection

RISK FACTORS:

PRIMARY:
- Smoking: 85% of cases (active, passive, occupational)
- Air pollution: Industrial exposure, outdoor/indoor pollution
- Indoor fuel burning: Biomass, wood smoke (developing countries)

GENETIC:
- Alpha-1 antitrypsin (AAT) deficiency: Rare (<1%), severe early-onset COPD
  * Accelerated by smoking
  * Consider in COPD <45 or minimal smoking history

OCCUPATIONAL:
- Dust, chemicals: Mining, foundries, grain dust
- Silica, asbestos
- Occupational asthma progression to COPD

SYMPTOMS:

EARLY:
- Dyspnea: Shortness of breath, especially with exertion
- Cough: Chronic (persistent, usually productive)
- Sputum production: Daily expectoration

PROGRESSIVE (As disease worsens):
- Increased shortness of breath: At rest later
- Cough: Constant, often morning worse
- Sputum: Yellow/greenish when infected
- Wheezing: From airflow obstruction
- Chest tightness
- Fatigue: From breathing effort
- Weight loss: From increased metabolism

EXACERBATIONS:
- Acute worsening: Increased cough, sputum volume/color change
- Shortness of breath: More severe
- Fever: If infection
- Triggers: Infections (viral URI, bacterial), pollution, cold air

COMPLICATIONS:
- Acute respiratory failure: Requires hospitalization, possible intubation
- Pneumonia: Superinfection
- Pneumothorax: Spontaneous from bullae rupture
- Cor pulmonale: Right heart failure from chronic hypoxia
- Depression, anxiety: Common comorbidities

DIAGNOSIS:

SPIROMETRY (Gold standard):
- FEV1 (Forced Expiratory Volume in 1 second) / FVC ratio <0.70: Diagnostic
- Grades severity:
  * GOLD 1 (Mild): FEV1 ≥80% predicted
  * GOLD 2 (Moderate): FEV1 50-79% predicted
  * GOLD 3 (Severe): FEV1 30-49% predicted
  * GOLD 4 (Very Severe): FEV1 <30% predicted

IMAGING:
- Chest X-ray: Shows hyperinflation, bullae (emphysema), bronchial thickening (bronchitis)
- High-resolution CT: Better defines emphysema, helps prognosticate
- ECG: Check for cor pulmonale signs (right axis deviation, prominent P waves)

BLOOD TESTS:
- Arterial blood gas: If severe, shows CO2 retention, hypoxemia
- Alpha-1 antitrypsin: If <45 age diagnosis or minimal smoking
- CBC: Check for polycythemia (from chronic hypoxia)

MEDICATIONS USED:

BRONCHODILATORS (Primary treatment):

1. SHORT-ACTING BETA-2 AGONISTS (SABA):
   - Salbutamol (Albuterol): 2-4 puffs every 4-6 hours
   - Rapid relief: 10-15 minutes, duration 4-6 hours
   - Rescue medication for acute dyspnea
   - Brand names: Ventolin rescue inhaler
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Tremor, tachycardia
   - Use as needed

2. LONG-ACTING BETA-2 AGONISTS (LABA):
   - Salmeterol: 2 puffs twice daily
   - Formoterol: 2 puffs twice daily
   - Duration: 12-24 hours
   - Maintenance therapy (NOT for acute)
   - Brand names: Seretide (with ICS), alone as maintenance
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Tremor, tachycardia

3. ANTICHOLINERGICS (Long-acting):
   - Tiotropium (Spiriva): 1 puff daily
   - Ipratropium: 2 puffs 3-4 times daily (short-acting)
   - Blocks acetylcholine, dilates airways
   - Often combined with LABA
   - Brand names: Spiriva

4. COMBINATION THERAPY (LABA + LAMA):
   - Salmeterol + Fluticasone (ICS/LABA): Seretide
   - Tiotropium + Olodaterol (LABA/LAMA): Stiolto
   - Triple therapy: ICS + LABA + LAMA
   - Standard for moderate-severe
   - Better efficacy than single agents

CORTICOSTEROIDS (ICS — Inhaled):

5. FLUTICASONE PROPIONATE:
   - Dosage: 100-250 mcg twice daily (with LABA in combination)
   - Reduces airway inflammation
   - Daily use
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Oral candidiasis (rinse mouth), hoarse voice
   - Used with LABA (Seretide)
   - Brand names: Flovent

OTHER INHALERS:
6. THEOPHYLLINE (Rare now):
   - Dosage: 300-600 mg daily
   - Weak bronchodilator, mostly historical interest
   - Narrow therapeutic window
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Arrhythmias, seizures if overdose

OXYGEN THERAPY:

7. SUPPLEMENTAL OXYGEN:
   - For severe COPD with hypoxemia (O2 sat <88%)
   - Oxygen concentrator: Home-based, 15-20 hours/day
   - Portable oxygen: For exertional dyspnea
   - Goal: O2 sat >88-90%
   - Improves survival, reduces hospitalizations

EXACERBATION TREATMENT:

8. ANTIBIOTICS (If infection suspected):
   - If sputum becomes purulent
   - Ampicillin-sulbactam: 375-750 mg orally
   - Levofloxacin: 500 mg daily
   - Duration: 5-7 days

9. SYSTEMIC CORTICOSTEROIDS:
   - Prednisone: 20-40 mg daily x 3-5 days (tapering not needed for short course)
   - Reduces inflammation, speeds recovery
   - Given if exacerbation

10. RESPIRATORY SUPPORT (Severe):
    - Noninvasive positive pressure ventilation (NIPPV/BiPAP): If acute respiratory failure
    - Mechanical ventilation: If life-threatening hypoxemia/hypercapnia

FOODS TO EAT (Lung health):
- Fatty fish: Salmon, sardines (omega-3, anti-inflammatory)
- Vegetables: Leafy greens, peppers (antioxidants)
- Fruits: Berries, grapes (antioxidants)
- Whole grains: Brown rice, oats
- Legumes / Dal: Protein, fiber
- Turmeric / Haldi: Anti-inflammatory (curcumin)
- Ginger / Adrak: Anti-inflammatory
- Garlic / Lahsun: Antimicrobial properties
- Herbs: Varied nutrients
- Small, frequent meals: Avoid fatigue from digestion
- Hydration: 1.5-2 liters daily (helps mucus clearance)

FOODS TO AVOID:
- Salt: Excess (limit fluid retention, pulmonary edema risk)
- Gas-producing foods: Cruciferous vegetables can cause bloating
- Processed foods: Low nutrients
- Excess dairy: May increase mucus (debated, but some avoid)
- Fried foods: Heavy, require more oxygen for digestion
- Alcohol: May impair immunity
- High-calorie foods (obesity): Worsens dyspnea

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:

SMOKING CESSATION:
- MOST important intervention
- Slows decline dramatically
- Never too late to quit

VACCINATIONS:
- Pneumococcal vaccine: PCV13, PPSV23
- Influenza: Annual
- COVID-19: Recommended
- Pertussis: Tdap if not recently immunized

EXERCISE:
- Pulmonary rehabilitation: 8-12 weeks program
  * Exercise training: Improves exercise capacity
  * Benefits: Reduced dyspnea, improved quality of life
  * Smoking cessation counseling
  * Nutritional support
  * Psychological support
- Walking: Daily activity
- Breathing exercises: Pursed-lip breathing, diaphragmatic
- Strength training: Maintaining muscle mass

BREATHING TECHNIQUES:
- Pursed-lip breathing: Exhale against resistance (improves CO2 clearance)
- Slow deep breathing: Improves oxygenation

DIU AVOIDANCE:
- Exercise at cool times: Heat/humidity worsens symptoms
- Avoid pollutant exposure: Industrial, traffic
- Indoor air quality: Use air purifier if needed
- Cold air: Cover mouth when cold, wear scarf

MENTAL HEALTH:
- Depression common: Screen and treat
- Anxiety: Relaxation techniques
- Support groups: Helpful

MONITORING:

SPIROMETRY:
- Baseline and annually: Track FEV1 decline

EXACERBATION FREQUENCY:
- ≥2 per year warrant escalation of therapy
- Keep diary: Symptom tracking

BLOOD TESTS:
- Annually: CBC, oxygen saturation
- AAT level: If early-onset or minimal smoking

IMAGING:
- Chest X-ray: As needed (exacerbation, new symptoms)

WHEN TO SEEK DOCTOR:
- Persistent cough: New or worsening
- Shortness of breath: Increasing severity
- Change in sputum: Color, volume, consistency
- Wheezing: New or worsening
- Symptoms affecting activity: Limiting function

WHEN URGENT/EMERGENCY:
- Severe dyspnea: At rest
- Confusion or stupor
- Cyanosis: Blue lips/fingers
- Inability to speak: Severe dyspnea
- Chest pain: Rule out cardiac

COMPLICATIONS (If untreated):
- Respiratory failure: Requires intubation
- Cor pulmonale: Right heart failure, prognosis poor
- Pulmonary hypertension
- Recurrent exacerbations: Lead to hospitalization
- Pneumonia, pneumothorax

PROGNOSIS:
- Progressive disease: FEV1 declines ~50 mL/year (faster if smoking, slower if quit)
- With smoking cessation: Decline slows to ~20 mL/year
- Median survival: GOLD 4 (very severe) ~4 years
- One-year mortality post-exacerbation requiring hospitalization: 10-15%
- Quality of life: Significant impact, improves with treatment/rehabilitation

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- AAT deficiency: Much more aggressive, requires AAT replacement therapy
- Occupational COPD: May benefit from disability, occupational changes
- Exacerbations: Frequent exacerbators may benefit from triple therapy earlier
- Pulmonary rehabilitation: Should be offered to all, dramatically improves outcomes
