DISEASE: Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)
CATEGORY: Infectious / Urological
ICD-10: N39.0
SEVERITY: Mild-Moderate; can become serious if untreated (urosepsis)

DESCRIPTION:
UTI is bacterial infection of any part of the urinary system: bladder (cystitis), urethra (urethritis),
ureters, or kidneys (pyelonephritis). More common in women due to shorter urethra. Lower UTIs (bladder/urethra)
more common than upper UTIs (kidney). Most commonly caused by E. coli bacteria.

RISK FACTORS:
- Female sex: 10x more common in women
- Sexual activity: Introduces bacteria
- Pregnancy: Urinary stasis increases risk
- Urinary catheter
- Enlarged prostate: In men
- Kidney stones
- Urinary retention: Incomplete bladder emptying
- Diabetes: High glucose in urine, impaired immunity
- Immunosuppression
- Age: Increase in elderly, especially women > 65
- Spermicide use: Alters vaginal flora
- Anatomical abnormalities

SYMPTOMS:

LOWER UTI (Cystitis):
- Dysuria: Painful urination (burning sensation)
- Frequency: Urinating 8-15+ times daily
- Urgency: Sudden urge to urinate immediately
- Nocturia: Waking 2-3+ times at night to urinate
- Suprapubic pain: Lower abdominal pain above pubic bone
- Hematuria: Blood in urine (may appear clear or cloudy with bacteria)
- Urine cloudiness or foul smell
- Urge incontinence: Leakage with strong urge

UPPER UTI (Pyelonephritis) — MORE SERIOUS:
- High fever: Often >101.5°F / 38.6°C
- Flank pain: One or both sides (costovertebral angle tenderness)
- Nausea and vomiting
- General malaise and fatigue
- Chills
- Can progress to sepsis (shock, hypotension, confusion)

ASYMPTOMATIC BACTERIURIA:
- Bacteria in urine but no symptoms
- Usually requires treatment only if pregnant or before urologic procedures
- Otherwise observe

DIAGNOSIS:
- Urinalysis: Pyuria (white blood cells), bacteriuria
- Urine culture: Confirms diagnosis, identifies organism and antibiotic sensitivity
- Positive culture >100,000 CFU/mL (colony-forming units)
- Dipstick test: Nitrites and leukocyte esterase suggest UTI

MEDICATIONS USED:

FIRST-LINE ANTIBIOTICS:
1. NITROFURANTOIN (Macrodantin)
   - Dosage: 100 mg twice daily for 5-7 days
   - For uncomplicated cystitis only (not pyelonephritis)
   - Well-tolerated, minimal resistance
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Nausea, peripheral neuropathy (long-term)
   - Gives urine dark yellow/brown color (harmless)
   - CAUTION: Avoid near term in pregnancy

2. TRIMETHOPRIM-SULFAMETHOXAZOLE (TMP-SMX / Cotrimoxazole)
   - Dosage: TMP 160 mg + SMX 800 mg twice daily for 3 days
   - Effective, inexpensive
   - Not recommended if resistance >20% locally
   - ALLERGIC REACTION risk: Rash, Stevens-Johnson syndrome (rare)
   - CAUTION: Folic acid deficiency with long-term use

3. CEPHALEXIN (First-generation cephalosporin)
   - Dosage: 250-500 mg four times daily for 7 days
   - Broader spectrum
   - Safe in pregnancy
   - Cost-effective

4. AMOXICILLIN-CLAVULANATE
   - Dosage: 250-500 mg three times daily for 7 days
   - Good choice in pregnancy

FOR PYELONEPHRITIS (Upper UTI):
5. FLUOROQUINOLONES
   - Levofloxacin: 750 mg daily for 5 days
   - Ciprofloxacin: 500 mg twice daily
   - Excellent tissue penetration, good for kidney infections
   - CAUTION: Tendon rupture risk, avoid in children if possible
   - Only use if susceptibility confirmed

6. CEFTRIAXONE (Third-generation cephalosporin)
   - Dosage: 1-2 grams IV/IM daily
   - For severe pyelonephritis, hospitalization often needed
   - Broad spectrum, safe in pregnancy

SYMPTOM RELIEF:
7. PHENAZOPYRIDINE (Azo Dye)
   - Dosage: 100-200 mg three times daily for 2-3 days only
   - Analgesic for urinary tract pain
   - Turns urine orange/red (stains clothes)
   - Only numbs symptoms (not a cure)
   - Stop after 2-3 days and continue antibiotics

ANTISPASMODICS (For pain/urgency, optional):
8. OXYBUTYNIN
   - For severe urinary frequency/urgency
   - Use with antibiotic, not instead of

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID:
- Avoid if allergy: Penicillins (in penicillin-allergic patients)
- Acidifying agents: Historically used but not recommended
- Frequent fluoroquinolone use: Reserve for serious infections

FOODS AND BEVERAGES:

FOODS TO EAT (Support healing, promote urine flow):
- Cranberry / Khatte karonda: Compounds may prevent E. coli adherence
  (Evidence weak but culturally accepted; cranberry juice best)
- Blueberries: Similar compounds to cranberry
- Water: 2-3 liters daily (flushes bacteria, dilutes urine)
- Lemon water: Acidifies urine (some benefit)
- Coconut water: Hydrating, electrolytes
- Plain yogurt / Dahi: Probiotics (may prevent recurrence)
- Garlic / Lahsun: Antimicrobial properties
- Ginger / Adrak: Anti-inflammatory
- Turmeric / Haldi: Antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory
- Celery / Ajmud: Diuretic
- Parsley: Diuretic
- Brown rice: Easy to digest
- Vegetables: Spinach, broccoli, leafy greens (vitamins)
- Citrus fruits: Vitamin C
- Cauliflower: Sulfur compounds

FOODS/BEVERAGES TO STRICTLY AVOID (Irritate bladder):
- Coffee and strong tea: Caffeine irritates bladder
- Alcohol: Irritates bladder, delays healing
- Spicy foods: Irritate urinary tract
- Artificial sweeteners: Aspartame may irritate (controversial)
- Acidic foods (some people): Tomato, citrus (if sensitive)
- Chocolate: Some patients report irritation
- High-sodium foods: Reduce urine dilution

HYDRATION:
- Drink plenty of water to flush bacteria
- Avoid holding urine — urinate every 2-3 hours
- Particularly important during treatment

PREVENTION (For recurrent UTIs):
- Drink plenty of water: 2-3 liters daily
- Urinate regularly: Don't hold urine
- Empty bladder completely: Avoid urinary retention
- Cranberry juice: 8-10 oz daily (evidence modest but safe)
- D-Mannose: 500 mg twice-three times daily (may prevent recurrence)
- Post-intercourse urination: Empty bladder within 30 minutes after sex
- Avoid irritants: Douches, feminine hygiene sprays (disrupt normal flora)
- Wipe front-to-back: Prevent fecal bacteria near urethra
- Avoid tight clothing: Allow proper ventilation
- Avoid spermicides: Use other contraception if recurrent UTIs
- Barrier methods: Condoms with lubricant safer than spermicide
- Cotton underwear: Better than synthetic

PREGNANCY CONSIDERATIONS:
- UTI screening: All pregnant women screened at first prenatal visit
- Asymptomatic bacteriuria: Treated in pregnancy (risk of pyelonephritis)
- Medicines safe in pregnancy: Cephalosporins, amoxicillin (avoid trimethoprim 1st trimester)
- Avoid: Fluoroquinolones, trimethoprim (1st trimester), nitrofurantoin (near term)

WHEN TO SEEK IMMEDIATE ATTENTION:
- Fever >101.5°F / 38.6°C with back pain (possible pyelonephritis)
- Nausea/vomiting with fever (possible pyelonephritis)
- Confusion or altered mental status
- Severe flank pain
- Inability to urinate despite urge
- Blood clots in urine

WHEN TO SEE DOCTOR:
- Urinary symptoms not improving after 2-3 days antibiotics
- Recurrent UTIs (>2 per year): Needs investigation
- Male patient with UTI: Usually indicates complicated infection
- Diabetes + UTI: Close monitoring essential
- Pregnancy + any urinary symptoms
- Signs of systemic infection with UTI

COMPLICATIONS IF UNTREATED:
- Pyelonephritis: Kidney infection, fever, flank pain
- Sepsis: Bacteria enters bloodstream (life-threatening)
- Scarring of kidneys: Especially if recurrent infections
- Chronic kidney disease: From repeated damage

RECURRENT UTI (>2 per year):
Requires investigation:
- Urological imaging: Ultrasound or CT to rule out stones, obstruction
- Cystoscopy: Visual examination of bladder
- Urologic abnormalities: Sometimes found and need correction
- Bladder diary: May reveal urinary retention
- Sexual transmitted infections screening

Long-term prophylaxis options:
- Continuous low-dose antibiotic: Trimethoprim 1/3 strength daily or every other day
- Post-intercourse prophylaxis: Single dose after sex (for intercourse-related)
- Self-start regimen: Patient starts antibiotic at first symptom (for frequent symptomatic infections)

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Catheterized patients: High risk; routine antibiotics not recommended unless symptomatic
- Elderly women: Often asymptomatic bacteriuria; treatment only if symptoms or pregnancy
- Men: UTI less common; if occurs, indicates underlying pathology (prostate obstruction, etc.)
- Diabetes: Impaired immune response; infections more severe; more diligent treatment needed
