DISEASE: Stroke (Cerebrovascular Accident — CVA)
CATEGORY: Neurological / Cardiovascular / Emergency
ICD-10: I63 (ischemic), I61 (hemorrhagic)
SEVERITY: Critical/Life-threatening

DESCRIPTION:
Stroke is sudden loss of blood flow to brain causing brain cell death. Leading cause of disability
in adults, third leading cause of death globally. Two types: ischemic (85% — blood clot) and
hemorrhagic (15% — bleeding). Time-critical emergency: "Time is brain" — every minute matters.
Thrombolysis effective if given within 4.5 hours of symptom onset. Prevention crucial.

RISK FACTORS:

MODIFIABLE:
- Hypertension: Single biggest risk factor
- Diabetes: 2-4x increased risk
- Smoking: Increases clot risk
- High cholesterol: Atherosclerosis
- Obesity: BMI >30
- Physical inactivity: Sedentary lifestyle
- Excessive alcohol: Especially binge drinking
- Cocaine use: Causes vasospasm, increases clot risk
- Atrial fibrillation: Irregular heartbeat promoting clots
- Patent foramen ovale (PFO): Allows clots to bypass lungs

NON-MODIFIABLE:
- Age: Increases with age (doubling each decade after 55)
- Male: Higher risk in males
- Race/ethnicity: African Americans, Hispanics at higher risk
- Family history: Genetic predisposition
- Prior stroke: Previous stroke increases risk

TYPES:

ISCHEMIC STROKE (85%):
- Thrombotic: Clot forms in brain artery (emboli from heart, plaque rupture in vessel)
- Embolic: Clot travels from heart/other source, lodges in brain artery
- Lacunar: Small vessel disease affecting deep brain structures

HEMORRHAGIC STROKE (15%):
- Intracerebral: Bleeding within brain tissue (hypertension, amyloid angiopathy)
- Subarachnoid: Bleeding in space between arachnoid/pia (aneurysm rupture)

SYMPTOMS (RECOGNIZE IMMEDIATELY):

REMEMBER "FAST":
- FACE: Drooping on one side (ask to smile)
  * Arm: Numbness, weakness (raise both arms — one drifts down)
  * Speech: Slurred, difficulty speaking or understanding
  * Time: If any symptom, CALL EMERGENCY (111/100/90 India)

OTHER COMMON SYMPTOMS:
- Sudden onset unilateral weakness: Arm, leg, face
- Sudden numbness: Usually one side of body
- Sudden vision changes: Blurred, loss in one eye
- Sudden difficulty walking: Unsteady, loss of coordination
- Sudden severe headache: Worst headache of life (hemorrhagic)
- Sudden confusion: Difficulty understanding
- Sudden trouble speaking: Slurred speech, can't find words
- Sudden loss of consciousness
- Sudden incontinence

TIMING:
- SUDDEN ONSET: That's KEY — stroke symptoms come on suddenly
- Non-stroke conditions are gradual

DIAGNOSIS:

EMERGENT CT/MRI BRAIN:
- CT non-contrast: Rules out hemorrhagic stroke (first test usually)
  * Shows blood as white
  * Takes minutes
- MRI brain: DWI (diffusion-weighted imaging) shows ischemic stroke
  * More sensitive for early ischemia
  * Takes longer (20 minutes) — if available and no hemorrhage
- CT angiography: Shows vessels
- CT perfusion: Shows tissue at risk

LABORATORY:
- Blood glucose: Can mimic stroke, must rule out
- Coagulation studies: PT, INR, PTT
- Troponin: Check for cardiac cause
- CBC: Check for infection, polycythemia
- Liver/kidney function

EKG:
- Check for atrial fibrillation, MI (cardiac source of clot)

MEDICATIONS USED:

ACUTE ISCHEMIC STROKE (Time-sensitive):

1. THROMBOLYTIC (tPA — Tissue Plasminogen Activator):
   - Dosage: 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg)
     * 10% as IV bolus
     * Remaining over 1 hour
   - MUST be given within 4.5 hours of symptom onset
     * 3 hours standard window
     * 4.5 hours extended window if proper criteria
   - Dissolves blood clot
   - Improves outcome 30% (increases good recovery)
   - CONTRAINDICATIONS: Active bleeding, recent surgery, coagulopathy
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Bleeding (serious), must monitor closely

2. THROMBECTOMY:
   - Mechanical removal of clot via catheter
   - Indicated within 24 hours of large vessel occlusion (if candidate)
   - More effective than thrombolytics for large clots
   - Requires specialized center (intervention radiology)
   - Can be done alone or with thrombolysis

ACUTE HEMORRHAGIC STROKE:

3. BLOOD PRESSURE MANAGEMENT:
   - Goal: <180/105 mm Hg (balance cerebral perfectusion vs hematoma expansion)
   - Labetalol: 20 mg IV, can repeat every 10 minutes (max 80 mg)
   - Nicardipine: 2.5 mg IV every 5-15 minutes (up to 15 mg/hr)

4. HEMOSTASIS:
   - Stop anticoagulation: If on warfarin/DOAC
   - Reversal agents: Idarucizumab (Praxbind) for dabigatran, FFP for warfarin
   - Platelet transfusion: If on antiplatelet agents
   - Tranexamic acid: May reduce hematoma expansion (controversial)
   - FFP (Fresh frozen plasma): Coagulation factor replacement

ACUTE STROKE CARE (Both types):

5. ASPIRIN:
   - Dosage: 325 mg (or 500 mg) chewed immediately post-confirmation
   - Reduces risk of recurrent stroke by 10-13%
   - Give unless contraindication or thrombolysis planned

6. BLOOD SUGAR CONTROL:
   - Insulin: If hyperglycemia (adverse outcome if high glucose)
   - Goal glucose: 140-180 mg/dL

7. TEMPERATURE MANAGEMENT:
   - Avoid fever: Fever worsens outcome
   - Acetaminophen: Keep temperature normal
   - Cooling measures if febrile

8. SUPPORTIVE CARE:
   - IV fluids: Hydration important
   - Oxygen: If hypoxic
   - Bed rest initially
   - Head of bed elevated: 30 degrees
   - Monitor vitals closely

SECONDARY PREVENTION (After initial stroke):

9. ANTIPLATELET AGENTS:
   - Aspirin: 75-100 mg daily
   - Clopidogrel (Plavix): 75 mg daily (if aspirin intolerant)
   - Aspirin + Dipyridamole: For additional protection

10. ANTICOAGULATION (If cardioembolic source):
    - Warfarin: INR 2-3, if atrial fibrillation, cardiac thrombus
    - DOACs (Dabigatran, apixaban, rivaroxaban): If atrial fibrillation
    - Enoxaparin: Initially, then transition to warfarin/DOAC

11. STATIN (HIGH-DOSE):
    - Atorvastatin: 80 mg daily
    - Reduces cardiovascular events, recurrent stroke

12. BLOOD PRESSURE MANAGEMENT:
    - Antihypertensives: Goal <130/80 for secondary prevention
    - ACE inhibitor or ARB
    - Beta-blocker
    - Calcium channel blocker
    - Thiazide diuretic
    - Combination often needed

13. TYPE 2 DIABETES MANAGEMENT:
    - Intensive glucose control: Reduces stroke risk
    - Metformin: First-line
    - SGLT2 inhibitors: Additional cardiovascular benefits

REHABILITATION:

14. PHYSICAL THERAPY:
    - Start early (within 24 hours)
    - Improve strength, mobility, function

15. OCCUPATIONAL THERAPY:
    - Activities of daily living
    - Adaptive equipment training

16. SPEECH/SWALLOW THERAPY:
    - If aphasia or dysphagia
    - Critical early (prevent aspiration pneumonia)

FOODS TO EAT (Recovery, secondary prevention):
- Vegetables: Leafy greens (DASH diet)
- Fruits: Berries, citrus
- Fatty fish: Salmon, sardines (omega-3)
- Whole grains: Brown rice, oats
- Legumes / Dal: Beans
- Nuts: Almonds, walnuts (omega-3)
- Olive oil: Heart-healthy
- Low-fat dairy: Yogurt

FOODS TO STRICTLY AVOID:
- High-sodium foods: Restrict salt (<2 g/day)
- Trans fats: Processed, fried foods
- Red meat: Limit
- Alcohol: Can increase BP, especially excess

WHEN TO SEEK EMERGENCY IMMEDIATELY:

CALL EMERGENCY (100/111/999):
- Sudden facial drooping
- Sudden arm numbness/weakness
- Sudden trouble speaking
- Sudden vision change
- Sudden difficulty walking, dizziness
- Sudden severe headache

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Age: Younger strokes often cardioembolic (PFO, atrial fibrillation)
- Pregnancy/Postpartum: Increased stroke risk (hypercoagulability)
- Oral contraceptives: Increase risk (especially with smoking)
- Migraine with aura: Slightly increased stroke risk
- Sickle cell disease: Vasculopathy increases risk
- Vasculitis: Inflammatory, can cause stroke

PROGNOSIS:
- With rapid thrombolysis: 30% chance of minimal/no disability
- Outcomes worsen with delay: 60% have significant disability if delayed treatment
- Recovery: Best in first 3 months, then plateaus
- Many recover substantially with rehabilitation
- Recurrent stroke: 10% per year if untreated secondary prevention

PREVENTION (MOST IMPORTANT):
- Blood pressure: Aggressive control <130/80
- Cholesterol: Statins
- Diabetes: Glucose control
- Smoking: Cessation critical
- Atrial fibrillation: Anticoagulation if high risk
- Exercise: 150 min/week
- Healthy diet: Low sodium, Mediterranean/DASH
- Weight: Healthy BMI
- Alcohol: Minimize
