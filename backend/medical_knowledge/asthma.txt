DISEASE: Asthma (Bronchial Asthma)
CATEGORY: Respiratory / Chronic
ICD-10: J45
SEVERITY: Chronic — can be life-threatening if not controlled

DESCRIPTION:
Asthma is chronic inflammation of the airways causing recurrent breathing problems.
Airways become swollen and narrowed, producing extra mucus. Can be triggered by allergens,
exercise, infections, cold air, pollution. Symptoms range from mild (occasional wheezing) to severe (acute attacks).

RISK FACTORS:
- Family history of asthma or allergies
- Childhood allergic diseases (eczema, allergic rhinitis)
- Environmental exposure: pollution, smoke, pet dander
- Obesity
- Hormonal changes in women (worsens around menstruation)

SYMPTOMS (ACUTE ATTACK):
- Shortness of breath (dyspnea) — even at rest
- Wheezing: high-pitched whistling sound when breathing
- Chest tightness or chest pain
- Coughing — worse at night, during play, or after exercise
- Fatigue
- Difficulty speaking (severe — must finish sentences in gasps)
- Blue lips or fingernails (cyanosis) — EMERGENCY

SYMPTOMS (BETWEEN ATTACKS):
- Frequent cough
- Cough triggered by cold, exercise, allergen exposure
- Fatigue during physical activity
- Sleep disturbance from coughing or wheezing

MILD PERSISTENT ASTHMA vs SEVERE:
- Mild: Symptoms 2-4 days/week, nighttime waking 2-4 times/month
- Moderate: Symptoms daily, nighttime waking 1-2 times/week
- Severe: Symptoms throughout day, nighttime waking 7+ times/week, frequent attacks

MEDICATIONS USED:

QUICK-RELIEF (RESCUE) INHALERS — For acute attacks:
1. SALBUTAMOL / ALBUTEROL (Beta-2 Agonist)
   - Fast-acting, works in 5-15 minutes
   - Use during acute attack: 2 puffs, wait 5 minutes, repeat if needed
   - Brand names: Ventolin, Asthalin, Cipla
   - Use 1-2 times per week: If more, indicates poor control — see doctor
   - SAFE in pregnancy

LONG-TERM CONTROLLER INHALERS — Daily use to prevent attacks:
1. FLUTICASONE / BECLOMETHASONE (Inhaled Corticosteroid)
   - Reduces airway inflammation
   - Used daily — MUST use even when feeling well
   - Take with mouth wash after use (prevents oral thrush)
   - Brand names: Flovent, Beclazone
   - SAFE in pregnancy

2. SALMETEROL (Long-acting Beta-2 Agonist)
   - Used twice daily
   - NEVER use alone — always combine with inhaled corticosteroid
   - Prevents nighttime symptoms and exercise-induced asthma

3. COMBINATION INHALERS
   - Fluticasone + Salmeterol (Seretide)
   - Beclomethasone + Formoterol
   - Convenient single inhaler for daily control

4. MONTELUKAST (Leukotriene Antagonist - tablet)
   - Tablet form, once daily in evening
   - Especially helpful for exercise-induced asthma
   - Brand name: Singulair, Montair

ACUTE ATTACK TREATMENT:
- Sit upright, loosen tight clothing
- Use rescue inhaler immediately (2 puffs)
- If no improvement in 15 minutes: Repeat 2 more puffs
- If still no improvement: SEEK EMERGENCY CARE IMMEDIATELY

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID WITH ASTHMA:
- Beta-blockers (affects asthma, avoid if possible): Atenolol, Metoprolol
- NSAIDs: Ibuprofen, Diclofenac (can trigger attacks in some patients)
- ACE inhibitors: May cause persistent cough (triggers asthma)
- Aspirin: Contraindicated in aspirin-sensitive asthma
- Antigen blockers: Some have asthma/wheezing as side effect

ASTHMA TRIGGERS TO AVOID:
- Allergens: Dust mites, pet dander, pollen, mold
- Smoke: Tobacco, incense, air pollution, fireplace smoke
- Air pollution: Smog, vehicle exhaust, factory pollution
- Cold air: Wear scarf over mouth in winter
- Strong odors: Perfumes, paint, cleaning chemicals, incense
- Exercise: Use rescue inhaler 15 minutes before exercise
- Stress and emotional upset
- Infections: Colds, flu
- Gastroesophageal reflux (acid reflux)
- Menstrual cycle: Symptoms worse around period in some women

FOODS TO EAT:
- Omega-3 rich foods (Fish, Flaxseed): Anti-inflammatory
- Ginger / Adrak: Anti-inflammatory, reduces airway constriction
- Garlic / Lahsun: Has antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties
- Turmeric / Haldi: Curcumin reduces inflammation
- Onions / Pyaaz: Quercetin reduces airway inflammation
- Citrus fruits (Orange, Mosambi): Vitamin C helps immunity
- Honey / Shahad: Soothing for throat, antimicrobial
- Nuts and seeds: Vitamin E, antioxidants
- Green leafy vegetables / Palak, methi: Antioxidants, magnesium
- Whole grains: B vitamins, magnesium
- Dairy (if not allergic): Calcium, Vitamin D

FOODS TO STRICTLY AVOID:
- Dairy excess (if milk-sensitive): Some asthmatics worsen with excess dairy
- Eggs (if allergic): Common allergen in some asthmatic patients
- Peanuts and tree nuts (if allergic): Can trigger severe asthma
- Seafood (if allergic): Can trigger attack
- Food additives: MSG, food dyes, sulfites (in dried fruits, wine)
- Excess salt: May increase airway reactivity
- Processed foods: Often contain triggers and lack nutrients

EXERCISE WITH ASTHMA:
- Do NOT avoid exercise — stay active!
- Use rescue inhaler 15 minutes BEFORE exercise
- Warm up gradually for 10 minutes
- Cool down slowly for 5-10 minutes
- Avoid exercise in cold, dry air without scarf
- Swimming is excellent (warm, humid environment)
- Avoid dry air sports (cross-country skiing, ice hockey in very cold conditions)

ASTHMA ACTION PLAN:
ZONE GREEN (WELL): No symptoms, peak flow 80-100%
- Take daily controller inhaler
- Avoid triggers

ZONE YELLOW (CAUTION): Mild symptoms, peak flow 50-80%
- Use rescue inhaler (2 puffs)
- Increase controller medication
- Monitor closely
- Call doctor if not improving in 1-2 hours

ZONE RED (DANGER): Severe symptoms, peak flow below 50%
- Use rescue inhaler (2-4 puffs)
- Return to normal activity only after 4 hours symptom-free
- Call doctor immediately
- If no improvement in 15 minutes: SEEK EMERGENCY CARE

EMERGENCY SIGNS:
- Cannot finish sentences due to shortness of breath
- Difficulty walking or talking
- Skin retractions (between ribs, at neck, at belly showing)
- Severe wheezing or no wheezing with severe distress (bad sign)
- Blue lips or fingernails
- Confusion or altered consciousness
- Peak flow less than 40% of personal best

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Pregnancy: Many asthma controllers are safe. Consult doctor — uncontrolled asthma is riskier than medication
- Menstrual-related asthma: Symptoms worse around period — adjust medication timing
- Occupational asthma: Triggered by workplace allergens (chemicals, dust, animal products)
- Status asthmaticus: Severe, life-threatening asthma attack not responding to treatment — ICU admission required
