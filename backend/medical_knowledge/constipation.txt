DISEASE: Constipation
CATEGORY: Gastrointestinal
ICD-10: K59.1
SEVERITY: Usually mild-moderate; can be serious if causing ileus or impaction

DESCRIPTION:
Constipation is infrequent or difficult bowel movements. Defined as <3 stools per week, OR straining
for ≥25% of defecations, OR hard stools (Bristol scale 1-2). Extremely common, affects 2-30% of population
depending on definition. Usually functional (no serious underlying disease) but can indicate serious pathology.

TYPES:
- Occasional constipation: Triggered by diet, travel, medications
- Chronic constipation: Persistent, >3 months
- Opioid-induced: From narcotic pain medications (very common)
- Medication-related: From various drugs

CAUSES:

LIFESTYLE/DIETARY:
- Low fiber intake: Most common cause
- Low water intake: Dehydration
- Sedentary lifestyle: Lack of movement
- Ignoring urge to defecate: Stool returns to colon, reabsorbs water
- Stress and anxiety
- Travel and schedule disruption
- Age: Increases with age

MEDICATIONS:
- Opioid pain medications: Morphine, codeine, tramadol (stop bowel movement)
- Anticholinergics: Antihistamines, antidepressants, antispasmodics
- Iron supplements
- Calcium channel blockers
- Certain antacids (aluminum-containing)
- Diuretics

MEDICAL CONDITIONS:
- Hypothyroidism: Slowed metabolism
- Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS)
- Diabetes: Affects nerve function
- Parkinson's disease: Reduces intestinal movement
- Colon cancer: Narrowing of colon
- Anal fissures: Pain with defecation causes retention
- Hemorrhoids: Pain discourages defecation
- Obstruction: Complete or partial blockage (rare, emergency)
- Megacolon: Enlarged colon loses contractility
- Neurological: Spinal cord injury, stroke

SYMPTOMS:
- Infrequent stools: <3 per week
- Difficulty passing stool: Straining, effort
- Hard or lumpy stools: Difficult to pass
- Feeling of incomplete evacuation: Still need to go
- Abdominal bloating and discomfort
- Abdominal pain (may be severe)
- Loss of appetite
- Nausea
- Rectal pain or anal fissures from straining
- Hemorrhoids from straining
- Sense of fullness

RED FLAGS FOR SERIOUS PATHOLOGY:
- Sudden onset in previously regular person
- Severe pain
- Blood in stool: Bright red (hemorrhoids vs dark red suggesting upper GI)
- Weight loss
- Age >50 with new-onset constipation (screen for cancer)
- Severe abdominal distension (megacolon risk)

MEDICATIONS USED:

BULK LAXATIVES (First-line, safest):
1. PSYLLIUM HUSK (Plantago ovata)
   - Dosage: 1 teaspoon (3-5 grams) mixed with 8 oz water, 1-3 times daily
   - Brand names: Isabgol, Metamucil
   - Works in 12-24 hours (sometimes longer)
   - MECHANISM: Absorbs water, increases stool bulk and water content
   - SAFE long-term use
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Bloating initially (reduces over time), gas
   - Must drink plenty of water with it
   - Indian: "Isabgol" widely available, affordable

2. METHYLCELLULOSE
   - Similar to psyllium
   - Dosage: 1 tablespoon three times daily
   - Doesn't ferment (less gas than psyllium)

OSMOTIC LAXATIVES (Draw water into stool):
3. POLYETHYLENE GLYCOL (PEG, Miralax)
   - Dosage: 17 grams (1 heaping teaspoon) in 8 oz water daily
   - Works in 24-48 hours
   - Does not cause cramping like other osmotic laxatives
   - Brand names: Miralax
   - Safe for long-term use

4. LACTULOSE
   - Dosage: 15-30 mL (1-2 tablespoons) once or twice daily
   - Works in 24-48 hours
   - MECHANISM: Fermented by bacteria, increases osmotic load
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Bloating, cramping, gas (common)
   - Brand names: Duphalac, Lactosol

5. MAGNESIUM HYDROXIDE (Milk of Magnesia)
   - Dosage: 30-60 mL once daily
   - Works in 6-8 hours
   - CAUTION: Kidney disease (magnesium clearance impaired)

6. SODIUM PHOSPHATE (Fleet enema, oral)
   - Oral: 20-30 mL mixed in small amount water
   - Enema: 1-2 bottles administered rectally
   - Works in 30 minutes to 3 hours
   - RISK: Electrolyte imbalance if overused
   - Use cautiously

STIMULANT LAXATIVES (More powerful, for occasional use):
7. SENNA
   - Dosage: 1-2 tablets (sennosides) at night
   - Works in 6-12 hours
   - MECHANISM: Stimulates colonic contractions
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Cramping
   - CAUTION: Not for chronic use (may cause dependence)
   - Brand names: X-Prep, Sennox

8. BISACODYL
   - Dosage: 10 mg suppository or 5 mg tablet at night
   - Works in 6-12 hours for tablets, 15-60 minutes for suppository
   - CAUTION: Not for chronic use
   - Brand names: Dulcolax

STOOL SOFTENERS (For straining prevention):
9. DOCUSATE SODIUM
   - Dosage: 100 mg twice daily
   - Works in 24-48 hours
   - MECHANISM: Allows water and fat to mix with stool
   - Does NOT stimulate bowel movement (adjunctive, combine with fiber)
   - Brand names: Colace, Stool Softener
   - For opioid-induced constipation (not alone, combine with osmotic laxative)

COMBINATION PRODUCTS:
10. SENNA + DOCUSATE
    - Combines stimulant and stool softener
    - More effective than either alone

CHLORIDE CHANNEL ACTIVATORS (Newer):
11. LINACLOTIDE (for chronic idiopathic constipation)
    - Dosage: 145 mcg daily
    - Works over days to weeks
    - Brand name: Linzess
    - More expensive, for resistant cases

PROKINETIC AGENTS:
12. DOMPERIDONE
    - Enhances motility
    - Dosage: 10 mg three times daily
    - Slower effect than laxatives
    - For functional constipation

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID/DISCONTINUE (If possible):
- Opioid pain medications: Discuss alternatives with doctor
- Anticholinergics: Review with physician for necessity
- Iron supplements: Take with vitamin C to minimize constipation
- Calcium supplements: May worsen constipation

FOODS TO EAT (High-fiber):
- Whole grains: Brown rice, oats, whole wheat bread (15g fiber/day minimum)
- Legumes / Dal: Beans, lentils (highest fiber foods)
- Vegetables: Spinach, broccoli, peas, corn, squash
- Fruits: Pear, apple, prune (especially prune juice), kiwi, orange
- Seeds: Pumpkin, sunflower, chia, flaxseed seeds (1-2 tbsp daily)
- Nuts: Almonds, walnuts (insoluble fiber)
- Dried fruits: Prunes (dried plums), figs, apricots
- Leafy greens / Palak, methi: High fiber, low calorie
- Sweet potato: More fiber than white potato
- Beans and chickpeas / Chana: Very high fiber
- Bran: Oat bran, wheat bran (sprinkle on foods)

FOODS TO STRICTLY AVOID (Or minimize):
- White bread and white rice: Low fiber
- Processed foods: Often depleted of fiber
- Dairy excess: Can worsen constipation
- Red meat: Low fiber, can slow intestinal transit
- Regular cheese: Low fiber, binding
- Fried foods: Low fiber, harder to digest
- Chocolate: Can contribute to constipation (in some)
- Bananas (unripe): Constipating (ripe OK)
- Fiber supplements: NOT whole foods (refined)

HYDRATION (CRITICAL):
- Drink 2-3 liters water daily
- More if increasing fiber intake (fiber needs water to work)
- Coffee and tea: Have mild laxative effect
- Limit alcohol: Dehydrating

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS (MOST IMPORTANT):
- Increase fiber gradually: Sudden increase causes bloating
  * Add 5-10g fiber per week to tolerance
  * Reach 25-35g/day (women), 38g/day (men)
- Exercise: 150 minutes/week moderate activity improves bowel motility
- Regular timing: Try to have bowel movement at same time daily (morning often optimal)
- Respond to urge: Don't ignore need to defecate (reflex fades)
- Adequate time: Allow 10-15 minutes without rushing
- Privacy and comfort: Relaxed environment helps
- Avoid straining: Relax, let nature take its time
- Elevate feet: While on toilet (squatting position more natural)
- Abdominal massage: Circular motion clockwise may help
- Warm beverages: Morning warm water or tea stimulates defecation

REMEDIES:
- Warm prune juice: 6 oz morning (natural laxative)
- Flaxseeds: 1 tablespoon whole seeds with water daily
- Chia seed pudding: 1 tbsp seeds in milk overnight
- Warm lemon water: Morning stimulates bowel
- Walking: After meals improves motility
- Yoga: Forward bends, twists

WHEN TO SEEK DOCTOR:
- Sudden onset constipation (new in previously regular person)
- Severe abdominal pain with constipation
- Blood in stool: Bright red vs dark red (determine source)
- Weight loss with constipation
- Unrelieved by over-the-counter laxatives after 1 week
- Age >50 with new-onset constipation: Screen for colon cancer
- Persistent severe bloating or distension

RED FLAGS FOR EMERGENCY CARE:
- Severe, sudden abdominal pain with distension
- Unable to pass stool or gas for >24 hours (possible ileus/obstruction)
- Fever with constipation (possible appendicitis, diverticulitis)
- Fecal impaction: Hard mass of stool unable to be expelled
  (Possible complications: megacolon, perforation)

PREVENTION (Long-term):
- Consistent high-fiber diet: Prevention is easiest
- Regular exercise: Maintains bowel motility
- Adequate hydration: Daily water intake
- Regular bowel habits: Train bowel
- Manage stress: Stress affects gut function
- Regular follow-up: Monitor if using laxatives chronically

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Elderly: Common with medications; increased falls risk from medications
- Opioid users: Anticipated; prophylactic fiber + osmotic laxative recommended
- Pregnancy: Common; high-fiber diet, hydration, docusate preferred
- Children: Similar approach (age-appropriate doses)
- IBS-C: Fiber helps some, worsens others; IBS-specific triggers to avoid
- Chronic laxative abuse: Risk of cathartic colon (lost contractility); gradual withdrawal from stimulants
