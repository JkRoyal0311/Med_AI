DISEASE: Anxiety Disorder (Generalized Anxiety Disorder — GAD)
CATEGORY: Psychiatric / Mental Health
ICD-10: F41.1
SEVERITY: Mild-Moderate; significantly impacts quality of life

DESCRIPTION:
Generalized anxiety disorder is persistent, excessive worry about daily activities lasting ≥6 months.
Differs from normal worry by intensity, frequency, duration. Affects focus, sleep, physical health.
Prevalent in 2-3% population. Often coexists with depression, other anxiety disorders. Treatable with
medication and psychotherapy.

SYMPTOMS:

PSYCHOLOGICAL:
- Excessive worry: About everyday matters (work, health, finances, relationships)
- Uncontrollable worry: Difficult to stop, mind "racing"
- Anticipatory anxiety: Worry about future events
- Difficulty concentrating: Due to intrusive thoughts
- Difficulty making decisions: Overthinking
- Irritability: From strained mind
- Feeling on edge: Constant alertness
- Fear of losing control
- Fear of dying or having medical emergency (health anxiety variant)

PHYSICAL:
- Restlessness: Inability to sit still
- Muscle tension: Shoulders, neck, jaw
- Muscle aches: From sustained tension
- Tremor: Shaking hands
- Headaches: Often tension-type
- Fatigue: From sustained anxiety
- Sleep disturbance: Difficulty falling asleep, frequent awakenings
- Shortness of breath: Feeling of not enough oxygen
- Palpitations: Aware of rapid/irregular heartbeat
- Dizziness or lightheadedness
- Sweating: Cold sweats
- Nausea: Sometimes vomiting
- Frequent urination: From sympathetic activation
- Diarrhea: GI sensitivity
- Hot flashes: Or feeling chilled

SYMPTOMS WORSEN WITH:
- Stress events: Work deadlines, relationship issues
- Caffeine: Exacerbates physical symptoms
- Lack of sleep
- Rumination: Thinking about worries

DIAGNOSIS:
Clinical diagnosis based on DSM-5 criteria:
- Excessive worry ≥6 months about multiple domains
- Difficulty controlling worry
- ≥3 physical symptoms (from above list)
- Significant functional impairment
- Rule out medical causes

MEDICAL WORKUP:
- TSH: Rule out hyperthyroidism
- Electrocardiogram: If cardiac symptoms prominent
- Caffeine assessment: Rule out stimulant overuse
- Substance use screening: Withdrawal from alcohol/benzodiazepines

MEDICATIONS USED:

FIRST-LINE ANTIDEPRESSANTS:

1. SSRI (SELECTIVE SEROTONIN REUPTAKE INHIBITORS):

   A. SERTRALINE
      - Dosage: Start 50 mg daily, increase to 50-100 mg (max 200 mg)
      - Once-daily dosing (morning)
      - Takes 2-4 weeks for effect
      - SIDE EFFECTS: Initial activation, sexual dysfunction, weight gain (10-15%)
      - Generally well-tolerated
      - Low drug interactions
      - Brand names: Zoloft

   B. PAROXETINE
      - Dosage: Start 20 mg daily, increase to 20-40 mg (max 60 mg)
      - Once daily (morning)
      - Takes 2-4 weeks for effect
      - SIDE EFFECTS: More sedating, sexual dysfunction, weight gain, difficult to discontinue
      - Less preferred due to discontinuation syndrome
      - Brand names: Paxil

   C. ESCITALOPRAM
      - Dosage: Start 10 mg, increase to 10-20 mg (max 20 mg due to cardiac concerns at higher doses)
      - Once daily (morning)
      - Better tolerated than paroxetine
      - Takes 2-4 weeks
      - SIDE EFFECTS: Sexual dysfunction (less than others), generally good profile
      - Brand names: Lexapro

   D. FLUOXETINE
      - Dosage: Start 20 mg daily, increase to 20-40 mg (max 80 mg)
      - Long half-life (helps if missed doses)
      - Takes 4-6 weeks for effect (slower onset)
      - SIDE EFFECTS: Activating, sexual dysfunction
      - Brand names: Prozac

2. SNRI (SEROTONIN-NOREPINEPHRINE REUPTAKE INHIBITORS):

   A. VENLAFAXINE (Extended-release)
      - Dosage: Start 37.5-75 mg, increase to 150-225 mg (max 225 mg)
      - Once daily dosing
      - Takes 2-4 weeks for effect
      - SIDE EFFECTS: Hypertension possible, sexual dysfunction, activating, difficult discontinuation
      - Monitor BP at higher doses
      - Brand names: Effexor

   B. DULOXETINE
      - Dosage: Start 30-60 mg daily, increase to 60 mg (max 120 mg)
      - Once daily (can divide dose)
      - Takes 1-2 weeks for effect
      - SIDE EFFECTS: Nausea (common initially), sexual dysfunction, dizziness
      - Take with food to reduce nausea
      - Brand names: Cymbalta

SECOND-LINE AGENTS:

3. BUSPIRONE (Anxiolytic, non-controlled)
   - Dosage: Start 7.5 mg twice daily, increase to 15-30 mg daily (max 60 mg)
   - Takes 2-4 weeks for effect (longer than SSRIs)
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Minimal; dizziness possible, headache
   - NO dependence potential
   - CAUTION: Less effective than SSRIs for severe anxiety
   - Good option for mild-moderate anxiety as monotherapy

4. TRICYCLIC ANTIDEPRESSANTS (Older, less preferred):
   - Imipramine, Amitriptyline
   - More side effects than SSRIs
   - Use if SSRIs/SNRIs failed or contraindicated

BENZODIAZEPINES (Short-term only):

5. LORAZEPAM
   - Dosage: 0.5-2 mg daily, divided doses
   - Takes effect within 30 minutes
   - IMPORTANT: Use only SHORT-TERM (2-4 weeks)
   - RISK: Dependence, tolerance, rebound anxiety (with discontinuation)
   - May be bridging therapy (while SSRI takes effect — takes weeks)
   - DO NOT use long-term
   - Brand names: Ativan

6. ALPRAZOLAM
   - Dosage: 0.25-1 mg daily, divided
   - Faster onset than lorazepam but shorter half-life
   - Same concerns as lorazepam (dependence, tolerance)
   - Brand names: Xanax

OTHER AGENTS:

7. PREGABALIN (Anticonvulsant)
   - Dosage: Start 150 mg/day, increase to 300-600 mg (max 600 mg)
   - Divided doses
   - Takes 1 week for effect
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Dizziness, drowsiness, weight gain
   - FDA-approved for GAD
   - Alternative if SSRIs not tolerated

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID:
- Abrupt discontinuation of benzodiazepines: Risk of seizures, rebound anxiety
- Exceeding SSRI doses: No additional benefit, more side effects
- Long-term benzodiazepines: Develop tolerance, dependence
- Non-prescribed stimulants: Worsen anxiety
- High-dose NSAIDs: May interact with some SSRIs

FOODS TO EAT (Mood and anxiety support):
- Complex carbohydrates: Whole grains, brown rice (serotonin support)
- Lean proteins: Chicken, fish, turkey (amino acids)
- Omega-3 fatty acid sources: Salmon, sardines, walnuts (anti-inflammatory)
- Leafy greens / Palak, methi: Magnesium, folate
- Legumes / Dal: B vitamins, magnesium
- Nuts and seeds: Magnesium, selenium (supports mood)
- Berries: Antioxidants
- Bananas: Tryptophan, B vitamins
- Eggs: B vitamins, choline
- Turmeric / Haldi: Curcumin has anti-inflammatory properties
- Ginger / Adrak: Calming properties
- Herbal teas: Chamomile, passionflower (mild calming)
- Dark chocolate: Small amounts (serotonin support, magnesium)

FOODS/SUBSTANCES TO AVOID:
- Caffeine: Worsens anxiety (coffee, tea, cola, chocolate, energy drinks)
- High sugar foods: Energy spikes/crashes
- Alcohol: Initially calming but worsens anxiety long-term
- Processed foods: Chemical additives, low nutrients
- Excess sodium: Can increase anxiety
- High-fat foods: Heavy, sluggish feeling
- Artificial sweeteners: Controversial, some worsen anxiety
- Stimulating foods: Spicy foods (sometimes trigger anxiety)

CAFFEINE REDUCTION:
- Gradual taper: Not abrupt (can cause headaches)
- Transition to decaf or herbal tea
- READ LABELS: Hidden caffeine in chocolate, soft drinks, non-cola sodas

BEHAVIORAL TREATMENTS (Equal to medication):

COGNITIVE BEHAVIORAL THERAPY (CBT):
- Identify worry triggers
- Challenge catastrophic thinking
- Behavioral experiments to test fear
- Relaxation techniques
- 12-20 sessions, 70-80% response rate
- Gold standard if combined with medication

RELAXATION TECHNIQUES:
- Progressive muscle relaxation: Tense, release muscles
- Diaphragmatic breathing: 4-7-8 technique
- Mindfulness meditation: Acceptance-based
- Yoga: Poses, breathing, meditation
- Tai Chi: Slow, meditative movement

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:
- Exercise: 150 min/week aerobic (anxiolytic effect)
- Sleep: 7-8 hours nightly
- Limit caffeine: Eliminate or very low
- Stress reduction: Identify and manage stressors
- Social support: Maintain relationships
- Proper nutrition: Regular, balanced meals
- Avoid alcohol: Even "calming" use is counterproductive
- Time outdoors: Nature exposure
- Meditation/mindfulness: Daily 10-20 minutes
- Grounding techniques: 5-4-3-2-1 sensory awareness

MONITORING TREATMENT:
- Assess after 2-3 weeks: Breakthrough symptoms?
- Assess after 4 weeks: Medication working?
- Adjust dose if insufficient response
- Continue for 6-12 months minimum once effective
- Gradual tapering if discontinuing (after response achievable)

WHEN TO SEEK DOCTOR:
- Worry interfering with work, relationships
- Physical symptoms: Insomnia, heart palpitations
- Substance use for anxiety relief (alcohol, drugs)
- Thoughts of self-harm
- Not improving with lifestyle changes
- Need of psychological support

WHEN TO SEEK URGENT ATTENTION:
- Severe panic attack with cardiac symptoms
- Thoughts of suicide or self-harm
[SEEK EMERGENCY CARE IF URGENT]

SIDE EFFECT MANAGEMENT:
- Initial activation/jitteriness: Usually resolves in 1-2 weeks; reassure patient
- Sexual dysfunction: May resolve given time; can switch agents or add bupropion
- Weight gain: Common with SSRIs; monitor, may need lifestyle intervention
- Nausea: Take with food, usually resolves
- Dry mouth: Sugar-free gum, mouthwash
- Discontinuation syndrome: Gradual taper over 4-12 weeks

PROGNOSIS:
- With treatment: 60-70% achieve significant improvement
- Without treatment: Often chronic, fluctuating course
- Combination (medication + CBT): Best outcomes
- Many require long-term treatment

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Pregnancy: SSRIs safe, some risk with benzodiazepines in 1st trimester
- Elderly: SSRIs preferred, avoid benzodiazepines (fall, memory risk)
- Substance abuse history: Avoid benzodiazepines (dependence risk)
- Medical conditions: Rule out hyperthyroidism, cardiac causes first
- Comorbid depression: SSRIs/SNRIs treat both
- Insomnia: Choose more sedating agents (sertraline, mirtazapine) if needed
