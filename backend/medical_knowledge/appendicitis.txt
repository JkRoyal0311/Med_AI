DISEASE: Appendicitis (Acute Appendicitis)
CATEGORY: Gastrointestinal / Surgical Emergency
ICD-10: K35
SEVERITY: Serious; surgical emergency if not treated

DESCRIPTION:
Appendicitis is acute inflammation of appendix (small tube attached to colon). Classic surgical
emergency. Most common indication for emergency abdominal surgery. Can progress to perforation,
peritonitis, sepsis if untreated. Classic presentation with periumbilical pain relocating to RLQ.
Diagnosis confirmed with imaging. Treatment is emergency appendectomy.

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY:
- Appendiceal obstruction: Lymphoid hyperplasia, fecal impaction, stricture, tumor
- Increased intraluminal pressure: Leads to bacterial translocation
- Mucosal ulceration: Bacterial invasion
- Inflammation/infection: If untreated, gangrene and perforation
- Perforation: Spills bacteria into peritoneal cavity (peritonitis)

RISK FACTORS:
- Young age: Peak 10-30 years (can occur any age, rare <5)
- Male: Slight predominance
- Family history: Rare
- Genetics: Some familial clustering
- Lymphoid hyperplasia: Post-viral infections (preceding URI common)
- Increased lymphoid tissue: Foods with seeds increasing obstruction (debated)

SYMPTOMS:

CLASSIC PRESENTATION (Migrating pain):
- Initial: Periumbilical (around belly button) pain
  * Dull, crampy
  * Lasts 2-12 hours
  * Often accompanies nausea/vomiting
- Then migrates to RLQ (right lower quadrant)
  * Below inguinal ligament, medial to anterior superior iliac spine
  * McBurney's point: Most tender area (one-third distance from anterior superior iliac spine to umbilicus)
  * Pain becomes sharp, constant
  * Worsened by movement, coughing, defecation

ASSOCIATED SYMPTOMS:
- Nausea and vomiting: 70-80% (usually after pain onset)
- Fever: Usually mild (37.5-38.5°C) — high fever suggests perforation
- Loss of appetite
- Possible diarrhea or constipation
- Urinary symptoms: If inflamed appendix near ureter

ATYPICAL PRESENTATIONS (More difficult diagnosis):
- Retrocecal appendix: Pain back/flank (not classically RLQ)
- Pelvic appendix: Suprapubic pain, diarrhea
- Left-sided: Very rare anatomical variation
- Pregnant women: Appendix displaced upward by pregnancy
- Elderly: Often subtle symptoms, delay in diagnosis

SEVERE/PERFORATED (Emergency):

- Sudden relief of pain (misleading — actually perforation!)
  * Before generalized peritonitis develops
- Then diffuse abdominal pain
- Severe, generalized tenderness
- Rigid abdomen: Hard, board-like ("surgical abdomen")
- Fever: Usually higher >38.5°C
- Septic appearance: Tachycardia, hypotension
- Shock: If advanced peritonitis

DIAGNOSIS:

CLINICAL EXAM:
- McBurney's point tenderness: Classic location
- Rebound tenderness: Pain worse when pressing then releasing (peritoneal irritation)
- Guarding: Involuntary muscle tension
- Rovsing's sign: RLQ pain with LLQ palpation (visceral peritoneal irritation)
- Psoas sign: Pain with hip extension (psoas muscle inflamed)
- Obturator sign: Pain with hip internal rotation (obturator internus inflamed)

IMAGING (Gold standard):
- CT abdomen/pelvis: 95% sensitivity, specifically shows:
  * Thickened appendiceal wall
  * Appendiceal distension
  * Periappendiceal inflammatory changes
  * Fat stranding
  * Fluid collection (if perforation/abscess)
  * Best modality
- Ultrasound: Operator-dependent, may miss
  * Shows inflamed appendix (non-compressible, >6 mm diameter)
  * Good for children, pregnant women (no radiation)
- MRI: Useful in pregnancy (best safety profile)

LABORATORY:
- CBC: WBC elevation (usually 10,000-18,000) — not always present
- CRP: Elevated (marker of inflammation)
- Pregnancy test: In women of reproductive age (to guide imaging)
- Urinalysis: Rule out UTI/kidney stone (similar pain)

DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS (Important to consider):
- Mesenteric adenitis: Viral inflammation of mesenteric lymph nodes
  * Similar presentation, resolves conservatively
  * Discovered on imaging as appendix normal
- Crohn's disease: Ileitis mimicking appendicitis
- Gastroenteritis: Viral gastroenteritis
- Pelvic inflammatory disease (Women): PID, ovarian pathology
- Kidney stone: Renal colic
- Ectopic pregnancy: Women reproductive age

MEDICATIONS USED:

PREOPERATIVE:
1. PAIN MANAGEMENT:
   - Morphine: IV 2-4 mg every 2-4 hours
   - Fentanyl: IV 25-50 mcg for acute pain
   - Modern practice: Analgesia doesn't mask diagnosis if imaging done

2. ANTIEMETICS:
   - Ondansetron: IV 4 mg for nausea
   - Metoclopramide: IV 10 mg

3. IV FLUIDS:
   - Normal saline: Large-bore IV
   - Replace dehydration from vomiting
   - NPO (nothing by mouth) until surgery

4. PROPHYLACTIC ANTIBIOTICS:
   - Ceftriaxone: 1-2 g IV every 8-12 hours
   - Metronidazole: 500 mg IV every 6-8 hours
   - OR: Piperacillin-tazobactam single-agent
   - To reduce post-op infection risk

PERIOPERATIVE:
5. ANESTHESIA:
   - General anesthesia with intubation
   - Agents: Propofol, sevoflurane, etc. (anesthesiologist choice)

6. OPERATIVE:
   - APPENDECTOMY: Surgical removal of appendix
   - Open: Standard midline/gridiron incision (becoming less common)
   - Laparoscopic: 2-3 small incisions (current standard)
     * Faster recovery
     * Less pain
     * Better visualization
   - Single-port: Emerging technique via umbilicus

POSTOPERATIVE:
7. PROPHYLACTIC ANTIBIOTICS:
   - Continue 24 hours post-op (or 48 hours if perforated)
   - Cefazolin: Standard gram-positive coverage
   - Metronidazole: If anaerobic coverage needed

8. PAIN MANAGEMENT:
   - IV opioids initially: Morphine, hydromorphone
   - Transition to oral acetaminophen/NSAIDs
   - Usually discharged to acetaminophen + codeine or similar

FOODS (Post-operative):

IMMEDIATELY AFTER SURGERY:
- NPO: Until bowel function returns
- IV fluids: Until ready for oral intake
- Clear liquids: Once GI sounds return
- Gradual advancement: To regular diet over days

POST-HOSPITALIZATION:
- Light meals initially
- Gradually resume normal diet
- Fiber: To prevent constipation post-op
- Hydration: Important for recovery

LIFESTYLE POST-OP:
- Activity restriction: 1-2 weeks avoiding strenuous activity
- Wound care: Keep incision clean, dry
- Suture removal: 7-10 days post-op (if non-absorbing)
- Return to work: 1-2 weeks for light duty, 4-6 weeks full activity

WHEN TO SEEK EMERGENCY ATTENTION:
[APPENDICITIS IS SURGICAL EMERGENCY — SEEK IMMEDIATE CARE IF SUSPECTED]

Classic presentation RLQ pain + fever + vomiting:
- CALL 911 / GO TO ER IMMEDIATELY
- Do not eat/drink (NPO before surgery)
- Do not apply heating pad (increases risk of perforation)

COMPLICATIONS:

PERFORATION (5-7% if diagnosis delayed):
- Appendix ruptures into peritoneal cavity
- Generalized peritonitis (life-threatening)
- Sepsis, septic shock possible
- Mortality: 5-10% if perforation (0.1-0.3% if uncomplicated)
- Treatment: Emergency surgery, post-op antibiotics 7-14 days

ABSCESS:
- Walled-off infection from perforated appendix
- Pain, fever persist post-op
- May need percutaneous drainage
- Can be managed conservatively with antibiotics in some cases

PERITONITIS:
- Diffuse peritoneal inflammation
- Medical emergency requiring immediate surgery
- Signs: Severe pain, rigid abdomen, fever, shock

PROGNOSIS:
- Uncomplicated appendicitis: >99% survival with appendectomy
- 1-2 day hospital stay typical
- Full recovery 4-6 weeks
- Perforation: 5-10% mortality if delayed treatment

WHEN TO SEE DOCTOR (Before emergency):
- RLQ pain lasting >6 hours
- Pain migrating from periumbilical to RLQ
- Pain worsening
- Associated fever, vomiting
- Any concern: Getting imaging study ensures diagnosis

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Pregnancy: Appendicitis still common during pregnancy
  * Difficult diagnosis (displaced appendix, mimics pregnancy pain)
  * Ultrasound/MRI preferred over CT (radiation concern)
  * Appendectomy still safe if diagnosed; misdiagnosis risks fetal loss
- Children: Present differently (more diffuse, less classic history)
- Elderly: Atypical presentation, higher perforation rate (diagnosis delay)
- Immunocompromised: Higher perforation risk, more aggressive course

CHRONIC APPENDICITIS (Rare):
- Recurrent RLQ pain from subclinical inflammation
- Imaging normal or shows mild changes
- Appendectomy may relieve symptoms (contentious diagnosis)

[APPENDICITIS IS ALWAYS A SURGICAL EMERGENCY — TREATMENT IS APPENDECTOMY]
