DISEASE: Hyperthyroidism (Overactive Thyroid)
CATEGORY: Endocrine
ICD-10: E05
SEVERITY: Chronic — manageable but serious if untreated (thyroid storm)

DESCRIPTION:
Hyperthyroidism occurs when the thyroid gland produces too much thyroid hormone (T3 and T4),
speeding up metabolism. Caused most commonly by Graves' disease (autoimmune) or toxic multinodular goiter.
Increases heart rate, burns energy rapidly, causes anxiety, weight loss. Opposite of hypothyroidism.
Manageable with antithyroid drugs, beta-blockers, or radioactive iodine.

CAUSES:
1. Graves' disease (60-90% of cases): Autoimmune, more common in women
2. Toxic multinodular goiter: Multiple overactive nodules
3. Toxic adenoma: Single overactive nodule
4. Thyroiditis: Inflammation of thyroid (transient hyperthyroidism)
5. Iodine excess: From medications or iodine contrast
6. Factitious hyperthyroidism: Ingesting thyroid hormone pills

SYMPTOMS:

METABOLIC/HEAT INTOLERANCE:
- Heat intolerance: Feeling excessively hot, sweating easily
- Profuse sweating: Even in cool environment, nighttime sweats
- Weight loss: Despite good appetite or increased eating
- Appetite increase: Hunger and food intake elevated

CARDIOVASCULAR:
- Tachycardia: Fast resting heart rate (>90 bpm, often >100 bpm)
- Palpitations: Feeling heart beating (especially with exertion)
- Heart irregularities: Atrial fibrillation in some (increases stroke risk)
- Shortness of breath: With minimal exertion

NERVOUS SYSTEM:
- Tremor: Shaky hands (fine tremor, visible when hands extended)
- Anxiety: Nervousness, restlessness, anxiety attacks
- Irritability and mood instability
- Difficulty concentrating
- Insomnia: Despite fatigue
- Hyperactivity: Excessive energy

EYES (Graves' disease specific):
- Exophthalmos: Bulging eyes (forward protrusion)
- Eye irritation, grittiness, tearing
- Diplopia: Double vision (from eye muscle changes)
- Light sensitivity

MUSCULAR:
- Muscle weakness: Generalized, proximal (shoulders, hips)
- Fatigue: Paradoxically, despite high metabolism
- Muscle pain or tenderness

GASTROINTESTINAL:
- Diarrhea: Loose stools, increased bowel movements
- Nausea
- Abdominal discomfort

REPRODUCTIVE:
- Amenorrhea or irregular periods in women
- Decreased libido
- Erectile dysfunction in men
- Infertility issues

SKIN/HAIR:
- Warm, moist, velvety skin
- Fine, thinning hair
- Pretibial myxedema (Graves'): Lumpy, thickened skin on shins (specific to Graves')

GENERAL:
- Goiter: Enlarged thyroid gland (visible swelling in neck)
- Low-grade fever sometimes

DIAGNOSIS:
- TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone): LOW or suppressed
- Free T4: HIGH
- Free T3: HIGH
- Thyroid antibodies: TPO, thyroglobulin (if autoimmune Graves')
- TSI (thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulin): Positive in Graves' disease
- Thyroid scan: Shows increased uptake in Graves' (multinodular pattern in toxic goiter)
- Ultrasound: To assess goiter size, nodules

MEDICATIONS USED:

ANTITHYROID DRUGS (Block thyroid hormone production):
1. PROPYLTHIOURACIL (PTU)
   - Dosage: 50-100 mg three times daily initially, adjust based on response
   - Also blocks T4 to T3 conversion (additional benefit)
   - Takes 1-2 weeks to see effect
   - PREFERRED in first trimester of pregnancy (lower placental transfer)
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Rash, nausea, abdominal pain, agranulocytosis (rare but serious: low WBC)
   - MONITORING: CBC, LFTs baseline and if symptoms develop

2. METHIMAZOLE
   - Dosage: 10-40 mg once or twice daily
   - Longer half-life (can take once daily)
   - Faster onset than PTU
   - CONTRAINDICATED in first trimester pregnancy (fetal abnormalities)
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Similar to PTU, including agranulocytosis
   - Preferred for long-term therapy (fewer liver problems than PTU)
   - MONITORING: CBC, LFTs

BETA-BLOCKERS (For symptom relief, not treatment):
3. PROPRANOLOL
   - Dosage: 40-80 mg three times daily
   - Controls tachycardia, tremor, anxiety within hours
   - Does NOT reduce thyroid hormone levels (symptomatic relief only)
   - Used while waiting for antithyroid drugs to work
   - ADVANTAGE: Also blocks T4 to T3 conversion (small additional benefit)
   - CAUTION: Avoid abrupt discontinuation

4. ATENOLOL
   - Alternative beta-blocker, once-daily dosing
   - Does NOT block T4-T3 conversion

RADIOACTIVE IODINE (Definitive treatment):
5. RADIOACTIVE IODINE-131 (I-131)
   - Dosage: Calculated based on gland size and uptake (typically 5-30 mCi)
   - Single oral capsule
   - Destroys thyroid tissue, eliminates hyperthyroidism
   - Takes 6-12 weeks for full effect
   - PERMANENT cure (after 6-12 weeks)
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Hypothyroidism develops in most patients (expected, managed with levothyroxine)
   - CONTRAINDICATED in pregnancy (damages fetal thyroid)
   - CAUTION in breastfeeding (wait 6+ months)
   - Risk of Graves' eye disease worsening (use beta blockers + antithyroid drugs first if significant eye disease)
   - Pregnancy: Avoid conception for 6 months after I-131

THYROID SURGERY:
6. THYROIDECTOMY (Subtotal or total removal)
   - Definitive cure (like I-131)
   - Requires perioperative preparation with antithyroid drugs
   - Risks: Recurrent laryngeal nerve damage (voice change), hypoparathyroidism (calcium problems)
   - Used less commonly now (I-131 preferred in most cases)
   - Used if: Pregnancy, desire to become pregnant soon, very large goiter, allergy to medication, I-131 contraindicated

TREATMENT APPROACH:
For Graves' disease:
- Initial: PTU or Methimazole + Beta-blocker
- After 1-2 years: Consider I-131 or thyroidectomy for definitive treatment
- OR: Continue antithyroid drugs long-term

For toxic nodules:
- Definitive treatment: I-131 or surgery (drugs less effective in nodular disease)

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID:
- Iodine excess: Iodized salt, iodine supplements (worsen hyperthyroidism)
- Excess caffeine: Worsens palpitations and anxiety
- Stimulant medications: Worsen symptoms
- Some antihistamines: May contain stimulants

FOODS TO EAT:
- Selenium-rich: Brazil nuts, fish, eggs (supports immune function)
- Zinc-rich: Pumpkin seeds, legumes, poultry (immune regulation)
- Antioxidant foods: Berries, citrus, leafy greens (reduce inflammation)
- Whole grains: Brown rice, oats (sustained energy)
- Protein: Fish, chicken, eggs, legumes (support muscle)
- Calm-promoting foods: Foods with L-theanine (green tea)
- Magnesium: Almonds, seeds, leafy greens (anxiety reduction)

FOODS TO STRICTLY AVOID (or LIMIT):
- Iodine-rich foods: Seaweed / Nori (very high iodine), shellfish (fish OK in moderation)
- Excess salt: Added iodine
- Caffeine: Coffee, strong tea, chocolate (worsens palpitations and anxiety)
- Excess sugar: Impairs immune function
- Fried and fatty foods: Difficult digestion with hypermetabolism
- Alcohol: Can interact with medications, worsen palpitations

IODINE AVOIDANCE (In Graves' disease):
- Excess iodine can paradoxically worsen hyperthyroidism
- Limit iodized salt
- Avoid excess seaweed, nori
- Check medications for iodine content (especially cough syrups, contrast agents)

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:
- Stress management: Stress exacerbates Graves' disease
- Yoga, meditation: Reduce anxiety
- Adequate sleep: 7-9 hours (support immune function)
- Avoid strenuous exercise: Until controlled (tachycardia, palpitation risk)
- Gentle exercise: Walking, stretching when stable
- Cool environment: More comfortable with heat intolerance
- Eye protection (Graves'): Dark glasses, artificial tears, elevate head at night

EYE CARE (If Graves' ophthalmopathy):
- Artificial tears: Frequent use for dryness
- Dark glasses: UV protection, reduces light sensitivity
- Sleep with head elevated: Reduces fluid retention in eyes
- Selenium supplement: 200 mcg daily (some evidence for eye disease)
- Avoid smoking: Worsens eye disease
- Doctor consultation: May need immunosuppressants if severe (corticosteroids, radiotherapy)

PREGNANCY CONSIDERATIONS:
- PTU preferred first trimester (lower fetal risk than methimazole)
- Block and replace regimen: Use higher PTU dose, add levothyroxine to avoid hypothyroidism in fetus
- Close monitoring: Thyroid function monthly
- Antithyroid drugs do cross placenta (risks fetal goiter, hypothyroidism)
- Graves' disease may worsen first trimester (exacerbate second trimester (remit))
- Postpartum: Graves' may flare postpartum

WHEN TO SEEK URGENT ATTENTION:
- Thyroid storm: High fever (>101.5°F), severe tachycardia (>140 bpm), confusion, abdominal pain
  [SEEK EMERGENCY CARE IMMEDIATELY]
- Signs of infection while on antithyroid drugs: Fever, sore throat, mouth ulcers (agranulocytosis)
- Severe palpitations or chest pain
- Difficulty breathing

WHEN TO SEE DOCTOR:
- Starting treatment: Baseline and 6-8 weeks after to assess response
- Every 3-6 months initially on antithyroid drugs
- After I-131 or surgery: 4-6 weeks after to check for hypothyroidism development
- Annually once stable

TIMELINE:
- Antithyroid drugs: 1-2 weeks initial effect, 6-8 weeks full effect
- Beta-blockers: Within hours to days (symptom relief)
- Radioactive iodine: 4-6 weeks noticeable improvement, 10-12 weeks near maximum
- Surgery: Immediate improvement after procedure

TREATMENT CESSATION (Antithyroid drugs):
- Possible after 1-2 years: Remission rate 20-40%
- Relapse: If occurs, usually within first year after stopping
- Consider I-131 after one drug failure
- Some stay on antithyroid drugs for 5-10 years (prolonging remission possible)

COMPLICATIONS:
- Thyroid storm: Medical emergency, mortality high
- Atrial fibrillation: Increases stroke risk
- Heart disease: From chronic tachycardia
- Bone loss: Leading to osteoporosis
- Hypothyroidism: Develops in 90%+ after I-131

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Children: Can develop Graves'; similar treatment approach
- Elderly: Often more cardiac complications; careful beta-blocker use
- Pregnancy: Requires specialist management (endocrinologist)
- Breastfeeding: Both PTU and methimazole transfer to milk (small amounts; generally considered safe)
