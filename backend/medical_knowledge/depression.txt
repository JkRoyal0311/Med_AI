DISEASE: Depression (Major Depressive Disorder)
CATEGORY: Psychiatric / Mental Health
ICD-10: F32, F33
SEVERITY: Chronic — can be disabling, suicide risk in severe cases

DESCRIPTION:
Depression is a common mental health disorder characterized by persistent sadness, loss of interest
in activities, and various physical and cognitive changes. Affects mood, thoughts, and physical health.
Caused by combination of genetics, brain chemistry (neurotransmitters), and life events. Different from
normal sadness — persistent for at least 2 weeks and interferes with daily functioning.

TYPES:
- Major Depressive Disorder (MDD): Single or recurrent episodes, most common
- Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia): Chronic, milder but long-lasting (≥2 years)
- Seasonal Affective Disorder: Occurs seasonally (winter severity)
- Postpartum depression: Occurs after childbirth

RISK FACTORS:
- Family history of depression
- Previous depressive episodes
- Chronic medical conditions
- Chronic stress or major life events (loss, trauma)
- Social isolation
- Substance abuse (alcohol, drugs)
- Brain chemistry imbalance
- Hormonal changes (menstrual, menopause)
- Certain medications (beta-blockers, some anticonvulsants)

SYMPTOMS (At least 5 for 2 weeks for diagnosis):

MOOD/EMOTIONAL:
- Persistent sadness or empty feeling
- Loss of interest/pleasure in activities (anhedonia)
- Difficulty experiencing any joy
- Irritability and anger (especially in men)
- Crying spells (or inability to cry despite sadness)
- Hopelessness and helplessness
- Guilt and worthlessness feelings
- Suicidal thoughts or death preoccupation

COGNITIVE:
- Difficulty concentrating and making decisions
- Indecisiveness
- Memory problems
- Negative thinking patterns
- Rumination (dwelling on negative thoughts)
- Difficulty organizing thoughts

PHYSICAL:
- Fatigue and low energy (despite adequate sleep)
- Sleep disturbance: Insomnia or hypersomnia (sleeping too much)
- Appetite changes: Increased or decreased
- Weight gain or loss (beyond 5% body weight)
- Physical aches and pains (back, head, muscles)
- Digestive problems
- Reduced libido and sexual dysfunction
- Psychomotor retardation: Slow movements, slurred speech
- Agitation: Restlessness, inability to sit still

SEVERITY:
- Mild: Few symptoms beyond minimum, minimal impact
- Moderate: Multiple symptoms, noticeable impact on functioning
- Severe: Many symptoms, significantly impaired, possible psychotic features
- With psychotic features: Hallucinations or delusions (lose touch with reality)
- With suicidal ideation: Thought of harming self

MEDICATIONS USED:

First-line antidepressants (SSRIs — Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors):
1. SERTRALINE
   - Dosage: 50 mg once daily, increase to 100-200 mg
   - Onset: 4-6 weeks for full effect
   - Brand names: Zoloft, Setra
   - Generally well-tolerated
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Sexual dysfunction (delayed orgasm, erectile dysfunction), sleep disturbance, nausea (usually transient)

2. FLUOXETINE
   - Dosage: 20-40 mg once daily (or split dose)
   - Long half-life (takes weeks to clear)
   - Brand names: Prozac, Fluoxat
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Same as sertraline, activating (take morning for insomnia-prone)

3. ESCITALOPRAM
   - Dosage: 10-20 mg once daily
   - Most selective SSRI for serotonin (fewer side effects)
   - Brand names: Lexapro, Esitalo
   - Good efficacy, good tolerability

4. PAROXETINE
   - Dosage: 20-40 mg once daily
   - More sedating (good if insomnia)
   - Brand names: Paxil, Parotin
   - Slightly higher withdrawal symptoms if stopped abruptly

Second-line antidepressants (Different mechanisms):
5. VENLAFAXINE (SNRI — Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitor)
   - Dosage: 75-150 mg once daily (or split)
   - For moderate-severe depression
   - Also helps with anxiety
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Like SSRIs, plus slight blood pressure increase (monitor)
   - Brand names: Effexor, Venlift

6. BUPROPION (Norepinephrine-Dopamine Reuptake Inhibitor)
   - Dosage: 150-300 mg daily
   - Activating (energizing), helpful with fatigue
   - DOES NOT cause sexual dysfunction (opposite problem: increased libido)
   - CAUTION: Increases seizure risk (avoid if seizure disorder)
   - Brand names: Wellbutrin, Bupropion

7. TRICYCLIC ANTIDEPRESSANTS (OLDER CLASS)
   - Amitriptyline: 25-100 mg at night
   - Imipramine, Nortriptyline: Similar dosing
   - Effective but more side effects than SSRIs
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, orthostatic hypotension, weight gain
   - ADVANTAGE: Overdose can be dangerous (avoid in suicidal patients)
   - Still used for depression with chronic pain or insomnia

AUGMENTATION STRATEGIES (If not responding):
8. ANTIPSYCHOTICS (Low-dose adjunct)
   - Aripiprazole 2-5 mg daily added to SSRI
   - Quetiapine 50-100 mg nightly
   - Improves response in 30-40% of treatment-resistant cases
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Weight gain, metabolic syndrome risk

9. THYROID HORMONE (Liothyronine)
   - 25 mcg daily added to antidepressant
   - Augments antidepressant effect

ANXIOLYTICS (For anxiety coexisting with depression):
10. BENZODIAZEPINES (Short-term only)
    - Lorazepam 0.5-1 mg twice daily
    - For acute anxiety while antidepressant takes effect
    - CAUTION: Risk of dependence; use ≤2-4 weeks
    - Long-term benzodiazepines contraindicated in depression

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID:
- Alcohol: Worsens depression, interacts with antidepressants
- Stimulants: May worsen anxiety (if present)
- Corticosteroids: Can trigger depression with long-term use

MEDICATIONS THAT MAY CAUSE DEPRESSION (Discuss alternatives):
- Beta-blockers: Especially propranolol
- Isotretinoin (for acne): High suicide risk
- Interferon-alpha
- Some anticonvulsants: Phenobarbital
- Hormonal contraceptives: In susceptible women

THERAPY (ESSENTIAL COMPONENT):
- Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): Most evidence-based
- Interpersonal Therapy (IPT): Effective for depression
- Psychodynamic therapy: Addresses underlying conflicts
- Combination therapy (medication + therapy): Most effective for moderate-severe

FOODS AND LIFESTYLE:

FOODS THAT SUPPORT MOOD:
- Omega-3 rich: Fish (salmon), walnuts, flaxseed (reduces depression risk)
- Complex carbohydrates: Brown rice, oats, whole wheat (increases serotonin)
- Protein with tryptophan: Chicken, turkey, eggs, cheese, nuts (serotonin precursor)
- Folate-rich: Green leafy vegetables, asparagus, beans (low folate linked to depression)
- Vitamin D sources: Fish, egg yolks, mushrooms (deficiency linked to depression)
- Fermented foods: Yogurt, kimchi (gut-brain axis, probiotics)
- Dark chocolate: Phenylethylamine, magnesium (mood boosters)
- Leafy greens / Palak, methi: Folate, vitamins
- Berries: Antioxidants
- Legumes / Dal: Folate, protein
- Turmeric / Haldi: Curcumin shows antidepressant properties in studies

FOODS/SUBSTANCES TO AVOID:
- Alcohol: Worsens depression, impairs sleep
- Excess caffeine: Can increase anxiety if present
- Processed foods: Nutrient-poor, may worsen mood
- Sugar excess: Causes energy crashes, mood dips
- Fried foods: Pro-inflammatory

SUPPLEMENTS THAT MAY HELP:
- Vitamin D: 1000-4000 IU daily (check levels; deficiency common in depression)
- Omega-3: 1000-2000 mg daily EPA (evidence for depression)
- B-complex vitamins: Support neurotransmitter synthesis
- Magnesium: 300-400 mg daily (mood support)
- Probiotics: Gut-brain axis influence
- SAM-e: 400-1600 mg daily (some evidence)
- 5-HTP: 50-100 mg daily (precursor to serotonin)
- NOTE: Some supplements interact with antidepressants (especially St. John's Wort), discuss with doctor

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS (CRITICAL):
- Aerobic exercise: 30 min, 5 days/week (as effective as some antidepressants for mild depression)
- Strength training: 2-3 times weekly
- Sleep: 7-9 hours, consistent schedule (insomnia worsens depression)
- Sunlight exposure: At least 30 minutes daily (vitamin D, mood improvement)
- Social contact: Maintain relationships (isolation worsens depression)
- Purpose and meaning: Engage in meaningful activities
- Stress management: Yoga, meditation
- Limit social media: Reduces mood disturbance
- Limit news consumption: Particularly negative news reduces mood
- Structure: Maintain daily routine (depression disrupts structure)
- Self-compassion: Reduce self-criticism and perfectionism

SUICIDE RISK ASSESSMENT:
High-risk indicators:
- Suicidal thoughts with plan and means
- Recent loss or major life stressor
- Social isolation
- Substance abuse
- Previous suicide attempts
- Access to means (firearms, medications)

IMMEDIATE ACTION IF SUICIDAL:
- CALL EMERGENCY SERVICES (112 in India) or go to emergency room
- Tell someone: Family, friend, healthcare provider
- DO NOT leave person alone if actively suicidal
- Ask directly: "Are you thinking of harming yourself?" (asking doesn't increase risk)
- Remove means: Secure medications, firearms
- Crisis line: Aasra (9820466726), iCall (9152987821), helpline (Vandrevala Foundation)

WHEN TO SEEK URGENT ATTENTION:
- Suicidal thoughts or self-harm urges
- Severe depression worsening despite medication
- Psychotic symptoms (hearing voices, paranoia)
- Not eating or drinking for extended period
- Complete inability to carry out daily tasks

WHEN TO SEEK DOCTOR APPOINTMENT:
- Starting antidepressant: 4-6 week follow-up to assess effect
- Side effects from medication: May need adjustment or alternative
- After 6-8 weeks If no improvement: May need different medication or higher dose
- Regularly on medication: Monitor every 3-6 months

TIMELINE OF TREATMENT:
- First 1-2 weeks: Sleep may improve, energy may increase slightly
- Weeks 2-4: Anxiety may improve (if present)
- Weeks 4-8: Mood and motivation gradual improvement
- Weeks 8-12: Full effect usually seen
- DON'T STOP: If not working by week 4, discuss adjustments (don't jump between medications too rapidly)

DISCONTINUATION:
- Do NOT stop abruptly: Causes withdrawal symptoms (flu-like, dizziness, brain zaps)
- Taper gradually: Reduce over 4-6 weeks
- Plan with doctor: When to stop (ideally when mood stable for 6-12 months)
- Risk of relapse: 40-50% relapse if stop after first episode; higher if previous episodes

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Pregnancy: Some antidepressants safer than others; untreated depression also risky; discuss with OB
- Breastfeeding: Most SSRIs safe; sertraline and paroxetine have least infant exposure
- Elderly: More sensitive to side effects; lower doses; higher falls risk
- Children/adolescents: Increased suicidal ideation risk in early weeks of SSRI; close monitoring
- Chronic medical conditions: Depression impacts diabetes, heart disease (treat both)
- Substance abuse: Higher depression rates; address both for success
