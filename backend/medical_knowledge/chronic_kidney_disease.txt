DISEASE: Kidney Disease (Chronic Kidney Disease — CKD)
CATEGORY: Nephrological / Metabolic
ICD-10: N18
SEVERITY: Stage 1-5; progressive if untreated

DESCRIPTION:
Chronic kidney disease is gradual loss of kidney function over months to years. Kidneys filter
waste products and excess water to form urine. When damaged, waste accumulates in blood (uremia).
Can progress to end-stage renal disease (ESRD) requiring dialysis or transplant. Major risk factors:
diabetes (primary), hypertension, chronic glomerulonephritis.

STAGES:
- Stage 1: eGFR ≥90 (normal kidney function, but protein/cells in urine)
- Stage 2: eGFR 60-89 (mild loss)
- Stage 3a: eGFR 45-59 (mild-moderate loss)
- Stage 3b: eGFR 30-44 (moderate-severe loss)
- Stage 4: eGFR 15-29 (severe loss)
- Stage 5: eGFR <15 (kidney failure, dialysis needed)

EGFR: Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate (measure of kidney function)

CAUSES:

PRIMARY (Direct kidney disease):
- Glomerulonephritis: Inflammation of glomeruli (filtering units)
- IgA nephropathy: Antibody accumulation in glomeruli
- Polycystic kidney disease: Genetic cysts in kidneys
- Lupus nephritis: SLE involving kidneys

SECONDARY (Systemic diseases):
- Diabetes: Most common cause (~36% of ESRD)
- Hypertension: Second most common (~25%)
- Chronic glomerulonephritis

RISK FACTORS:
- Diabetes (Type 1 or 2)
- Hypertension (HTN)
- Age: >60 years
- Family history of CKD
- Chronic lung disease (smoking)
- Obesity
- Chronic NSAIDs use
- Certain medications: ACE inhibitors (when not from diabetes)
- Autoimmune disease (lupus, rheumatoid)
- Frequent infections

SYMPTOMS:

EARLY STAGES (1-3):
- Often asymptomatic: Detected only by lab tests
- Mild fatigue
- Mild ankle swelling (can be other causes)

LATE STAGES (4-5):
- Fatigue and weakness: Anemia from reduced erythropoietin
- Nausea and vomiting: From uremia
- Loss of appetite
- Metallic taste in mouth
- Brain fog and difficulty concentrating: Uremia effects
- Muscle cramps: From electrolyte imbalances
- Bone pain (renal osteodystrophy): From calcium/phosphorus imbalance
- Itching: Uremic pruritus
- Edema: Swelling of feet, ankles, face (from fluid retention)
- Hypertension: Worsening or new onset
- Shortness of breath: From fluid overload or anemia
- Headaches
- Restless leg syndrome
- Decreased urine output: In advanced disease

DIAGNOSIS:
- eGFR: Calculated from serum creatinine
- Serum creatinine: Higher levels = worse function
- Blood urine nitrogen (BUN): Elevated in renal failure
- Urinalysis: Protein (proteinuria), RBCs, casts
- Serum electrolytes: Potassium, phosphorus, calcium
- Imaging: Ultrasound or CT (assess kidney size, exclude obstruction)
- Biopsy: If etiology unclear

MEDICATIONS USED:

BLOOD PRESSURE CONTROL (Most important):
1. ACE INHIBITORS (First-line)
   - Lisinopril, Enalapril, Ramipril
   - Dosage: Variable (start low, titrate up)
   - PROTECTS kidneys (reduces proteinuria, slows CKD progression)
   - Causes dry cough in 10-15%
   - AVOID if potassium >5.5 (hyperkalemia risk)
   - CAUTION: Monitor renal function (initial Cr rise expected)

2. ARB (ANGIOTENSIN RECEPTOR BLOCKERS) — Alternative to ACE-I
   - Losartan, Telmisartan, Valsartan
   - Similar renal protection as ACE-I
   - No cough side effect
   - Interchange: Can't use both ACE-I and ARB together

3. CALCIUM CHANNEL BLOCKERS
   - Amlodipine, Diltiazem
   - BP control but less renal protection
   - Often combined with ACE-I/ARB

4. THIAZIDE DIURETICS
   - Hydrochlorothiazide: 25 mg daily
   - CAUTION in advanced CKD (ineffective if eGFR <30)

ANEMIA TREATMENT:
5. ERYTHROPOIETIN STIMULATING AGENTS (ESAs)
   - Darbepoetin: 200-300 mcg IV weekly
   - For hemoglobin <10 g/dL
   - Goal hgb: 10-11.5 g/dL (not higher — complications)
   - Usually given with dialysis

6. IRON SUPPLEMENTATION
   - Iron supplement if iron-deficient
   - Oral or IV (oral poorly absorbed in renal failure)

PHOSPHATE AND CALCIUM MANAGEMENT:
7. PHOSPHATE BINDERS
   - Calcium acetate: 667 mg three times daily with meals
   - Sevelamer: Non-calcium alternative
   - Reduce blood phosphate (prevents bone disease)

8. ACT (If hypocalcemia):
   - Calcitriol (active vitamin D): 0.25 mcg twice daily
   - Corrects low calcium, reduces PTH

MINERAL SUPPLEMENTS (If deficient):
9. POTASSIUM RESTRICTION (Don't supplement):
   - Dangerous in CKD (hyperkalemia risk)
   - Restrict dietary potassium

10. PHOSPHORUS RESTRICTION (Dietary)

BLOOD GLUCOSE CONTROL (If diabetic):
11. ANTIDIABETIC AGENTS
    - SGLT2 inhibitors: Empagliflozin, Dapagliflozin
      * Reduce glucose, slow CKD progression (newer benefit)
      * Preferred in diabetic CKD
    - Metformin: CAUTION if eGFR <30 (lactic acidosis risk)
    - Insulin: Adjustment needed (reduced clearance)

OTHER:
12. LIPID-LOWERING
    - Statins: Cardiovascular protection in CKD
    - High-dose often needed

13. BETA-BLOCKERS
    - If hypertension + heart disease
    - Some clearance impairment (dose adjustment needed)

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID/ADJUST DOSING:
- NSAIDs: Can worsen kidney function, avoid or minimize
- Aminoglycosides: Nephrotoxic (if infection, use alternatives)
- Contrast dye: Risk of contrast-induced nephropathy
- Amphotericin B: Nephrotoxic
- Cisplatin: Chemotherapy agents (dialysis needed)
- COX-2 inhibitors: Avoid
- Metformin: If eGFR <30
- ACE-I/ARB: Temporary rise in creatinine (usually acceptable)
- Trimethoprim: Increases creatinine (usually reversible)

FOODS TO EAT:
- Vegetables (low potassium): Cabbage, carrots, celery, cucumber
- Fruits (low potassium): Apple, berries, pear (avoid banana, orange)
- Whole grains (controlled portions): Brown rice limited
- Lean proteins (limit): Chicken, fish (portions not quantity)
- Low-phosphorus: Vegetables, fruits, rice
- Unsalted: Avoid salt, cooking with salt
- Low-sodium overall: <2g sodium/day
- Choose fresh: Avoid canned, processed (high sodium, phosphorus)

FOODS TO STRICTLY AVOID:
- High potassium: Banana, orange, tomato, potato, spinach (large amounts)
- High phosphorus: Dairy excess, processed cheeses, nuts
- High sodium: Pickles, papads, processed meat, canned foods
- Excess protein: Limits to individual tolerance (usually 0.8 g/kg/day)
  * Too much worsens uremia
- Alcohol: If advanced CKD

FLUIDS:
- Fluid restriction: In advanced CKD (stages 4-5)
- Output + insensible loss + 500 mL = daily limit
- Monitor: Weight gain (indicates fluid overload)

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:
- Blood pressure control: Goal <120/80 (especially if diabetic)
- Smoking cessation: Smoking accelerates CKD
- Exercise: Regular moderate activity helps BP, weight, metabolism
- Weight management: Obesity worsens CKD
- Stress reduction: Yoga, meditation help BP

TREATMENT BY STAGE:

Stage 1-2: Monitor, control HTN/diabetes
Stage 3: Intensive BP/glucose control, prepare for dialysis education
Stage 4: Pre-dialysis counseling, vascular access planning, dietary counseling
Stage 5: Dialysis or transplant

DIALYSIS:
- Hemodialysis: 3-4 hours, 3 times/week (in-center)
- Peritoneal dialysis: Continuous, multiple daily exchanges (home-based)
- Kidney transplant: Best option, if donor available

MONITORING:
- Quarterly: Cr, BUN, electrolytes initially, then per stage
- Annual: Lipid profile, glucose (if diabetic)
- Ultrasound: Assess kidney size, obstruction
- DEXA scan: Bone density assessment
- Cardiovascular assessment: High CV risk

WHEN TO SEEK URGENT ATTENTION:
- Shortness of breath: Possible pulmonary edema
- Severe potassium elevation: Can cause arrhythmias
- Encephalopathy: Confusion, seizures (uremia, hypertensive emergency)

WHEN TO SEE DOCTOR:
- Annual screening: If diabetes or hypertension
- Change in kidney function: Detected on lab tests
- New symptoms: Fatigue, edema, etc.
- Before starting new medications: Check kidney-safe options, dosing

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Diabetes + CKD: Highest priority for aggressive control
- Pregnancy: Complicates management; close monitoring needed
- Children: Different staging, growth considerations
- Elderly: Age-related creatinine rise normal; calculate eGFR carefully
- African ancestry: Higher CKD incidence (genetic/social factors)
- Medication interactions: Many drugs accumulate in renal failure
