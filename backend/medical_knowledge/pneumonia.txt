DISEASE: Pneumonia (Bacterial)
CATEGORY: Infectious / Pulmonary
ICD-10: J13-J18
SEVERITY: Serious — can be life-threatening if untreated

DESCRIPTION:
Pneumonia is infection of the lung alveoli (air sacs) causing inflammation and fluid/pus accumulation.
Prevents oxygen exchange. Can be caused by bacteria (most common), viruses, or fungi. Severity ranges from
"walking pneumonia" (mild, outpatient) to severe requiring hospitalization. Most common cause of death
from infectious disease in India.

RISK FACTORS:
- Age: >65 years or <5 years
- Smoking: Damages lung defenses
- Chronic lung disease: COPD, asthma
- Chronic heart disease
- Diabetes: Impaired immune response
- Immunosuppression: HIV/AIDS, medications
- Recent hospitalization
- Aspiration: From swallowing difficulties, unconsciousness
- Alcoholism
- Malnutrition

COMMON BACTERIA:
- Streptococcus pneumoniae: Most common community-acquired
- Haemophilus influenzae
- Legionella: From contaminated water
- Staphylococcus aureus: Hospital-acquired often

SYMPTOMS:
RESPIRATORY:
- Cough: Initially dry, becomes productive with phlegm
- Sputum: Often green, yellow, or rust-colored
- Shortness of breath: Even at rest in severe cases
- Rapid breathing: >20 breaths/minute
- Chest pain: When breathing or coughing
- Wheezing or crackles on examination

SYSTEMIC:
- Fever: Usually >101°F / 38.3°C
- Chills and sweating
- Severe fatigue and weakness
- Malaise: General feeling of illness
- Loss of appetite
- Myalgia: Muscle aches
- Headache

PROGRESSION:
- May start as upper respiratory infection
- Progresses to lower respiratory symptoms
- Untreated: Can progress to sepsis, respiratory failure

SEVERITY:

MILD (Outpatient treatment possible):
- Alert, cooperative
- Respiratory rate <30/min
- Oxygen sat >90% on room air
- Systolic BP >90 mmHg

SEVERE (Hospitalization):
- Respiratory rate >30/min
- Altered mental status
- Shock or sepsis signs
- Respiratory failure

DIAGNOSIS:
- Chest X-ray: Shows infiltrates (consolidation)
- Sputum culture: Identifies organism, guides antibiotics
- Blood culture: If febrile (prognostic)
- Complete blood count: WBC elevation
- Lactate: Elevated in severe pneumonia (prognostic)
- COVID-19/Influenza testing: If acute cough/fever (differentiate from viral)

MEDICATIONS USED:

FIRST-LINE ANTIBIOTICS (Community-acquired pneumonia):
1. AMOXICILLIN-CLAVULANATE
   - Dosage: 500/125 mg three times daily for 7 days (oral)
   - For outpatient mild-moderate pneumonia
   - Brand names: Augmentin
   - Covers pneumococcus and H. influenzae

2. FLUOROQUINOLONES (Respiratory)
   - Levofloxacin: 750 mg once daily for 5 days (IV or oral)
   - Moxifloxacin: 400 mg once daily for 5 days
   - Excellent lung penetration
   - For community-acquired if beta-lactam allergy or resistant organism suspected
   - Brand names: Levaquin, Avelox

3. AZITHROMYCIN (macrolide)
   - Dosage: 500 mg day 1, then 250 mg daily for 4 days
   - Often combined with amoxicillin
   - For atypical organisms (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia)

SEVERE PNEUMONIA (Hospitalization):
4. CEFTRIAXONE (3rd generation cephalosporin)
   - Dosage: 1-2 grams IV every 12 hours
   - Broad spectrum, excellent lung penetration
   - Added macrolide (azithromycin) for atypical coverage
   - Brand names: Rocephin

5. PIPERACILLIN-TAZOBACTAM
   - Dosage: 4.5-gram dose IV every 6-8 hours
   - For severe or hospital-acquired pneumonia
   - Broader spectrum, covers pseudomonas

SEPTIC SHOCK:
6. Combination broad-spectrum: Often vancomycin + cephalosporin + fluoroquinolone

SUPPORTIVE CARE:
7. OXYGEN therapy: To maintain oxygen saturation >90%
8. Mechanical ventilation: If respiratory failure
9. Fluids: IV hydration
10. Fever management: Paracetamol (aspirin avoided — risk of fluid worsening)

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID:
- Antihistamines: Can dry secretions (worsen pneumonia)
- Cough suppressants: Suppress cough needed to clear secretions (avoid)

FOODS TO EAT (Support immune function):
- Protein: Fish, chicken, eggs, legumes / Dal (rebuild strength)
- Citrus fruits: Orange, lemon, Mosambi (vitamin C, immune support)
- Ginger / Adrak tea: Anti-inflammatory
- Garlic / Lahsun: Antimicrobial
- Turmeric / Haldi milk: Anti-inflammatory
- Honey / Shahad: Soothing for cough
- Leafy greens / Palak, methi: Nutrients
- Whole grains: Brown rice (energy)
- Nuts and seeds: Magnesium, vitamins
- Warm broths: Chicken or vegetable broth (hydrating, comforting)
- Bean soup: High protein, easy to digest

FOODS TO AVOID:
- Very cold foods: Can increase congestion
- Excess dairy: May increase mucus (controversial)
- Fried foods: Difficult to digest when ill
- Spicy foods: Initially irritating
- Alcohol: Impairs immunity, increases dehydration

HYDRATION:
- Drink plenty of fluids: 2-3 liters daily
- Warm fluids preferred: Help loosen secretions
- Avoid caffeine excess: Dehydrating

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:
- Rest: Critical for recovery; sleep as much as possible
- Fresh air: But avoid cold drafts
- Humidified environment: Moisture helps breathing
- Avoid smoking: Impairs recovery
- Avoid exposure to others: Prevent spread (contagious first 24-48 hours after antibiotics)
- Elevation: Sleep with head elevated 30-45 degrees (aids breathing)
- Monitor: Check temperature, breathing rate

PROPHYLAXIS (Prevention):
- Pneumococcal vaccine: PCV13, then PPSV23 (high-risk patients)
- Influenza vaccine: Annual (prevents secondary bacterial pneumonia)
- Smoking cessation: Reduces risk significantly
- Avoid aspiration: Proper swallowing, avoid lying down while eating

WHEN TO SEEK URGENT ATTENTION:
- Severe shortness of breath: Difficulty speaking in sentences
- Cyanosis: Blue lips or fingertips
- Confusion or altered mental status
- Chest pain
- High fever not improving with antibiotics after 48-72 hours
- Bloody sputum: May indicate lung tissue damage

WHEN TO SEE DOCTOR:
- Cough >2 weeks (not improving)
- Signs of pneumonia: Fever, cough with productive sputum
- At-risk patients: Elderly, chronic disease, immunocompromised
- Not improving on antibiotics: May need different agent or imaging
- Complications suspected: Empyema (pus in space around lungs), abscess

COMPLICATIONS:
- Sepsis: Bacteria in bloodstream (life-threatening)
- Respiratory failure: Requiring mechanical ventilation
- Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS): Severe respiratory failure
- Empyema: Pus collection around lungs (may need drainage)
- Lung abscess: Cavity with pus inside lung
- Aspiration pneumonia: From inhalation of vomit/food
- Secondary pneumonia: Fungal (in immunocompromised)

TREATMENT OUTCOMES:
- Mild pneumonia: Often improves in 1-2 weeks with antibiotics
- Severe pneumonia: May require 2-4 weeks hospitalization, ICU
- Mortality: 5-15% in hospitalized patients, varies by age/severity/organism

MONITORING DURING TREATMENT:
- Clinical improvement: Fever resolves, energy increases (24-48 hours typical)
- If not improving: Redo chest X-ray, reassess antibiotics
- Repeat CXR at end of treatment: Some patients need repeat imaging
- Follow-up: Ensure complete resolution (prevent relapse)

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Elderly: Often presents with atypical symptoms (confusion, no fever)
- Diabetes + pneumonia: Complications higher, hospitalization often needed
- COPD + pneumonia: More serious, may need stronger antibiotics
- Pregnancy: Treatment tailored to safe medications
- Children: Different treatment, vaccinations important
- Aspiration pneumonia: Often mixed organisms, may include anaerobes
- Healthcare-associated pneumonia: Different organisms (antibiotic coverage adjusted)
- Immunocompromised: May need additional antimicrobials (fungi, viruses)
