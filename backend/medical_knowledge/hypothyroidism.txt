DISEASE: Hypothyroidism (Underactive Thyroid)
CATEGORY: Endocrine
ICD-10: E03
SEVERITY: Chronic — manageable with daily medication

DESCRIPTION:
Hypothyroidism occurs when the thyroid gland does not produce enough thyroid hormones (T3 and T4). 
This slows metabolism and affects nearly every body function. Most common in women and increases with age.
Hashimoto's thyroiditis is the most common cause in iodine-sufficient areas.

SYMPTOMS:
- Fatigue and weakness (most common)
- Weight gain despite normal appetite
- Bradycardia (slow heart rate, below 60 bpm)
- Cold intolerance — feeling cold more than others
- Dry skin and brittle hair
- Hair loss (alopecia)
- Constipation (slowed gut motility)
- Depression and mood changes
- Slow speech and hoarseness
- Elevated cholesterol levels
- Menstrual irregularities in women
- Memory problems and concentration issues ("brain fog")
- Facial puffiness and swelling (myxedema in severe cases)

MEDICATIONS USED:
1. LEVOTHYROXINE (Synthetic T4)
   - First-line treatment for hypothyroidism
   - Brand names: Synthroid, Thyroxine, Eltroxin
   - Take on empty stomach (30-60 minutes before food)
   - Requires periodic TSH testing to adjust dose
   - Once daily dosing
   - AVOID taking with: calcium supplements, iron, vitamins with iron
   
2. LIOTHYRONINE (Synthetic T3)
   - Faster acting than levothyroxine
   - Used when levothyroxine alone is insufficient
   - Requires careful monitoring
   - More expensive

3. DESICCATED THYROID
   - Natural extract, less commonly used
   - Variable hormone content
   - Less predictable than synthetic hormones

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID OR USE WITH CAUTION:
- Excess iodine (may paradoxically worsen hypothyroidism in autoimmune thyroiditis)
- Amiodarone (heart medication): High iodine content
- Lithium (psychiatric medication): Destroys thyroid function
- Estrogen supplements (in women): May increase levothyroxine requirements
- Interferon and Interleukin treatments: Can worsen autoimmune thyroiditis

FOODS TO EAT:
- Seafood and seaweed / Nori: Rich in iodine (thyroid requires iodine)
- Eggs / Ande: Complete protein with selenium (helps convert T4 to T3)
- Brazil nuts: Highest natural source of selenium
- Shellfish / Jhinga: Iodine + selenium + zinc for thyroid function
- Whole grains (brown rice, oats): B vitamins support thyroid metabolism
- Legumes / Dal: Iodine + iron + protein
- Dairy milk / Dahi: Iodine + vitamin D + calcium
- Spinach / Palak: Iron + magnesium (though goitrogenic, OK in small amounts)
- Nuts and seeds: Zinc and selenium
- Chicken / Murgh: Protein + B vitamins + selenium

FOODS TO LIMIT (Goitrogenic — interfere with iodine absorption):
- Cruciferous vegetables IF RAW and in excess: Raw broccoli, cabbage, cauliflower, brussels sprouts
  (Cooking inactivates goitrogens, so cooked vegetables are fine)
- Soy products in excess: Tofu, soy milk, soy sauce
- Cassava: High goitrogen content
- Pine nuts: Can increase goitrogen compounds
- NOTE: Small amounts of these foods are fine; problem is excessive consumption

DANGEROUS INTERACTIONS:
- Levothyroxine + Metformin: May reduce levothyroxine absorption
- Levothyroxine + Proton Pump Inhibitors (for acid reflux): Reduce levothyroxine absorption
- Levothyroxine + Antacids with aluminum or magnesium: Reduce absorption
- Levothyroxine + Cholestyramine (cholesterol medication): Reduce absorption

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Levothyroxine must be taken on an empty stomach
- Wait 4 hours after levothyroxine before taking supplements or other medications
- Pregnancy: Hypothyroid women need higher doses during pregnancy (TSH should be 0.5-2.5)
- Menopause: Estrogen changes may require dose adjustments
- Periodic TSH monitoring: Check every 6-8 weeks after dose change, then annually
