DISEASE: Fatty Liver Disease (Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease — NAFLD)
CATEGORY: Hepatological / Metabolic
ICD-10: K76.0
SEVERITY: Mild-Severe; can progress to cirrhosis

DESCRIPTION:
NAFLD is excessive fat accumulation in liver (≥5-10% liver weight) without significant alcohol consumption.
Common in developed and developing countries (affects up to 30% globally). Often associated with obesity,
diabetes, metabolic syndrome. Most have benign course, but 10-20% progress to cirrhosis. Increasing cause
of liver transplantation need.

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY:
- Insulin resistance: Primary mechanism leading to hepatic fat accumulation
- Inflammation: Two-hit hypothesis (fat + inflammatory triggers)
- Progressive fibrosis in some patients

RISK FACTORS:
- Obesity: BMI >30
- Type 2 diabetes: 50% overlap
- Metabolic syndrome features:
  * Abdominal obesity
  * Hypertension
  * Dyslipidemia (high triglycerides, low HDL)
  * Fasting glucose dysregulation
- Sedentary lifestyle
- High carbohydrate/fructose diet
- Excessive alcohol (though called "non-alcoholic")
- Genetic factors
- Some medications: Corticosteroids, methotrexate, certain antiretrovirals

SYMPTOMS:

EARLY (NAFL — simple steatosis):
- Often asymptomatic: Found incidentally on imaging
- Mild fatigue
- Mild discomfort in right upper quadrant (vague)

ADVANCED (NASH — nonalcoholic steatohepatitis):
- Fatigue: More pronounced
- Abdominal discomfort or pain (right upper quadrant)
- Hepatomegaly: Enlarged liver (palpable below ribs)
- Jaundice: If advanced cirrhosis
- Spider angiomas: if cirrhosis develops
- Portal hypertension signs: Ascites, varices

CIRRHOSIS SIGNS:
- Ascites: Abdominal fluid, distension
- Splenomegaly: Enlarged spleen
- Esophageal varices: Bleeding from distended veins
- Hepatic encephalopathy: Brain dysfunction from ammonia
- Coagulopathy: Easy bruising, bleeding

DIAGNOSIS:
- Elevated transaminases: ALT more specific than AST
- Ultrasound: Increased echogenicity (fatty infiltration)
- CT or MRI: More sensitive for fat, can assess fibrosis
- FibroScan: Ultrasound-based elastography (assess fibrosis stage)
- Liver biopsy: Gold standard but invasive (not routinely done)
- FIB-4 score: Blood-based fibrosis assessment (AST, platelet count, age)
- APRI score: Another non-invasive fibrosis assessment

STAGING:
- F0: No fibrosis (simple steatosis)
- F1: Mild fibrosis
- F2: Moderate fibrosis
- F3: Advanced fibrosis
- F4: Cirrhosis

MEDICATIONS USED:

NO FDA-APPROVED TREATMENT YET (Management is supportive):

For weight loss:
1. ORLISTAT
   - Dosage: 120 mg three times daily with meals
   - Inhibits fat absorption
   - 5-10% weight loss possible
   - SIDE EFFECTS: GI (oily stools)
   - Works adjunctive to diet

2. GLP-1 AGONISTS (For diabetes patients)
   - Semaglutide: 0.5-1 mg weekly
   - Better weight loss, cardiovascular benefits
   - Also improving NASH parameters in studies

For insulin resistance/metabolic issues:
3. METFORMIN
   - Dosage: 500-1000 mg twice daily
   - Improves insulin sensitivity
   - Modest effect on steatosis
   - Safe, cheap

4. PIOGLITAZONE (Thiazolidinedione)
   - Dosage: 15-30 mg daily
   - Improves insulin sensitivity, reduces hepatic inflammation
   - Evidence for NASH improvement
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Weight gain, bone loss risk
   - Used less due to side effects

For inflammation:
5. VITAMIN E
   - Dosage: 800 IU daily (if non-diabetic, biopsy-proven NASH)
   - Antioxidant, reduces inflammation
   - No benefit if simple steatosis or diabetic NASH
   - Long-term safety unclear
   - May increase mortality in diabetics (controversial)

Investigational agents (not yet approved):
6. FXFR4 AGONISTS: Showing promise in studies

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID (Hepatotoxic):
- Acetaminophen: Excess (>2g/day)
- NSAIDs: Can worsen liver function (use minimally)
- Certain anticonvulsants
- Some antifungals
- High-dose statins: Check LFTs
- Methotrexate: Direct hepatotoxicity

FOODS TO EAT (Weight loss, metabolic support):
- Vegetables: Leafy greens, broccoli, cauliflower (low calorie, nutrients)
- Fruits: Berries, apple, pear (moderate)
- Whole grains: Limit refined carbs
- Legumes / Dal: Fiber, protein
- Fatty fish: Salmon, sardines (omega-3 anti-inflammatory)
- Nuts and seeds: Moderate (calories add up)
- Olive oil: For cooking (unsaturated fat)
- Turmeric / Haldi: Anti-inflammatory
- Ginger / Adrak: Anti-inflammatory
- Green tea: Contains EGCG (may help liver)
- Coffee: 3+ cups daily shows hepatoprotective effect (mechanisms unclear)
- Lean poultry / Murgh

FOODS TO STRICTLY AVOID:
- Refined carbohydrates: White bread, white rice, pasta (drive fat synthesis)
- Sugar and fructose: Soda, candy (direct fat storage)
- Alcohol: Even small amounts problematic in NAFLD (increases liver injury)
- High-fructose corn syrup: Soft drinks, processed foods
- Processed foods: High sugar, refined carbs, trans fats
- Saturated fats: Ghee, butter excess
- Trans fats: Packaged snacks, fried foods
- High-calorie foods: Anything contributing to obesity

BEVERAGES:
- Coffee: 3-4 cups daily (hepatoprotective)
- Green tea: 2-3 cups daily
- Water: Main beverage
- AVOID: Alcohol (even wine, 0 consumption recommended)
- AVOID: Sugary drinks, soft drinks

WEIGHT LOSS (MOST IMPORTANT):
- Target: 5-10% reduction improves steatosis
- 10-15% reduction improves inflammation
- >15% can reverse fibrosis
- Methods:
  * Caloric restriction: 500-1000 calories/day deficit
  * Exercise: 150-200 minutes weekly aerobic
  * Behavioral changes: Food diary, portion control

EXERCISE:
- Aerobic: 30 minutes, 5 days/week
- Resistance: 2-3 times weekly
- Even without weight loss: Exercise improves metabolic parameters

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:
- Smoking cessation: Improves outcomes
- Alcohol: Complete avoidance recommended
- Stress reduction: Lower stress improves metabolism
- Sleep: 7-8 hours nightly (sleep deprivation drives insulin resistance)
- Gut health: Probiotics showing some benefit (though not established)

MONITORING:
- LFTs: AST, ALT, ALP, bilirubin
- Metabolic markers: Glucose, triglycerides
- FIB-4 score: Annually (assess fibrosis progression)
- FibroScan: If advanced disease suspected
- Ultrasound: If advanced disease
- Platelet count: Decreases with fibrosis
- Hepatitis viral testing: To rule out coinfection

SCREENING FOR COMPLICATIONS (If cirrhosis):
- Endoscopy: Check for esophageal varices (bleeding risk)
- Ultrasound with Doppler: Check portal vein flow
- Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP): Tumor marker if cirrhosis

PROGNOSIS:
- Simple steatosis: Good prognosis, often stable
- NASH: 10-30% progress to cirrhosis
- Cirrhosis: 10-year mortality ~5% per year
- Liver transplant: Only option for decompensated cirrhosis

WHEN TO SEEK DOCTOR:
- Incidental finding of fatty liver: Needs evaluation
- Risk factors present: Screening
- Elevated LFTs: Evaluation for cause
- Right upper quadrant pain
- Signs of cirrhosis: Abdominal distension, jaundice
- Planning weight loss: Need monitoring

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Obesity: Weight loss is cornerstone, even 5-10% helps
- Diabetes: NAFLD in >50%; aggressive metabolic control essential
- Metabolic syndrome: Full component management needed
- Pregnancy: NAFLD usually improves postpartum
- Children: Pediatric NAFLD increasing (obesity in children)
- Bariatric surgery: Can improve NASH in severe obesity, but monitors liver closely
- Cardiovascular risk: NAFLD patients at higher CV risk (treat holistically)
