DISEASE: Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)
CATEGORY: Rheumatological / Autoimmune
ICD-10: M05
SEVERITY: Mild-Severe; progressive if untreated

DESCRIPTION:
Rheumatoid arthritis is chronic autoimmune disease causing joint inflammation, pain, stiffness. Affects
1% population, more common in women (3:1). Autoantibodies (RF, anti-CCP) attack synovial joint lining.
Can progressively destroy joint cartilage/bone. Extra-articular manifestations common (eyes, heart,
lungs, anemia). Early diagnosis and aggressive treatment prevent joint damage.

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY:
- Autoimmune attack: T and B cells target joint synovium
- Inflammatory cascade: Cytokines (TNF, IL-6, IL-17)
- Joint destruction: Pannus formation, cartilage loss
- Systemic inflammation: Elevates cardiovascular risk

RISK FACTORS:
- Female: 3:1 female preponderance
- Age: Typically 40-60 onset, can occur any age
- Genetics: HLA-DR4 gene associated
- Smoking: Significantly increases risk, worsens disease
- Occupational: Some exposures trigger
- Environmental: Infections implicated as triggers
- Stress: May exacerbate
- Obesity: Increases inflammation

SYMPTOMS:

JOINT INVOLVEMENT:
- Pain: Bilateral, symmetric ("glove and stocking" pattern)
  * Hands, wrists, feet most common (small joints)
  * Knees, ankles, shoulders can be involved
  * Gradual onset over weeks-months
- Stiffness: Morning stiffness lasting >1 hour (versus OA <30 min)
  * Severe stiffness early morning (hallmark)
  * Improves with activity (unlike OA)
- Swelling: Visible joint swelling
- Warmth: Joint feels warm to touch
- Limitation: Difficulty gripping, fine motor tasks
- Deformity: Later (swan neck, boutonniere deformities)

SYSTEMIC SYMPTOMS:
- Fatigue: Often significant
- Fever: Low-grade, intermittent
- Weight loss: From inflammation
- General malaise

EXTRA-ARTICULAR MANIFESTATIONS (30% of patients):
- Eyes: Scleritis, episcleritis (redness, pain)
- Heart: Myocarditis, pericarditis
- Lungs: Pulmonary nodules, interstitial lung disease
- Anemia: From chronic inflammation
- Felty syndrome: RA + hepatosplenomegaly + neutropenia (rare)
- Heart disease: Increased cardiovascular mortality (50% risk)

DIAGNOSIS:

SEROLOGY:
- Rheumatoid Factor (RF): Positive in 70-80% (not specific)
- Anti-CCP (anti-cyclic citrullinated peptide): Positive in 95% RA
  * More specific than RF
  * Predicts joint damage
  * Can be positive years before symptoms
- ANA: May be positive
- ESR (Erythrocyte sedimentation rate): Elevated in active disease
- CRP (C-reactive protein): Elevated

JOINT IMAGING:
- X-rays: Early normal, later shows joint space narrowing, bone erosions
- Ultrasound: Detects synovitis, smaller erosions than X-ray
- MRI: Most sensitive, detects early joint damage

CLINICAL CRITERIA:
- ACR/EULAR criteria: Joint symptoms + serology + inflammation markers

MEDICATIONS USED:

DISEASE-MODIFYING ANTIRHEUMATIC DRUGS (DMARDs) — Must use:

CONVENTIONAL SYNTHETIC (csDMARD):

1. METHOTREXATE
   - Dosage: 15-25 mg weekly (IV, SC, or oral)
   - Gold standard first-line agent
   - Takes 6-12 weeks for effect
   - Most effective, best studied
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Hepatotoxicity, bone marrow suppression, GI upset
   - Requires: Monthly LFTs + CBC monitoring
   - PREGNANCY: Teratogenic (women must use contraception)
   - Brand names: Rheumatrex (generic available)

2. SULFASALAZINE
   - Dosage: 500 mg twice daily, increase to 1000 mg twice daily
   - Often used with methotrexate for synergy
   - Takes 6-12 weeks for effect
   - SIDE EFFECTS: GI upset (more than others), skin reactions, Steven-Johnson syndrome (rare)
   - Monitor CBC
   - Often used as adjunct

3. HYDROXYCHLOROQUINE
   - Dosage: 200-400 mg daily
   - Takes 6-12 weeks for effect
   - Mild effect, often combined with other DMARDs
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Retinopathy (rare, requires eye exams), GI upset
   - Usually used in combination, less effective monotherapy
   - Brand names: Plaquenil

BIOLOGIC DMARDS (Second-line or earlier if aggressive disease):

4. TNF-ALPHA INHIBITORS:
   - ETANERCEPT (Enbrel): 25-50 mg SC weekly
   - INFLIXIMAB (Remicade): 3 mg/kg IV infusion every 8 weeks
   - ADALIMUMAB (Humira): 40 mg SC every other week
   - Dosage varies by agent
   - Very effective: 50-60% achieve low disease activity
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Infection risk (TB reactivation — screen first), injection site reactions, immunosuppression
   - Requires TB testing before starting
   - Used with or without methotrexate

5. IL-6 INHIBITORS:
   - Tocilizumab: IV infusion 4-8 mg/kg monthly or SC 162 mg weekly
   - JAK inhibitors: Baricitinib, tofacitinib (oral)
   - Very effective, especially if TNF-inhibitor failure
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Infection risk, lipid elevation, GI perforation

FIRST-LINE AGENT SELECTION:
- Early RA (diagnosis <6 months): DMARDs should start immediately
- Monotherapy: Methotrexate standard
- Dual therapy: Often methotrexate + biologics for better response
- Goal: Low disease activity or remission (modern target)

SYMPTOM RELIEF (Adjunctive):

6. NSAIDs:
   - Ibuprofen: 400-600 mg every 6 hours
   - Naproxen: 500 mg twice daily
   - Indomethacin: 25-50 mg daily
   - Provides inflammation relief, pain control
   - Used short-term (weeks-months) until DMARD effect
   - CAUTION: GI complications, cardiovascular risk (with RA, doubled risk anyway)
   - If long-term needed: Use lowest effective dose, PPI protection

7. CORTICOSTEROIDS (Low-dose, short-term):
   - Prednisone: 5-10 mg daily initially
   - Taper as DMARD takes effect
   - Avoid long-term (osteoporosis, infection risks)
   - Bridge therapy while awaiting DMARD effect
   - Intra-articular injections: Tri-amcinolone for single joint flare

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID:
- Stopping DMARD therapy: Increases flare risk
- Long-term excessive corticosteroids: Secondary effects dominate
- Excess NSAIDs: GI, cardiovascular complications

FOODS TO EAT (Anti-inflammatory):
- Fatty fish: Salmon, sardines, mackerel (omega-3, anti-inflammatory)
- Vegetable oils: Olive oil, walnut oil (omega-3, polyphenols)
- Vegetables: Leafy greens, broccoli, cauliflower, carrots
- Fruits: Berries (antioxidants), citrus, apples
- Whole grains: Brown rice, oats, whole wheat
- Legumes / Dal: Fiber, plant protein
- Nuts and seeds: Almonds, walnuts (omega-3)
- Turmeric / Haldi: Curcumin (anti-inflammatory, take with black pepper for absorption)
- Ginger / Adrak: Gingerols (anti-inflammatory)
- Garlic / Lahsun: Allicin (anti-inflammatory)
- Green tea: Polyphenols, EGCG (anti-inflammatory)
- Tomato: Lycopene

FOODS TO AVOID/LIMIT:
- Red meat: High in arachidonic acid (inflammatory)
- Processed foods: Trans fats, refined carbs
- High-omega-6 foods: Vegetable oils (soy, corn, safflower)
- Alcohol: Excess (interferes with methotrexate)
- High-fat dairy: Butter, cream
- Fried foods: Trans fats
- Sugar: Inflammatory
- Salt: May exacerbate

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:

EXERCISE:
- Regular, moderate: Preserves joint function
- Low-impact: Swimming, water aerobics, cycling (easier on joints than running)
- Range-of-motion: Gentle stretching daily
- Strength training: 2-3 times weekly (maintains muscle, supports joints)
- Consistency: More important than intensity
- Pain guide: Should not increase joint pain

PHYSICAL/OCCUPATIONAL THERAPY:
- Joint protection: Proper body mechanics
- Assistive devices: Jar openers, ergonomic utensils
- Adaptive strategies: Reducing fine motor demands
- Heat/cold: Warm baths reduce morning stiffness, cold for acute inflammation

STRESS REDUCTION:
- Meditation, yoga, tai chi: Reduce stress, improve flexibility
- Adequate sleep: 7-8 hours nightly (inflammation worsens with sleep deprivation)

SMOKING CESSATION:
- Critical: Smoking worsens disease, reduces DMARD efficacy
- Increases extra-articular disease

WEIGHT MANAGEMENT:
- Obesity increases inflammation
- Weight loss improves disease activity

MONITORING:

CLINICAL ASSESSMENT:
- Every 4-12 weeks: Disease activity assessment (joint count, pain, function)
- Goal: Low disease activity or remission (current target)
- DAS28 score: Disease activity calculation (swollen/tender joints, CRP/ESR, global assessment)

LABORATORY MONITORING:
- Monthly initially: CBC, LFTs, creatinine (especially on methotrexate)
- Every 3 months: Once stable
- RF, anti-CCP: Yearly (not for monitoring activity, but for prognosis)

IMAGING MONITORING:
- Baseline: X-rays of hands, feet (assess damage)
- Repeat: Annually if progressive, less often if stable
- Ultrasound: More sensitive for early erosions

CARDIOVASCULAR MONITORING:
- Baseline lipids: Biologics can raise cholesterol
- HTN assessment: RA increases cardiovascular risk
- Consider: Aspirin, statin consideration in some patients

WHEN TO SEEK DOCTOR:
- Joint pain/swelling lasting >6 weeks
- Morning stiffness >1 hour
- Systemic symptoms: Fever, weight loss, fatigue
- Extra-articular symptoms: Eye redness, shortness of breath, rash
- During treatment: Disease not improving on DMARD after 3 months

WHEN TO SEEK URGENT ATTENTION:
- Acute eye redness and pain (scleritis)
- Severe fever with joint swelling
- Chest pain or severe shortness of breath

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Pregnancy: Some DMARDs teratogenic (methotrexate), others safe (sulfasalazine, biologics)
- Males: Rarer, similar disease but less studied
- Young-onset: Often aggressive, early biologic therapy beneficial
- Seronegative RA (RF-, anti-CCP-): Subset, similar treatment response
- Secondary infections: Higher risk on biologics (TB, fungal infections screening critical)

PROGNOSIS:
- With modern DMARDs: 70-80% achieve low disease activity/remission
- Without treatment: Progressive joint destruction (30% have severe disability at 5 years)
- With early aggressive treatment: 50% achieve drug-free remission
- Extra-articular disease: Requires treating specific manifestations
