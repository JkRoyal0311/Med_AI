DISEASE: Tuberculosis (TB)
CATEGORY: Infectious / Pulmonary
ICD-10: A15-A19
SEVERITY: Serious — life-threatening if untreated

DESCRIPTION:
Tuberculosis is a serious infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis bacteria.
Spreads through airborne droplets when infected person coughs, sneezes, or speaks.
Primarily affects lungs (pulmonary TB) but can affect any organ. Two forms: latent TB (dormant,
non-infectious) and active TB (disease). Major health problem in India — one of highest TB burden countries globally.

RISK FACTORS:
- Close contact with TB patient
- Weak immune system (HIV/AIDS, immunosuppressive medications)
- Malnutrition and poverty
- Diabetes
- Smoking
- Alcohol abuse
- Overcrowding conditions
- Homeless or incarcerated populations

SYMPTOMS (Pulmonary TB):
- Persistent cough: >3 weeks, often worse in morning
- Cough with sputum: Initially dry, later with phlegm
- Blood in sputum / Hemoptysis: Indicates cavitary disease
- Chest pain: When breathing or coughing
- Fever: Low-grade, usually afternoon/evening
- Chills and night sweats: Drenching, especially at night
- Fatigue and weakness
- Weight loss and loss of appetite
- Shortness of breath: In advanced disease

PROGRESSION:
- Early: Mild cough, attributed to cold
- Progressive: Worsening cough, weight loss, sweats
- Advanced: Hemoptysis, severe dyspnea, cavitary disease on X-ray

DIAGNOSIS:
- Sputum AFB (Acid-Fast Bacilli) smear microscopy: Gold standard in endemic areas
- GeneXpert MTB/RIF: Rapid WHO-recommended test
- Chest X-ray: Shows infiltrates, cavitations (usually upper lobes)
- Tuberculin skin test (TST): Mantoux test (positive doesn't distinguish active vs latent)
- TB IGRA blood test: More specific than TST

MEDICATIONS USED (All TB drugs must be taken together for full duration):

FIRST-LINE TB DRUGS (Standard regimen):
1. ISONIAZID
   - Dosage: 300 mg daily or 900 mg twice weekly
   - Duration: 6 months total
   - Penetrates lung tissue well
   - SIDE EFFECT: Peripheral neuropathy (prevented by vitamin B6 supplementation)

2. RIFAMPICIN (Rifampin)
   - Dosage: 450-600 mg daily
   - Duration: 6 months total
   - Most potent TB drug
   - Turns body fluids orange/red color (normal, harmless)
   - CAUTION: Reduces birth control pill effectiveness

3. PYRAZINAMIDE
   - Dosage: 1.5-2 grams daily
   - Duration: First 2 months only
   - SIDE EFFECT: Hyperuricemia (elevated uric acid, may worsen gout)
   - CAUTION: Liver toxicity — monitor LFTs

4. ETHAMBUTOL
   - Dosage: 15 mg/kg/day
   - Duration: First 2 months
   - SIDE EFFECT: Color blindness (red-green), reversible if stopped early
   - Requires regular eye testing

STANDARD REGIMEN:
- Intensive Phase (First 2 months): Isoniazid + Rifampicin + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol
- Continuation Phase (Next 4 months): Isoniazid + Rifampicin
- TOTAL: 6 months

SECOND-LINE DRUGS (For drug-resistant TB):
5. FLUOROQUINOLONES
   - Levofloxacin, Moxifloxacin
   - Used in MDR-TB (multidrug-resistant)
   - Longer treatment duration (20-24 months)
   - More side effects, more expensive

MONITORING DURING TREATMENT:
- Monthly clinical assessment
- Monthly sputum testing (should become negative by 2 months)
- Baseline and periodic LFTs (liver function tests) — TB drugs hepatotoxic
- Visual testing if on ethambutol
- Vitamin B6 supplementation: 25 mg daily (prevents isoniazid neuropathy)

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID:
- Alcohol: Increases hepatotoxicity risk
- Hepatotoxic drugs: Avoid if possible (acetaminophen, other TB drugs)

FOODS TO EAT (Support immune function):
- Protein-rich: Chicken, fish, eggs, meat (rebuild lost weight)
- Legumes / Dal: Protein, iron, vitamins
- Nuts and seeds: Energy-dense, micronutrients
- Citrus fruits: Vitamin C boosts immunity
- Leafy greens / Palak, methi: Nutrition essential
- Whole grains: B vitamins
- Eggs: Complete protein
- Milk and dairy / Dahi: Calcium, protein
- Honey / Shahad: Energy, antimicrobial
- Turmeric / Haldi: Anti-inflammatory
- Ginger / Adrak: Anti-inflammatory

FOODS TO AVOID:
- Alcohol: Increases hepatitis risk, impairs immunity
- Excess salt: Avoid considering any hypertension
- Very spicy foods: May irritate lungs if coughing
- Excess sugar: Impairs immune function
- Processed foods: Lack nutrients needed

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS:
- Complete adherence to medications: CRITICAL for cure and preventing resistance
- Take all medications every day for full 6 months (even if feeling well by month 2-3)
- Medical reminder systems: Alarms, calendar markings
- Contact tracing: Family members need testing and preventive therapy if latent TB
- Infection control: Cover mouth/nose when coughing (first 2 weeks of treatment)
- Isolation: Not needed after 2 weeks of appropriate treatment + clinical improvement
- Nutritious diet: Essential for immune recovery
- Avoid smoking and alcohol
- Rest: Adequate sleep while body fights infection

LATENT TB INFECTION (No symptoms):
- Positive TST/IGRA but normal chest X-ray, no symptoms
- Risk of reactivation 5-10% lifetime (higher with immunosuppression)
- Preventive therapy: Isoniazid 300 mg daily for 6-9 months (or shorter regimens available)
- Family members of TB patient: Get tested for latent TB, may need preventive therapy

TB/HIV COINFECTION:
- Major cause of death in HIV patients
- Drug interactions complex (rifampicin reduces antiretroviral levels)
- Requires coordination with HIV specialist
- Co-trimoxazole prophylaxis often added

MDR-TB (Multidrug-resistant TB):
- Resistant to both isoniazid and rifampicin
- Requires second-line drugs
- Treatment 20-24 months
- Higher cost, more side effects, lower success rates
- Reason: Incomplete treatment, counterfeit drugs, initial infection with resistant strain

XDR-TB (Extensively drug-resistant TB):
- Resistant to fluoroquinolones and injectable drugs
- Even more serious
- Newer drugs (bedaquiline, linezolid) needed

COMPLICATIONS:
- TB meningitis: CNS involvement, very serious
- TB peritonitis: Abdominal TB
- TB pericarditis: Heart sac involvement
- Disseminated/miliary TB: Bloodstream spread to multiple organs
- TB-associated diabetes: Complication, requires insulin
- Post-TB pulmonary fibrosis: Scarring after cure

TREATMENT OUTCOME DEFINITIONS:
- Cured: Completed treatment + bacteriological evidence of cure
- Completed: Completed treatment without evidence of cure (but clinically improved)
- Died: Death during treatment
- Failed: Smear/culture positive at 5 months
- Lost to follow-up: Interruption of treatment >2 months

WHEN TO SEEK IMMEDIATE ATTENTION:
- Coughing up large amounts of blood
- Severe chest pain with breathing
- High fever (>101°F) unresponsive to treatment after 2 weeks
- Signs of meningitis: Stiff neck, high fever, confusion
- Allergic reaction to medication: Rash, itching, difficulty breathing

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Pregnancy: TB treatment safe in pregnancy; untreated TB is more dangerous
- Children: Can develop TB from adult exposure (close contact); preventive therapy if latent TB
- Elderly: Similar treatment but monitor more closely
- Diabetes + TB: Worsens diabetes control, slower TB healing, higher mortality
- Alcoholism: Increased hepatotoxicity, poor compliance
- Homeless: Partner with social services for treatment adherence
- Counterfeit drugs: Major problem in India; use government/certified providers
- DOT (Directly Observed Therapy): Healthcare worker observes patient taking each dose (gold standard)
