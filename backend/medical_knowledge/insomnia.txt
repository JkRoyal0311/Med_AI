DISEASE: Insomnia (Chronic Insomnia Disorder)
CATEGORY: Sleep / Psychiatric
ICD-10: F51.01 (Sleep-onset insomnia), F51.02 (Sleep-maintenance insomnia)
SEVERITY: Mild-Moderate; affects quality of life and health

DESCRIPTION:
Insomnia is persistent difficulty initiating sleep, maintaining sleep, or nonrestorative sleep
despite adequate opportunity and circumstances for sleep, ≥3 nights per week for ≥3 months.
Affects 10-15% of population. Can be primary (no underlying cause) or secondary (from medical/psychiatric condition).
Impacts daytime functioning, mood, productivity, health.

TYPES:
- Sleep-onset insomnia: Difficulty falling asleep (>30 minutes to sleep onset)
- Sleep-maintenance insomnia: Frequent awakenings, difficulty returning to sleep
- Early morning awakening: Waking 2+ hours earlier than desired
- Non-restorative sleep: Sleep but feeling unrefreshed

CAUSES:

PSYCHOLOGICAL:
- Anxiety disorders
- Depression
- Stress: Work, family, financial, health
- Trauma or PTSD
- Obsessive thinking: "Racing mind"

MEDICAL CONDITIONS:
- Chronic pain: Arthritis, back pain, fibromyalgia
- Sleep apnea: Breathing interruptions (often undiagnosed)
- Gastroesophageal reflux: Acid causing awakenings
- Urinary frequency: Nocturia from diabetes, prostate, diuretics
- Hot flashes: Menopause or medications
- Asthma or COPD: Nighttime symptoms
- Hyperthyroidism: Increased metabolism, racing thoughts
- Restless leg syndrome: Uncomfortable sensations preventing sleep

MEDICATIONS:
- Stimulants: Caffeine (coffee, tea, chocolate, cola, energy drinks)
- Corticosteroids: Prednisone (can cause insomnia)
- Decongestants: Pseudoephedrine
- Some antidepressants: SSRIs can initially worsen sleep
- Beta-blockers: Some formulations
- Alcohol: Initially sedating, later disrupts sleep architecture
- Nicotine: Stimulant

LIFESTYLE/ENVIRONMENTAL:
- Irregular sleep schedule: Varying bed/wake times
- Stimulating activities before bed: Screen time, work
- Caffeine after 2 PM: Half-life 5-6 hours
- Exercise too close to bedtime: Stimulating
- Uncomfortable sleep environment: Noise, light, temperature
- Large meals before bed: Discomfort
- Napping: Especially afternoon naps
- Clock-watching: Anxiety about sleep

BEHAVIORAL PATTERNS:
- Cognitive arousal: Worrying about not sleeping (paradoxical)
- Using bed for work/study: Condition bed with wakefulness
- Trying too hard to sleep: Creates performance anxiety
- Negative sleep associations

SYMPTOMS:
- Difficulty falling asleep: >30 minutes to sleep onset
- Frequent or prolonged nighttime awakenings: >30 minutes total
- Waking too early without ability to return to sleep
- Non-restorative sleep: Sleep feels inadequate
- Daytime functional impairment:
  * Fatigue
  * Difficulty concentrating
  * Irritability
  * Mood disturbance
  * Reduced productivity
  * Slower reflexes (increased accident risk)
- Anxiety about sleep: Fear of insomnia (perpetuates cycle)

MEDICATIONS USED:

NONBENZODIAZEPINE HYPNOTICS (Z-drugs):
1. ZOLPIDEM
   - Dosage: 5-10 mg at bedtime
   - Onset: 15-30 minutes
   - Duration: 6-8 hours
   - Brand names: Ambien
   - Short-acting (good for sleep onset)
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Morning drowsiness, dizziness, complex sleep behaviors (rare)
   - CAUTION: Risk of dependence; use ≤2-4 weeks ideally
   - Avoid: >6 hours before needed waking time

2. ZOPICLONE
   - Dosage: 7.5 mg at bedtime
   - Similar to zolpidem
   - Leaves metallic taste (common side effect)
   - Available in India (Zimovane)

3. ZALEPLON
   - Dosage: 5-10 mg at bedtime
   - Very short-acting (1 hour), ideal if mid-sleep awakening
   - Can take if wake at night with 4+ hours remaining

BENZODIAZEPINES (Second-line):
4. TRIAZOLAM
   - Dosage: 0.125-0.25 mg at bedtime
   - Very short-acting
   - Used less due to dependence risk
   - CAUTION: Highest abuse potential

5. LORAZEPAM
   - Dosage: 0.5-2 mg at bedtime
   - Also has anxiolytic properties (good if anxiety contributes)
   - Longer half-life (hangover effect next day)
   - CAUTION: Dependence risk, falls risk (especially elderly)

MELATONIN RECEPTOR AGONIST:
6. RAMELTEON
   - Dosage: 8 mg right before bedtime
   - Works on melatonin receptors (different mechanism)
   - Minimal dependence risk
   - NO abuse potential (Schedule III controlled)
   - Brand names: Rozerem

7. MELATONIN (Natural supplement)
   - Dosage: 1-10 mg 30 minutes before bed
   - Non-prescription supplement
   - Very safe, no dependence
   - Effectiveness variable (works better for jet lag, shift work)
   - Available OTC

ANTIDEPRESSANTS (For insomnia with depression):
8. AMITRIPTYLINE
   - Dosage: 25-100 mg at bedtime
   - Sedating antidepressant
   - Treats both insomnia and depression
   - SIDE EFFECTS: Morning drowsiness, dry mouth, weight gain
   - Takes weeks for mood effect

9. TRAZODONE
   - Dosage: 50-100 mg at bedtime
   - Sedating antidepressant
   - Used off-label for insomnia (even without depression)
   - Minimal abuse potential
   - Brand names: Desyrel

HERBAL/NATURAL SUPPLEMENTS:
10. VALERIAN ROOT
    - Dosage: 300-900 mg 1-2 hours before bed
    - Herbal, available OTC
    - Modest efficacy, safe
    - Takes 2-3 weeks to work
    - May cause headache or dizziness

11. PASSIONFLOWER
    - Dosage: 500-1000 mg before bed
    - Herbal anxiolytic
    - Helps sleep with anxiety

12. MAGNESIUM
    - Dosage: 200-400 mg before bed
    - Mineral supplement
    - Helps relaxation, many people deficient
    - Also helps with muscle cramps

MEDICATIONS TO AVOID:
- Alcohol: Initially deceptive (helps fall asleep) but disrupts sleep architecture and REM sleep
- Stimulants: Caffeine, pseudoephedrine, other decongestants
- Some cold medications: Contain stimulants
- Sudden cessation of benzodiazepines: Risk of rebound insomnia

FOODS TO EAT (Sleep-promoting):
- Tryptophan-containing: Turkey, chicken, cheese, nuts (precursor to serotonin/melatonin)
- Complex carbohydrates: Brown rice, whole wheat (enhance tryptophan absorption)
- Milk / Dahi: Tryptophan + calcium
- Almonds: Magnesium, tryptophan
- Pumpkin seeds: Magnesium, zinc
- Leafy greens / Palak, methi: Magnesium
- Kiwifruit: Serotonin content, may promote sleep (studies suggest)
- Tart cherry juice: Natural melatonin
- Honey / Shahad: Small amount before bed may help (promotes tryptophan entry to brain)
- Warm milk with turmeric / Haldi: Relaxing, traditional remedy
- Herbal teas: Chamomile, passionflower, valerian

FOODS TO STRICTLY AVOID (Evening):
- Caffeine: Coffee, strong tea, chocolate, cola, energy drinks
  * Avoid after 2-3 PM (half-life 5-6 hours)
- High-fat foods: Heavy, take longer to digest
- High-protein foods (alone): Without carbs (can stimulate)
- Spicy foods: Can cause reflux/discomfort at night
- Excess sugar: Energy surge, then crash
- Alcohol: Deceptive initially sedating effect

DRINKS TO AVOID:
- Coffee: Obvious caffeine source
- Black/green tea: Caffeine + theine
- Cola drinks: Caffeine
- Chocolate drinks: Caffeine, sugar
- Energy drinks: High caffeine, stimulants
- Alcohol: Disrupts sleep quality

BEDTIME ROUTINE (Behavioral treatment — Most important):
1. CONSISTENT SCHEDULE:
   - Same bed time every night (even weekends)
   - Same wake time every morning
   - 7-8 hours sleep target (7-9 hours ideal)
   - Trains body's circadian rhythm

2. PRE-BEDTIME WIND-DOWN (30-60 minutes):
   - Dim lights: Reduces blue light exposure
   - Avoid screens: Phone, laptop, TV (blue light suppresses melatonin)
   - Relaxing activities: Reading, gentle stretching, meditation
   - Warm bath or shower: Temperature drop after bath aids sleep
   - Breathing exercises: Deep, slow breathing (4-7-8 technique)
   - Progressive muscle relaxation: Tense and release muscles

3. SLEEP ENVIRONMENT:
   - Dark: Blackout curtains or eye mask
   - Quiet: Earplugs if needed
   - Cool temperature: 65-68°F (18-20°C) optimal
   - Comfortable bed: Quality mattress and pillow
   - No clocks visible: Reduces clock-watching anxiety

4. BEDROOM ASSOCIATION:
   - Use bed only for sleep and intimacy (not work, eating, TV)
   - Leave bed if awake >20 minutes: Do quiet activity until drowsy, then return
   - Avoid napping: Especially after 3 PM

5. DAYTIME PRACTICES:
   - Exercise: 150 min/week moderately (but not within 3 hours of bed)
   - Sunlight exposure: 30 minutes bright light in morning (circadian entrainment)
   - Reduce stress: Yoga, meditation, stress management
   - Avoid daytime naps: Especially afternoon
   - Limit caffeine: Cut-off by 2-3 PM

COGNITIVE BEHAVIORAL THERAPY FOR INSOMNIA (CBT-I):
- Gold standard, more effective long-term than medications
- 4-8 week program
- 70-80% success rate
- Teaches sleep hygiene, thought patterns, stimulus control
- Ideal baseline before starting medication

RELAXATION TECHNIQUES:
- Progressive muscle relaxation: Tense each muscle group 5 seconds, release
- Guided imagery: Visualize calm, peaceful scene
- Meditation: Mindful awareness of breath
- Yoga: Gentle stretching, breathing
- Autogenic training: Self-directed relaxation

MONITORING TREATMENT:
- Sleep diary: Track sleep quality, timing, daytime function
- Assess after 2-3 weeks: If not improving, adjust
- Don't jump between medications: Give adequate trial (2-3 weeks minimum)

WHEN TO SEEK DOCTOR:
- Insomnia ≥1 month not improving with sleep hygiene
- Suspect sleep apnea: Loud snoring, gasping at night, daytime somnolence
- Insomnia with depression/anxiety: Requires treatment
- Medication side effects: Daytime grogginess, complex sleep behaviors
- Not improving after medication trial: May need adjustment

SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS:
- Elderly: Reduced sleep duration normal; medications increase falls risk
- Pregnancy: Sleep problems common; insomnia usually improves postpartum
- Shift workers: Circadian rhythm disruption; melatonin, light therapy helpful
- Sleep apnea: Must be treated (cause of insomnia-like symptoms)
- Restless leg syndrome: Treat underlying (iron deficiency, dopamine agonists)
- Chronic pain: Treat underlying condition + insomnia-specific approaches
- Dependence risk: Benzodiazepines/Z-drugs should be short-term; long-term CBT-I preferred

DISCONTINUATION:
- Gradual taper: 50% reduction then full tapering over 2-4 weeks
- Rebound insomnia: Common with sudden stoppage; tapered discontinuation reduces this
- Long-term use: Not recommended (tolerance, dependence, side effects)
